## Definition

Backscattering, in the UAP context, is the phenomenon where sunlight reflects off snow-covered ground and scatters upward through the atmosphere, illuminating low-level clouds from below. This can produce a luminous, stationary, or slowly morphing airborne apparition visible to ground observers — especially when the sun is low over the horizon and snow cover is present. The effect can vanish abruptly if either the cloud layer or the sun's angle shifts.

## Characteristics

- **Requirements**: Snow-covered ground, low sun angle, partial cloud cover at low-to-mid altitudes
- **Appearance**: Can produce a stationary, defined-edge white/shimmering illumination on the underside of cloud layers
- **Disappearance**: Sudden if the cloud drifts out of the reflection zone or if the sun angle changes
- **Observer position matters**: The effect depends on the observer's line of sight aligning with the illuminated cloud patch
- **Shape changes**: As cloud layers undulate or thin, the illuminated patch can appear to change shape
- **Seasonal**: Most likely in winter months when snow cover is present and sun angles are low

## Documented Examples

- **Colorado Springs / Cheyenne Mountain, 15 February 2022** — AARO assessed that five Army witnesses observing a stationary, potato-shaped, translucent white object over Cheyenne Mountain likely saw backscattering of sunlight off 6–12 inches of snow. AARO holds low confidence in this assessment. See [[summaries/ica-uap-d001-analysis-colorado-springs-uap-incident]] and [[entities/cheyenne-mountain-uap-2022]].
