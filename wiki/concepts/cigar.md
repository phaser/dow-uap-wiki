## Definition

A "cigar-shaped" UAP is an elongated cylindrical object, typically described as having a length-to-diameter ratio of 3:1 or greater, with no visible wings, tail surfaces, or conventional aircraft features. This morphology appears recurrently in the Project SIGN catalog and is often associated with reports of windows, decks, exhaust plumes, or glowing undersides — suggesting a structured craft rather than a natural phenomenon.

## Characteristics

- Elongated cylindrical/fuselage shape, 3:1 or greater ratio
- No wings, no tail surfaces, no standard aircraft marker lights
- Often reported with visible exhaust (flame, jet, rocket-like tail)
- Sometimes described with windows or deck structures
- Typical reported length: 100 ft (Chiles-Whitted), "larger than B-29" (Los Angeles)
- High speed: 500-700+ MPH at relatively low altitudes (~5,000 ft)

## Documented Examples

- **Chiles-Whitted Encounter (24 July 1948)** — [[entities/chiles-whitted-encounter]]: DC-3 pilots observed 100-ft cigar-shaped object with two rows of windows, blue fluorescent glow, and 50-ft red-orange exhaust near Montgomery, AL
- **The Hague, Netherlands (20 July 1948)** — [[summaries/dow-uap-d088-us-airforce-analysis-of-flying-objects-in-the-us-101-172]] Incident #168: "Aircraft with 2 decks and no wings" — independent description 4 days before Chiles-Whitted
- **Los Angeles (30 August 1948)** — [[summaries/dow-uap-d088-us-airforce-analysis-of-flying-objects-in-the-us-101-172]] Incident #166: Object "resembled the fuselage of a V-2 rocket but much larger" — larger than B-29, silver, blue exhaust, 700+ MPH at 20,000+ ft
- **Worthington, Ohio (30 July 1948)** — [[summaries/dow-uap-d088-us-airforce-analysis-of-flying-objects-in-the-us-101-172]] Incident #159: Cylindrical silver object, no wings or tail surfaces, steady silver glow, 3,000 ft, visible for 15 minutes
