## Definition

The "training and debunking" program was the central policy recommendation of the CIA's 1953 Robertson Panel. It proposed a coordinated U.S. government effort to reduce public interest in Unidentified Flying Objects through mass-media educational campaigns ("debunking") and improved military personnel training to correctly identify conventional objects. The program aimed to eliminate UFOs' "aura of mystery," reduce the volume of low-quality sighting reports that could overload defense communications, and inoculate the public against mass hysteria that might be exploited by enemy psychological warfare.

## Characteristics

- **Two aims**: (1) "Training" — teaching military personnel to correctly identify known phenomena (balloons, aircraft reflections, meteors, mirages, noctilucent clouds) to reduce misidentification reports; (2) "Debunking" — reducing public interest through mass media (television, motion pictures, popular articles) and case history education where puzzling sightings are later explained.
- **Duration**: Minimum 1.5-2 years required for effect
- **Methods proposed**: Walt Disney animated cartoons, Jam Handy training films, business club/school/college presentations, television documentaries, popular science articles. "The use of true cases showing first the 'mystery' and then the 'explanation' would be forceful."
- **Psychological consultation**: Panel recommended psychologists familiar with mass psychology (Dr. Hadley Cantril of Princeton — author of "Invasion from Mars" study of the 1938 Orson Welles radio panic; Don Marquis of University of Michigan; Leo Rosten); advertising experts; communication specialists (Arthur Godfrey suggested as mass audience channel).
- **Implementation**: (1) Air Force Letter 200-5 (1952) directing all USAF units to wire UFO reports directly to ATIC; (2) JANAP 146 (1953) prohibiting service personnel from discussing UFOs under threat of 10 years imprisonment and $10,000 fine; (3) Reduction of Blue Book staff from ~10 to 1 airman by September 1953; (4) Transfer of investigation authority from Blue Book to 4602nd AISS (reporting to ADC and USAF Intelligence, NOT Blue Book); (5) AFR 200-2 (1953) directing that only identified sightings be released to the public; (6) AFR 80-17 (1966) replacing AFR 200-2 and transferring responsibility from Intelligence to Research and Development, stating that material on UFOs would be safeguarded to "prevent any downgrading or alterations which might reduce its value for intelligence examination."
- **Effect**: According to the Australian DoD assessment ([[summaries/cia-uap-019-australian-dod-ufo-assessment]]), the program was remarkably effective — Blue Book's public-facing "unknowns" rate dropped from ~20% to near zero, while the covert intelligence collection on UFO performance and propulsion was intensified.
- **CIA covert divergence**: While the public debunking program was underway, the CIA was simultaneously "collecting data on UFO performance and propulsion methods" and influencing funding of advanced projects including the Avro Canada saucer and multiple gravity research programs.

## Documented Examples

- **Blue Book Special Report No. 14's two versions**: The complete statistical report showing 33.3% unknowns for "excellent" cases was suppressed; a publicly available "summary" concentrated on 1953-1955 data "designed to reduce the residual unknowns to an insignificant number"
- **JANAP 146**: Service personnel gag order, progressively tightened (146 → 146E in 1960, adding Espionage Act penalties)
- **1953-1955 period**: Blue Book staff reduced almost to zero; public reporting funneled to 4602nd AISS; only identified cases publicly acknowledged
- **NASA 1976-1977 astronaut-UFO analysis**: A late-1970s extension of debunking methodology applied specifically to astronaut UFO sighting claims. The NASA analysis ([[summaries/usg-uap-d001-congress-whitehouse-ufo-correspondence-1998]]) systematically reviewed Mercury through Skylab claims, demonstrating that each celebrated case was explainable as spacecraft debris, tracking balloons, NORAD errors, ice flakes, or transcript misreadings. NASA photo analyst [[entities/richard-underwood|Richard Underwood]] served as the internal expert identifying photographic anomalies.
- **NASA 1997 UFO Fact Sheet (FS-1997-01-017-HQ)**: By the late 1990s, NASA's standardized response to UFO inquiries was a 2-page fact sheet routing inquiries to SETI Institute, National Archives, and law enforcement — a bureaucratic continuation of the debunking-by-referral approach.
