## Definition

The **Apollo light flashes** are point and streak flashes of light reported by Apollo and Skylab astronauts during dark-adapted periods inside their spacecraft cabins. The phenomenon was first systematically described by Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin during the [[entities/apollo-11]] mission and later confirmed across all subsequent lunar missions, Skylab, and (much later) ISS. The leading explanation — confirmed by the **ALFMED** (Apollo Light-Flash Moving Emulsion Detector) experiment on Apollo 16 and 17 — is that high-energy galactic cosmic rays and trapped Van Allen / South-Atlantic-Anomaly particles cross retinal cells and produce direct visual percepts (Cherenkov radiation in the vitreous humor and / or direct ionization of retinal photoreceptors).

## Characteristics

- Two morphologies: **point flashes** (single blink) and **double flashes** at points "separated by maybe a foot," which Aldrin interpreted as particle entry and impact on cabin structure.
- Sometimes appear as a "line with no direction of motion" (track-shaped events).
- Frequency: Armstrong counted ~90 events in approximately one hour of dedicated observation on Apollo 11.
- Strongly correlated with the [[entities/south-atlantic-anomaly]] in low-Earth-orbit observations (Skylab crew, per [[summaries/nasa-uap-d7-skylab-technical-crew-debriefing-1973]]).
- Cernan described them as a "headlight between my eyes" during [[entities/apollo-17]].
