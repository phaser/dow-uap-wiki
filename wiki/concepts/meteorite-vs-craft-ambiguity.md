## Definition

A common methodological dispute in 1947–1955 UFO investigation: whether a given fireball / aerial luminous event was a natural meteor / bolide or an unidentified controlled craft. [[entities/project-sign]] routed ambiguous cases to Dr. Lincoln La Paz ([[entities/lapaz-lincoln]]) of the Institute of Meteoritics, University of New Mexico, for adjudication. The dispute is preserved in the Norcatur, KS fireball case of 18 February 1948 (Incident 101 in [[summaries/army-incident-summaries-101-172]]), where La Paz argued for natural-meteor origin while Markham argued for a controlled craft based on near-encounter testimony from witness Leland Sammons.

## Characteristics

- Discriminating features cited in the files: trajectory shape (parabolic = ballistic, complex = controlled), velocity profile, fragmentation pattern, presence of ground-recovered material (a 109 lb meteorite was recovered in the Norcatur case — strong meteorite evidence).
- Multi-state luminous events are particularly hard to discriminate.
- The debate continues structurally into the modern UAP record: AARO often classifies events as "satellite re-entry," "Starlink train," or "orbital debris" rather than "meteor," but the question — *natural object or controlled craft?* — is structurally identical.

## Documented Examples

- **Norcatur, KS, 18 February 1948** — multi-state fireball, 109 lb meteorite recovered; La Paz: natural meteor; Markham: controlled craft based on Leland Sammons's near-encounter testimony — see [[summaries/army-incident-summaries-101-172]]
