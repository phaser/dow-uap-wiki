## Definition

Radar anomalies in the context of UFO reporting refer to known radar phenomena recognized by the Robertson Panel (and later studies) as a significant source of false UFO reports. The primary mechanism identified was **mutual interference** between radar stations: when two stations operate at approximately the same pulse frequency, Station A's signal can be picked up on Station B's screen, appearing as a high-speed target track or series of dots not corresponding to any physical object. This was recognized as a "characteristic problem of radar operation" and was cited as a probable cause of numerous UFO radar reports, including the July 1952 Washington D.C. incidents.

## Characteristics

- **Mutual interference mechanism**: When Station A transmits a pulse at approximately the same frequency as Station B's receiver, the pulse appears on Station B's screen as a target that can show tracks at speeds of 1,000-10,000 mph — consistent with many UFO radar reports
- **DC flap connection**: The July 1952 Washington National Airport radar-visual incidents that prompted the Robertson Panel's convening were partly attributed to mutual interference
- **ADC concern**: Air Defense Command was actively studying the mutual interference identification problem ahead of expected deployment of very high-speed aircraft and guided missiles (1955-1956)
- **Proposed solutions**: (1) "Doppler filter" in the receiving circuit (Dr. Berkner's suggestion); (2) "Controlled jitter" — operator receiving a "fast track" would alter their station's pulse frequency slightly; if the signal was mutual interference, the track would disappear or shift; (3) Dr. Alvarez's simpler technical solution (not detailed in the record)
- **Anomalous propagation**: Temperature inversions causing radar beams to bend and reflect off ground objects, creating apparent contacts — discussed separately from mutual interference but also a factor

## Documented Examples

- **July 19/26, 1952, Washington D.C.** — Radar-visual mass sightings. Robertson Panel recognized mutual interference as contributing factor
- **Various ADC radar tracks (1952)** — ADC expressed "concern in solving this problem of signal identification" ahead of high-speed aircraft deployment
