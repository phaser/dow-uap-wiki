## Definition

Soviet UAP refers to both (a) the Soviet Union's official and scientific interest in unidentified aerial phenomena, and (b) UAP sightings reported from within the Soviet bloc during the Cold War. CIA intelligence collection on this topic addressed two key questions: whether the USSR was responsible for U.S. "flying saucer" reports (i.e., were they advanced Soviet technology), and whether the Soviet scientific community was independently investigating the phenomenon. The CIA's posture evolved from concern about Soviet origin (late 1940s) to dismissal of this possibility (Robertson Panel, 1953), while continuing to collect intelligence on Soviet scientific perspectives.

## Characteristics

- **Soviet origin hypothesis (1947-1949)**: Initial U.S. intelligence assessment (ATIC, Project SIGN) considered flying saucers "real and probably of Soviet origin" — a hypothesis later rejected but not before shaping collection requirements.
- **CIA assessment (1953)**: Robertson Panel concluded no evidence that UAP were Soviet artifacts. Panel noted the "absence of Russian propaganda on the subject" as possibly indicating "a possible Russian official policy" — suggesting the Soviets may have been deliberately silent.
- **Soviet scientific interest**: Multiple CIA reports document Soviet scientists investigating the phenomenon. The CIA report "Conversations with Soviet Scientists on the Subject of UFOs" (CIA-UAP-010, content unreadable due to scan quality) indicates the CIA was actively collecting on Soviet scientific perspectives.
- **Hungarian 1955 wave**: Civilian letters from Hungary (CIA-UAP-013, -018) reported widespread public excitement about "flying saucers" moving at 12,000 km/h. "Scores of scientists" were reportedly investigating under Soviet-bloc auspices. This suggests the phenomenon was acknowledged and studied, not suppressed, within the Soviet sphere.
- **Azerbaijan 1955 unconventional aircraft**: CIA report of a triangular object launched from a Soviet airfield suggests the possibility that some UAP sightings may have been Soviet experimental aircraft — or that the Soviets were encountering the same phenomena as the U.S.
- **Sary Shagan UAP (1973)**: A Soviet ABM test range employee observed a green circular UAP with expanding concentric rings at the Sary Shagan range in Kazakhstan — a weapon test site. The report was collected by the CIA and distributed in December 1973.
- **Cosmic ray phosphenes (1976)**: Soviet researchers (Dr. Akoyev) were investigating cosmic ray-induced light flashes in pilots — a potential confusion factor with UAP pilot reports.
- **Kardashev and Sakharov (1971)**: While their paper was purely theoretical (charged mass collapsing through spacetime), the CIA's collection of the paper in its UAP-related files suggests the agency was monitoring Soviet theoretical physics for concepts relevant to UAP propulsion.

## Documented Examples

- **November 1955, Hungary**: Multi-week UAP wave, objects at 12,000 km/h, scientific groups investigating — reported via civilian letter intercepted by CIA
- **October 4, 1955, Azerbaijan SSR**: Triangular object launched like a missile from Soviet airfield, witnessed by U.S. tourists
- **Late summer 1973, Sary Shagan, Kazakhstan**: Green circular UAP with concentric rings at ABM test range
- **1946 Swedish ghost rockets**: Preceded the U.S. 1947 wave; many intelligence assessments linked them to Soviet testing of captured German V-2 technology from Peenemünde
