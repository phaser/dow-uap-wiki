## Definition

The methodological challenge — pervasive in [[concepts/orbital-uap]] reports — of distinguishing genuine unidentified objects from spent rocket stages, jettisoned hardware, water/urine dump particulates, paint/Mylar fragments, or LM-jettison residue trailing the host spacecraft.

## Characteristics

- Cislunar geometry favors confusion: at long ranges, debris can have ambiguous shapes that switch under focus changes (Apollo 11's "hollow cylinder ↔ open book" through the sextant).
- Apollo crews explicitly considered S-IVB stages, SLA adapter panels, high-gain antenna fragments, Mylar, and water dumps as candidate identifications.
- The "limit of optical resolution" plus passive-thermal-control "barbecue roll" (PTC) conspire to make repeated independent observation difficult.
- Skylab crews experienced a milder form: known Earth-orbit debris populations interleave with the unidentified targets.

## Documented Examples

- Apollo 11 hollow-cylinder object, ~1 day from the Moon — crew speculated on S-IVB, SLA panel, LM Mylar debris, high-gain antenna. None definitively confirmed in debrief. See [[summaries/nasa-uap-d4-apollo-11-technical-crew-debriefing-1969]]
- Apollo 17 post-S-IVB-sep tumbling debris field — see [[summaries/nasa-uap-d2-apollo-17-transcript-1972]]
