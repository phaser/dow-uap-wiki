## Definition

"Two-deck aircraft" refers to a specific and recurring description of UAP in the Project SIGN files: a wingless, cigar-shaped craft with **two rows of illuminated windows** suggesting an upper and lower deck (passenger/structure level). The description appeared in at least two independent sightings on different continents within 4 days of each other in July 1948, making it one of the most specific and cross-corroborated object descriptions in the early U.S. Air Force catalog.

## Characteristics

- **Cigar/cylindrical fuselage**: Approximately 100 ft long, diameter roughly twice that of a B-29 fuselage
- **No wings**: Explicitly noted in both reports
- **Two rows of windows**: Square windows (~1-2 ft per side), "upper and lower deck" configuration, with bright internal illumination
- **Propulsion**: Flames/jet exhaust from rear (~50 ft length), blue fluorescent glow beneath fuselage
- **Speed**: 500-700+ MPH at relatively low altitude (~5,000 ft)

## Documented Examples

- **Chiles-Whitted Encounter (24 July 1948)** — [[entities/chiles-whitted-encounter]], [[summaries/dow-uap-d088-us-airforce-analysis-of-flying-objects-in-the-us-101-172]] Incident #144: Eastern Air Lines DC-3 pilots Chiles and Whitted observed a 100-ft cigar-shaped craft with two rows of windows indicating an upper and lower deck, blue fluorescent glow, and 50-ft red-orange exhaust flame, near Montgomery, Alabama. Passenger corroboration.
- **The Hague, Netherlands (20 July 1948)** — [[summaries/dow-uap-d088-us-airforce-analysis-of-flying-objects-in-the-us-101-172]] Incident #168: A.D. Otter, Chief Investigator of the Court of Damage, described an "aircraft with 2 decks and no wings" traveling at very high altitude at high speed comparable to V-2, seen 4 times through clouds. Visibility unlimited.

The Netherlands sighting occurred 4 days before Chiles-Whitted. Whether SIGN connected these two cases is unknown, but the specificity of "two decks" and "no wings" in both reports is striking.
