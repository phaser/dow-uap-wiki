## Definition

"**Unconventional aircraft**" is the deliberately-neutral designation introduced by **AFOIN Memorandum No. 4** of **15 February 1949**, signed by Maj. Gen. Charles P. Cabell ([[entities/cabell-charles-p]]) as USAF Director of Intelligence. The memorandum reframed "flying disc / saucer" reports as observations of *possibly* foreign or *possibly* novel domestic aircraft, deliberately avoiding the [[concepts/extraterrestrial-hypothesis]] framing. The concept evolved as multiple intelligence reports documented objects that fit this category — craft that appeared technological but did not match any known aircraft — from Soviet-bloc military ranges to Himalayan borders to African airports.

## Characteristics

- The memo's vocabulary — "unconventional aircraft" — sits between the press's "flying saucer" and the eventual neutral acronym **UFO**.
- Functional purpose: keep the reporting pipeline open without making institutional commitments to any explanatory hypothesis.
- Lists candidate explanations: foreign (Soviet) advanced aircraft, domestic black-program aircraft, captured German technology, atmospheric/natural phenomena, hoax.
- **U-2/SR-71 connection**: The CIA has acknowledged that many "unidentified" sightings in the 1950s-1960s were actually U-2 and SR-71 flights at altitudes (~70,000 ft for U-2; 85,000+ ft for SR-71) far above commercial traffic. CIA and USAF deliberately left these unresolved in Blue Book investigations because explaining them would compromise the programs' existence. This makes the U-2 and OXCART programs the dominant source of "unconventional aircraft" reports in their era — but also a convenient cover for reports that were genuinely anomalous.
- The term also encompasses Soviet experimental craft: CIA reports document triangular objects with missile-type launch systems observed at Soviet airfields (Azerbaijan, 1955), rocket-like objects over the Himalayas (1968), and disc-shaped objects over African airports (Zimbabwe, 2008).
