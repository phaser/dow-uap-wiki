## Definition

The practice of explicitly grading the credibility of UFO/UAP witnesses, observed in [[entities/project-sign]] case files from 1947 onward and continuous through to modern AARO investigation. Witness reliability is treated as a primary input to whether a case is "Confirmed by other sources," "Insufficient data," or "No further action."

## Characteristics

- Project SIGN investigators routinely included verbatim assessments: *"appeared calm and normally sensible," "Witness appeared honest in his convictions,"* through to *"Mrs. Evans appears to be a 'crackpot'."*
- Investigator factors: occupation (military pilots, police officers, ATC controllers, scientists weighted highly), prior reporting history (repeat reporters down-weighted), demeanor during interview, internal consistency of account, alignment with corroborating witnesses.
- Witness reliability interacts with [[concepts/multi-witness-corroboration]]: a low-credibility single witness was usually filed as inconclusive, while a low-credibility witness whose account matched a high-credibility independent witness could be reclassified as confirmed.
- The Sign-era checklist (see [[concepts/incident-checklist-template]]) embeds this by requesting **occupation** as field 5.

## The Battelle Four-Factor System

Blue Book Special Report No. 14 (see `[[summaries/cia-uap-015-blue-book-special-report-14]]`) formalized reliability evaluation into four factors weighed in relation to each other:

1. **Experience** — Deduced from occupation, age, and training
2. **Consistency** — Internal coherence among the separate portions of the description
3. **Quality/completeness** — General quality and completeness of the report
4. **Fact-reporting ability and attitude** — Disclosed by the observer's manner of describing the sighting

These produced four reliability groups: Poor, Doubtful, Good, and Excellent. Excellent cases required instrumented values (radar data, theodolite measurements) and/or highly qualified observers (astronomers, pilots, scientists, surveyors, meteorologists, radar operators).

## Documented Examples

- Incident 50 / 50a, Wildwood, NJ, 10 January 1948 — Mrs. Ethel Evans dismissed as "crackpot" (see [[summaries/army-incident-summaries-1-100]])
- Conversely, the Muroc AAF cluster (Incidents 1–4) carried full investigator weight precisely because every witness was a serving USAF officer or test pilot
- Blue Book Special Report No. 14: 213 excellent-reliability sightings produced the highest unknown rate (33.3%) and lowest insufficient-information rate (1.2%) — a finding that contradicted the hypothesis that "more data would reduce unknowns" (see [[summaries/cia-uap-015-blue-book-special-report-14]])
