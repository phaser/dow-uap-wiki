## Overview

Andrei Dmitrievich Sakharov (May 21, 1921 – December 14, 1989) was a Soviet theoretical physicist who played a key role in developing the Soviet hydrogen bomb. He later became an internationally famous human rights activist and dissident, winning the Nobel Peace Prize in 1975. In 1971, he co-authored a speculative paper with N.S. Kardashev on charged mass collapsing in space, which the CIA collected as part of its intelligence monitoring of Soviet scientific developments.

## Role / Activity

- **1971 charged-mass paper**: Co-authored with Kardashev on what happens when a charged mass collapses — concluding it could "bounce back out" into "a different part of space time." According to the CIA report, after his presentation, Kardashev told a US attendee that Sakharov was "mostly interested in this charged mass phenomenon study because it indicates that the structure of space may be much more complicated than is presently thought."
- **UAP relevance**: The paper was purely theoretical physics, not about UFOs. However, the CIA's collection of it in UAP-related files suggests the agency recognized the potential implications of the physics for exotic propulsion or interdimensional travel concepts.
- **Broader context**: By 1971, Sakharov was already known as a dissident for his opposition to nuclear testing and advocacy for human rights. The CIA's ongoing monitoring of his scientific work continued even as he became an increasingly prominent critic of the Soviet regime. He was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1975 (which he was not permitted to collect in person) and was internally exiled to Gorky from 1980-1986.
