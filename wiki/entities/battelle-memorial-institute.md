## Overview

Battelle Memorial Institute was the research organization contracted by the USAF under Project STORK to perform statistical analysis of UFO sighting reports. Their work produced Project Blue Book Special Report No. 14 — the most comprehensive statistical analysis of UFO data ever conducted. Battelle's findings contradicted the official USAF public position and the report was distributed on a restricted basis.

## Role / Activity

- **Project STORK contract**: Worked under contract to ATIC/Blue Book to transfer sighting data onto IBM punch cards and perform systematic statistical analysis. The analysis covered 3,201 ALL SIGHTINGS reports (reduced to 2,199 OBJECT SIGHTINGS representing distinct phenomena), out of approximately 4,000 received by the end of 1952. About 800 reports were rejected as too nebulous or contradictory.

- **IBM punch card system**: Battelle developed the entire data processing pipeline — three iterations of a questionnaire form, a master coding system (CODES), intermediate WORK SHEET (CARD BIBLE) forms, and a serial numbering system for tracking reports. A "panel of consultants" of scientists both within and outside ATIC provided guidance covering "major scientific fields and numerous specialized fields."

- **Blue Book Special Report No. 14** (5 May 1955, 312 pages): Key findings:
  - **Unknowns INCREASED with sighting reliability**: 16.6% for poor → 33.3% for excellent reliability. Excellent cases had the LOWEST "insufficient information" rate (1.2%), meaning evaluators had data but still couldn't explain them.
  - **Six chi-square tests** compared KNOWN vs. UNKNOWN distributions; 5 of 6 showed probability <1% that the two populations were the same. However, the report **explicitly declined to compute a combined probability**, stating the analysis would not be carried to its "logical conclusion...since it was felt that the inaccuracies in the reports would give a distorted and meaningless result." The results were characterized as "inconclusive."
  - **Important note**: The "less than 1 in 10^28" odds claim attributed to this report in secondary sources does NOT appear in the report text. The report's chi-square tests show <1% for individual characteristics but never combine them into an overall probability. This figure likely derives from external re-analysis.
  - **The "Flying Saucer" model attempt**: Battelle identified 12 "good UNKNOWN" cases with sufficient detail to attempt constructing a physical model. After detailed case-by-case analysis, concluded "it is not possible to derive a verified model of a 'flying saucer' from the data." The 12 cases were internally contradictory — each had unique features (propellers, portholes, windows, fins, flames) that conflicted with the others.
  - **"Good Unknown" cases** (see `[[summaries/cia-uap-015-blue-book-special-report-14]]` for full details): Included a 100-foot disc with portholes observed by two airline pilots (>1000 mph, Case XI), a hovering craft with a visible occupant and propellers (Case XII, considered the single most compelling case), a cigar-shaped object witnessed by DC-3 pilots with rocket-like exhaust and two rows of windows (Case V), and a metallic object observed with 8-power binoculars whose "skin was dirty" (Case X).
  - **Radar-only sightings dismissed**: Report states unidentified radar returns are "most logically" ground targets reflected by temperature inversion layers and "are of no significance whatsoever unless a visual sighting of the object also is made."
  - **Psychological observation**: The report notes a "universal" experience among project personnel — reading a few reports "convinces" the reader that saucers are real, but this fades as more reports are read until reaching a "point of saturation" requiring "conscious effort...to remain objective."

- **1953-1955 data**: A separate evaluation of 854 post-1952 reports showed unknown rates declining (9% in 1953-1954, 3% in early 1955). Battelle attributed this to improved rapid-response field investigations by the 4602d AISS under AF Reg. 200-2.

- **Methodological innovations**:
  - Three-tier sighting classification: ALL SIGHTINGS (reports), UNIT SIGHTINGS (events), OBJECT SIGHTINGS (phenomena) — still the most sophisticated framework for de-duplicating sighting data
  - Sun-position calculation for every sighting to identify mock suns, specular reflections, and illumination conditions
  - Four-person identification conference with mandatory group consensus for any UNKNOWN classification
  - Four-factor observer reliability rating: experience/occupation/age/training, internal consistency, report quality, and fact-reporting ability/attitude
