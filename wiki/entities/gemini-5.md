## Overview

Gemini 5 (GT-5) was launched August 21, 1965, crewed by L. Gordon Cooper Jr. (Command Pilot) and Charles "Pete" Conrad Jr. (Pilot). The 8-day, 120-revolution mission was the longest manned spaceflight at the time and tested fuel cell power, rendezvous radar (with a deployed Radar Evaluation Pod / REP), and long-duration crew endurance. The OAMS thruster system degraded significantly over the mission, with multiple thrusters failing by day 5.

## Role / Activity

- First use of fuel cells for electrical power on a U.S. crewed mission.
- Deployed and repeatedly tracked a Radar Evaluation Pod (REP) for rendezvous practice; the REP blanket became a persistent visual reference.
- Crew reported extensive visual observations including: zodiacal light, 7th-magnitude stars, an aurora-type green luminous phenomenon in the airglow layer, meteorites, and one unidentified object during drifting flight.
- Extensive geographical photography — cities, roads, airports, storms, desert wind tracks, and cloud formations.
- The mission demonstrated that windows must be cleaner for optimal celestial visibility; the crew could see 7th magnitude stars but noted coating degradation.
