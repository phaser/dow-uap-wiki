## Overview

Gemini 7 (GT-7) was launched December 4, 1965, crewed by Frank Borman (Command Pilot) and James A. Lovell Jr. (Pilot). The 14-day, 206-revolution mission was the longest manned spaceflight until Skylab and served as the passive target for the Gemini 6A rendezvous — the first crewed-vehicle rendezvous in space. The mission is notable for Borman's in-flight "bogey at 10 o'clock high" call, preserved in separate PAO transcripts.

## Role / Activity

- Longest crewed duration at the time (330 hours), testing crew endurance, fuel cell systems, and extended orbital operations.
- Passive rendezvous target for Gemini 6A (Schirra and Stafford), which launched December 15, 1965 and performed station-keeping at distances as close as 1 foot.
- Crew conducted optical communications laser experiment (MSC-4) with ground sites at White Sands and Hawaii.
- Station-keeping with the spent GLV second stage after insertion; the booster vented fuel and created debris fields.
- Borman reported sighting an unidentified satellite at 16:40 GET — lower altitude, higher inclination, passed underneath Gemini 7, described as "a reflection" like "a sighting from the earth."
- The "bogey" call is documented in the separate T-00763 PAO transcript (see [[summaries/255_t_763_r1b_transcripts]] and [[entities/borman-gemini-7]]).
