## Overview

Dr. Josef Allen Hynek (May 1, 1910 – April 27, 1986) was an American astronomer at Ohio State University. He served as Scientific Consultant to Project Blue Book from 1948 to 1969 — the longest continuous involvement of any scientist with the U.S. Air Force UFO investigation. He was an associate member of the CIA's 1953 Robertson Panel and is known for his evolution from UFO skeptic to one of the most prominent advocates for scientific UFO study.

## Role / Activity

- **Blue Book Scientific Consultant (1948-1969)**: Began his association with the conviction that all sightings could be conventionally explained. "Even though doubts grew in his mind, he found himself obliged to support official USAF public policy" (Australian DoD assessment). After 1966, became increasingly outspoken against the USAF attitude.
- **Robertson Panel associate member**: Participated in the panel's 4-day review but was not a voting member. Contributed astronomical expertise alongside Thornton Page. Suggested that amateur astronomers might be "a potential source of enthusiastic talent to spread the gospel" of the educational debunking program.
- **Amateur astronomer proposal**: Proposed that a program of UFO watching could be an adjunct to planned astronomical programs at little cost, with trained astronomical personnel potentially photographing unidentified objects.
- **Condon Committee disillusionment**: Initially supported the Condon Committee but "became disillusioned and critical of it with the passing of time."
- **Post-Blue Book**: After his contract was not renewed (1969, after 21 years), Hynek went on to found the Center for UFO Studies (CUFOS), develop the "Close Encounter" classification system (CE1-CE3, later CE4-CE5), and serve as consultant to Steven Spielberg's "Close Encounters of the Third Kind." He became one of the most scientifically credible voices arguing for serious UFO investigation.
- **Evolution of views**: The Australian DoD assessment notes Hynek's trajectory as significant — from "conviction that all sightings could be conventionally explained" to "obliged to support official USAF public policy" despite growing doubts, to public opposition. This trajectory exemplifies the institutional pressure faced by scientists within the Blue Book/ATIC system.
