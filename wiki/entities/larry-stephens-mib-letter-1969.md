## Overview

**Larry Stephens** (Rt. 12 Box 206, Del City OK 73115) wrote J. Edgar Hoover on **14 May 1969** asking the Bureau to confirm or refute rumors then circulating in UFO subcultural press concerning the **Men-in-Black** phenomenon. Stephens's letter is significant because it lays out the MIB folklore in unusual structural completeness and lodges it directly with the FBI for response — which is, as far as the file shows, the **earliest such inquiry in the 62-HQ-83894 corpus**.

Stephens wrote: "There currently are rumors over the grapevine and in print that suggest **men with oriental features wearing dark clothes go around terrorizing people who have had close-up views of UFO's**. It is also rumored these creatures have impersonated armed forces officers and FBI investigators. They are supposed to ride around in **black or dark automobiles that either have old licence tags or none at all**. Several are reported to have attempted to **run down witnesses of UFO sightings, made disturbing, almost macabre phone calls, silenced several investigators** who were supposed to have learned some dark secret about extraterrestrial craft or mission plans and **opened mail, tapped telephones and even taken pictures of several homes where UFO witnesses lived.**"

The Bureau's reply (21 May 1969, signed via Helen W. Gandy on Hoover's behalf): "the FBI has no information it can send to you regarding the rumors you have heard." A copy of Stephens's letter was forwarded to OSI / USAF.

## Role / Activity

- A **canonical primary-source articulation of the MIB folklore** at a transitional moment — late-1960s, after the popularization by Albert K. Bender and Gray Barker (whose *They Knew Too Much About Flying Saucers* is preserved as Serial 403 of the same FBI HQ file — see [[summaries/62-hq-83894-serial-403]]), but several years before John Keel's *The Mothman Prophecies* (1975) re-popularized the trope to mass audiences.
- Stephens's specifics ("oriental features", "old licence tags or none at all", surveillance methods drawn from contemporaneous Cold-War tradecraft imagery) capture how the folklore had crystallized by 1969.
- The Bureau's response models its standing protocol: deny information, refer to OSI, take no investigative action — even when the inquiry alleges impersonation of FBI personnel (a federal offense the Bureau *did* normally investigate). This mirrors the Bureau's posture toward [[entities/ivan-t-sanderson|Ivan Sanderson]]'s UAO-book chapters two years earlier (July 1967), which also alleged FBI-Agent impersonation.
