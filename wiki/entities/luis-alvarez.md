## Overview

Dr. Luis Walter Alvarez (June 13, 1911 – September 1, 1988) was an American experimental physicist at the University of California, Berkeley. He served on the CIA's 1953 Robertson Panel on Unidentified Flying Objects and later won the Nobel Prize in Physics (1968) for his contributions to particle physics using hydrogen bubble chambers. His participation on the panel is significant because his technical credibility lent weight to the panel's skeptical conclusions.

## Role / Activity

- **Panel participation**: One of five voting members of the Robertson Panel. Contributed expertise in physics, radar, and instrumentation.
- **Key contributions**: (1) Personally investigated Foo Fighter phenomena during WWII alongside Robertson and David T. Griggs; (2) Dismissed two cases where cosmic ray coincidence counters allegedly behaved anomalously during saucer sightings as "instrumental effects" — examining circuit diagrams and quickly identifying the cause; (3) Proposed a simpler technical solution to the radar mutual interference problem (alternative to Berkner's "Doppler filter"); (4) Participated in the panel's rejection of the Navy Photo Interpretation Lab's analysis of the Tremonton film — contributed technical critiques of the methodology.
- **Post-panel career**: Awarded Nobel Prize in Physics (1968) for the development of the hydrogen bubble chamber and discovery of resonance states. Later known for the Alvarez hypothesis (asteroid impact causing dinosaur extinction) with his son Walter Alvarez.
- **Scientific approach to UFOs**: Represented the hard-nosed experimental physicist's perspective — demanded repeatable, well-documented evidence; dismissed anecdotal accounts; quickly identified instrumentation errors in seemingly anomalous data.
