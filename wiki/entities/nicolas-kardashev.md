## Overview

Nicolai Semenovich Kardashev (April 25, 1932 – August 3, 2019) was a Soviet astrophysicist at the Institute for Space Research in Moscow. He is best known for proposing the Kardashev Scale — a method of classifying civilizations by their energy consumption (Type I: planetary, Type II: stellar, Type III: galactic). In 1971, he co-authored a paper with Andrei Sakharov on charged mass collapsing into black holes, which was collected by the CIA as part of its intelligence on Soviet scientific developments.

## Role / Activity

- **1971 Yerevan Conference paper**: Presented a speculative paper co-authored with Andrei Sakharov at the Conference on the Origins of Life in Yerevan, Armenia (September 6-8, 1971). The paper concerned what happens when a charged mass collapses in space — concluding it could "bounce" back into "a different part of space time."
- **Research motivation**: Kardashev was interested in the astrophysical implications of the charged-mass-bounce phenomenon. His approach to scientific problems was described as "very much like his superior and former teacher I.S. Shklovskiy — not afraid to speculate in his effort to find a solution."
- **CIA collection significance**: The CIA's collection of this paper in its UAP intelligence files suggests the agency was monitoring Soviet theoretical physics for concepts potentially relevant to exotic propulsion, even if the paper itself was not about UFOs.
- **Kardashev Scale**: His most famous contribution — proposed in 1964 — classified civilizations based on energy usage: Type I (10^16 watts, planetary), Type II (10^26 watts, stellar), Type III (10^36 watts, galactic). The scale has been widely adopted in SETI and astrobiology discussions.
