## Overview

Peter Bartkus and Theodore McColm were amateur astronomers in Rockford, Illinois, who observed a spherical object ascending from the moon through a 6" Cassegranian reflector telescope on September 5, 1954.

## Role / Activity

Between 10:35 and 11:15 PM, observing the moon through a 6" Cassegranian reflector (100" focal length, 150 and 200 power oculars), Bartkus observed a spherical object ascending from the northern section of Mare Humboldtianum. The object was not glowing or brilliant, seemed like dull reflected light of a planet. Size estimated at diameter of craters Pitiscus or Vlaco. At 11:15, had traveled more than 29° 30" of arc in about 40 minutes. Object was ascending while moon was descending at 1081 mph — "object must have been a powered body because... ascending and not following a true orbit." McColm through calculus estimated object to be 12,500 ft. in diameter. Both checked out atmospheric diffraction; object "was definitely in space, and in the same field as the moon." McColm provided drawings of object's size and movement at 4-minute intervals. The case was published in BSRA's Round Robin and cited by the editor as transcending "any notion that 'saucer' vehicles are confined to earth's atmosphere."
