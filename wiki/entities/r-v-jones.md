## Overview

Dr. Reginald Victor Jones (September 29, 1911 – December 17, 1997) was a British physicist and scientific intelligence expert. During WWII, he served as Assistant Director of Intelligence (Science) at the Air Ministry, where he was instrumental in the "Battle of the Beams" — countering German radio navigation systems. By 1952, he headed a British standing committee on flying saucers, which concluded that UFO observations were not enemy aircraft and that none had been over Britain.

## Role / Activity

- **British UFO committee head**: Presided over a standing committee on flying saucers created approximately August 1951. The CIA learned of this through a messenger from Britain who reported to H. Marshall Chadwell on December 15, 1952. The committee included RAF personnel as "action people."
- **Committee conclusions**: Observations were not enemy aircraft and none had been over Britain.
- **Yorkshire incident (December 1952)**: A "perfect flying saucer" observed by RAF officials and pilots during an air show disturbed Jones because it generated public press attention — he recognized that "the creation of the correction of public opinion is a part of his responsibilities."
- **Historical parallel**: Referenced Chladni's 1760-1780 paper on meteorites (which proved that stones could fall from the sky — once dismissed by the French Academy of Sciences) — implicitly suggesting that something dismissed today could later prove real.
- **Interest in Tremonton film**: Through the CIA messenger, Jones learned of the Tremonton, Utah UFO film and indicated he might write requesting a copy.
- **WWII background**: Designer of scientific intelligence methods; discovered the German Knickebein, X-Gerät, and Y-Gerät radio beam navigation systems; identified the V-1 and V-2 programs before operational deployment. Published his memoirs as "Most Secret War" (1978), considered a classic of intelligence literature.
- **Connection to Robertson**: Jones' WWII CROSSBOW experience (investigating V-1/V-2) was directly referenced by H.P. Robertson during the Robertson Panel as the model for how intelligence should operate — finding physical "hardware" (crashed vehicles in Sweden) gave investigators a basis to work from, unlike the UFO problem.
