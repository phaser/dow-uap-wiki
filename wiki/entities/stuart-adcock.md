## Overview

Stuart E. Adcock was a Knoxville, Tennessee radio operator and ham radio enthusiast who operated radio station WROL. He is known for using a surplus Army APN-7 radar set installed in his home to detect unidentified objects at 40,000–100,000 ft directly over the Oak Ridge nuclear facility in March 1950.

## Characteristics

- Recognized as a "reputable and substantial citizen" in Knoxville
- Credited as a skilled radio technician with development and research work in radio
- Claimed familiarity with the Navy's radar equipment from assisting in its development at Harvard University during WWII
- Found to have "some degree of inebriation" on multiple visits by USAF investigators
- Technically incorrect on several matters of radar theory and practice per Captain Cross (NEPA radar expert)
- Reliability ultimately deemed "extremely dubious" by Colonel Gasser after Adcock could not be found at his residence on March 5

## Role / Activity

From March 1–8, 1950, Adcock detected objects on his home radar at altitudes of 40,000–100,000 ft over Oak Ridge TN. His detections triggered a multi-agency response: AEC Security Division, OSI (Mackell Field, Alabama), CIA (technician dispatched from Washington), Naval Reserve radar, 3rd Army, and USAF NEPA Oak Ridge. Colonel Gasser and Captain Cross examined his equipment and found him technically wrong on radar theory. Despite questions about his reliability, Gasser stated that flight at 100,000 ft was "absolutely possible from a scientific and engineering standpoint."

The AEC's detailed procedural assessment of this incident is preserved in section 5 and is notable for documenting inter-agency coordination gaps — "an impressive lack of any agency actually taking the responsibility."
