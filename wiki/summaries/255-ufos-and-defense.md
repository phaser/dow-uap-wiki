## Source Metadata

- **Originating agency** (of the report): **COMETA** (Comité d'Études Approfondies) — a private French committee of high-ranking former auditors of the Institut des Hautes Études de Défense Nationale (IHEDN), generals, defense officials, and scientists. Originally published as "Les OVNI et la Défense: à quoi doit-on se préparer?" in a special issue of *VSD* magazine, July 1999. The English translation in this PDF dates from 2000-2001.

## Key Points

### Provenance and significance
- The COMETA Report (Foreword by **André Lebeau**, former chairman of CNES) is one of the most significant European governmental-adjacent assessments of the UFO question. While COMETA is a private association, its membership consists almost entirely of senior French defense, intelligence, and aerospace figures (former Institut des Hautes Études de Défense Nationale auditors), giving the report quasi-official weight.
- Cover letter: Carol Rosin (former von Braun spokesperson) hand-delivered the English report to NASA in 2001; her note urges "your perusal" and emphasizes the report's "supportive documentation" via cases, particularly Air France flight AF 3532 (Capt. Jean-Charles Duboc).

### Structure
- Preface, Foreword, Introduction.
- Part 1: Facts and Testimonies (Chapters 1-5) — French pilot testimonies, aeronautic cases worldwide, ground sightings, close encounters in France, counter-examples of explained phenomena.
- Part 2: The Extent of Our Knowledge (6-9) — French research org, GEPAN/SEPRA, hypotheses & modeling, foreign organization.
- Part 3: UFOs and Defense (10-14) — strategic, aeronautical, scientific & technical, political/religious, media implications.
- Conclusion and Recommendations.
- Appendices include Radar Detection in France, and Appendix 5: "The Roswell Affair — Disinformation."

### Headline cases (pages 1-30 sample)
- **M. Hervé Giraud Mirage IV pilot encounter, March 7, 1977** — Dijon flyover at 9600 m, Mach 0.9. Two successive "assailants" (A1, A2) on collision course; pilot performed up to 6.5 g evasive bank; targets always re-positioned behind aircraft; never picked up by Contrexéville military radar. Implication: maneuvers at apparent supersonic speed without sonic boom, no radar contact.
- **Colonel Claude Bosc T-33 trainer encounter, March 3, 1976** — Tours-Rennes-Nantes-Poitiers itinerary, 6000 m altitude. Green flare-like object exceeded altitude by 1500 m in 1-2 seconds, reversed course, came on collision path, aircraft enveloped in "very bright phosphorescent green light," sphere ~1-2 m diameter with magnesium-fire white core and green comet-like tail passed over right wing within fraction of a second. Two other pilots in the same flight stream confirmed seeing it from a distance; ground radar no contact.
- **Air France Flight AF 3532, January 28, 1994** — Capt. Jean-Charles Duboc, Copilot Valérie Chauffour. Cruising at 11,900 m near Coulommiers; brown-bell-shaped phenomenon transformed into chestnut-brown lens shape ~50 km away at ~10,500 m altitude. Disappeared instantaneously. Reims ATC informed Taverny Air Defense Operations Center (CODA); CODA confirmed a 50-second radar track from Cinq-Mars-la-Pile control center crossing AF 3532's trajectory, no flight plan filed, weather-balloon hypothesis ruled out, estimated craft length **~250 m**. CRNA-North handles 3000 movements/day and has investigated only 3 UAP cases in 7 years; AF 3532 was one.
- **Lakenheath / Bentwaters (UK), August 13-14, 1956** — Detailed multi-radar + visual + RAF Venom interceptor case from Condon Commission's "unidentified" file; Thayer (Astronautics & Aeronautics 1971) and MacDonald (1969) both judged it strongest known case. Object made 2000-4000 mph passes, abrupt stops, reversed pursuit on RAF Venom locking weapons. Followed pilot at constant distance.
- **RB-47 Aircraft, July 17, 1957** — RB-47 over south-central U.S. detected pulsed microwave emissions from a luminous unidentified object, plus visual + ground-radar correlation; MacDonald (1971) and Brad Sparks (1997) re-investigations. Object position correlated radar/microwave/visual sightings simultaneously.

### Roswell appendix (pages 60-90 sample)
- COMETA includes Appendix 5 "The Roswell Affair — Disinformation" which presents the case as a serious data point.
- Key COMETA-stated facts (per their reading):
  - Summer 1947: Roswell base houses the only nuclear-armed bomb group in the world.
  - July 4, 1947 (~2330 hrs): per COMETA's reading, "the crash occurred on July 4, Independence Day."
  - July 8 morning: Roswell base provides local radio stations with the famous "flying disk crashed on a ranch and military personnel" announcement.
  - General Ramey then substitutes weather-balloon debris for Marcel's recovered material.
  - September 1994: Office of the Secretary of the Air Force publishes Roswell report: debris not aircraft or missile, "probably debris from a series of balloons from the secret Mogul project" — Ramey's weather-balloon ruse claimed as cover for Project Mogul.
- COMETA cites a GAO inquiry and TF1 (French TV) videos of principal witnesses.
- COMETA discusses the "Extraterrestrial Biological Entities (EBEs)" hypothesis and references claims that some U.S. base in New Mexico/Nevada is jointly owned by the U.S. Army and "extraterrestrials baptized EBEs." (Editorial caveat: this is reported as a claim, not endorsed as fact.)
- Discusses recommendations of a "disinformation policy" intensified by an unspecified U.S. committee that allegedly recommended "monitoring ufological movements."

### Conclusions and Recommendations (pages 60-90)
- COMETA's headline judgment: "**The extraterrestrial hypothesis is by far the best scientific hypothesis.**"
- Recommends:
  - Provide civilian and military pilots sufficient information to teach adapted conduct when faced with these phenomena.
  - Develop SEPRA and promote supplemental scientific monitoring/research.
  - Consider strategic, political, and religious consequences of a possible confirmation of the extraterrestrial hypothesis.
  - "Formulating all prospective hypotheses."
- Notes that, per Sturrock-style polling, many French scientific colleagues "would refuse to" engage publicly even if they had data — the report's authors push for de-stigmatization.
