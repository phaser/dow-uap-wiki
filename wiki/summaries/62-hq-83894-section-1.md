## Key Points

- Section 1 captures the FBI's first weeks of receiving "flying disc" reports following the Kenneth Arnold sighting (24 June 1947) and the Roswell-area events of early July 1947.
- The Bureau's mailroom is flooded with newspaper clippings, citizen letters to Director [[entities/j-edgar-hoover]], and field-office teletypes from across the country.
- An early teletype (7-8 July 1947, New Orleans) reports that one allegedly recovered "disc" turned out to be a prank.
- A 5 August 1947 letter from Hoover thanks Fred R. Reibold for his "flying discs" letter and notes the matter has been forwarded to the War Department, attention Colonel L. R. Forney, Chief, Security Group.
- An informant memo (SAC New Haven, 18 July 1947) details Edwin M. Bailey, Jr. of Stamford, Connecticut — an American Cyanamid Research Laboratories physicist, formerly of MIT Radiation Laboratory / Manhattan Project — who advanced his own theory: that "flying saucers" might be **radio-controlled germ bombs or atom bombs orbiting the earth**, citing sightings on what he called a great-circle line through Mexico City, New Orleans, Philadelphia, New York, Boston, Halifax, Newfoundland, Paris, Milan, Bologna, Yugoslavia, and Albania.
- Prank/hoax reports: a Catholic priest at Grafton, Wisconsin reportedly found a "round metal disc" with "gadgets and wires" — later identified by Dr. C. L. Kenny (Creighton College of Dentistry) as ashes of pipe tobacco; the "Combined and Amalgamated Committee of Sky-Scanners, Disc Decipherers and New-Product Introducers" mass-mails a satirical promotional letter dated 11 July 1947 that ends up forwarded to Hoover by Mrs. J. M. Clark of Westport, Connecticut.
- An FBI Seattle teletype (14 August 1947) discusses the Maury Island players Fred Crisman and [[entities/harold-dahl]] (Tacoma, Washington); notes Dahl did NOT admit to SAC Smith that the story was a hoax — only that he intended to claim it was a hoax if questioned, to avoid further trouble — and that Crisman or Dahl probably made the anonymous phone call to the press hoping to "build up their story" for a profitable deal with Fantasy magazine, Chicago.
- Numerous routine acknowledgement letters from Hoover to citizens (e.g., J. U. Watts of Darlington, SC; John Coper of San Marcos, TX) thanking them for their flying-disc reports and informing them the matter is War-Department business.
- Considerable contemporary newspaper material is pasted in: Washington Post (July 7 1947) coverage of "Whirring Disc" found in a Wisconsin priest's yard, "Flying Saucers Reported From 39 States" wire stories, etc. Sightings cataloged include reports from Hagerstown, MD, Idaho (St. Maries — alleged landing of eight saucers seen by 10 witnesses), Los Angeles area, Halifax, and Hazen Kennedy of 2615 4th St NE Washington DC ("orange lamp bulb without the socket... over 1000 mph at 1200-1500 ft altitude").

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **Maury Island incident (Tacoma, WA, 21 June 1947)** — discussed in this section as background for Dahl/Crisman correspondence; covered in detail in Section 3. See [[entities/maury-island-incident]].
- **Grafton, WI "priest's disc" (early July 1947)** — Catholic priest found 18-inch metal disc with hole and "gadgets and wires"; later analyzed as pipe-tobacco ash residue.
- **St. Maries, ID landing claim (early July 1947)** — A. Dishman, Idaho housewife, with 10 witnesses says eight wash-tub-sized saucers descended onto a mountainside near St. Maries.
- **Hazen Kennedy Washington DC sighting (~early July 1947)** — student pilot reports a saucer over the NE section of the District of Columbia at 8:40 pm, "well over 1000 mph" at 1200–1500 ft.
- **Mrs. Mary Ganoe, Hagerstown MD (1947)** — three discs in V-formation at "terrific speed" over her back porch, sounding "like a faraway train."

## Open Questions

- How many of the citizen letters in this section are from individuals who already had FBI files for other reasons? (The Bureau's habitual cross-indexing seems to be running on every name.)
- What did Col. Forney's Security Group at the War Department do with the material the FBI forwarded? (Section 1 only documents the hand-off.)
- Was Bailey's "orbital atom-bomb" theory ever taken seriously by FBI leadership, or was it filed and forgotten? The fact it was retained suggests the Atomic Energy Commission angle attracted some notice.
- The famous **Guy Hottel memo** (22 March 1950, three "saucers" with humanoid bodies near a New Mexico AEC installation) — frequently advertised as the most-viewed page in the FBI's Vault — was not encountered in this sampled span; based on date it may live in Section 5 or as a serial elsewhere in the same file series.
