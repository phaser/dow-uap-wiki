## Key Points

### Maury Island / Kelso B-25 Crash (central thread)

- **Air Rescue Service Mission #39 Final Report (4 August 1947)**: at 0400 PST 1 Aug 1947, a B-25 (#1316) departed McChord Field at 0212 PST for Hamilton Field, California. Crashed in flames near Kelso, Washington (13-20 miles east, vicinity of Goble Creek; coordinates 46°09'N 122°43'W). Sgt. E. L. Taff and T/Sgt. W. D. Mathews bailed out and survived; the pilot **Capt. William L. Davidson** and co-pilot **Lt. Frank M. Brown** (CIC Sub-Detachment Commander, A-2 Fourth Air Force) died. Cause: fire in the left engine — exhaust stack burned through, caught left wing. Wing ripped off just outboard of left engine before impact. Crew chief Mathews' detailed account: flames engulfed left side almost immediately; Taff bailed at ~10,000 ft; Mathews helped Davidson and Brown attach chest packs, left plane as co-pilot was "preparing to follow." Davidson partially standing, holding control wheel with left hand. Mathews lit in a tree, cut himself loose with pocket knife. Before leaving plane, saw an "object afire thrown clear" — didn't know if person or part of plane. Top Secret material was aboard (navigator's kit).
- **CIC-R1 Counter-Intelligence Report by Lt. Col. Donald L. Springer (28 August 1947, classified Confidential)**:
  - 31 July 1947: Kenneth Arnold telephones Lt. Brown from Idaho saying he has "very vital information" on flying discs. Brown and Davidson fly to McChord, change into civvies, meet Arnold, Capt. E. J. Smith (United Air Lines), Harold Dahl, and Fred Crisman at the Winthrop Hotel, Tacoma, night of 31 July.
  - Journalist who funded Arnold's trip: **Ray Palmer** (R. A. Palmer of Venture Press, 305 Studio Bldg, 1718 Sherman Avenue, Evanston IL — editor of *Amazing Stories* / *Fate*). Palmer paid Arnold $200. FBI checked Chicago indices, Chicago police, and Credit Bureau on Palmer/Venture Press — all negative (no record found).
  - **Palmer's June 26, 1947 letter to Arnold** (enclosed in file): requests article about Arnold's Mt. Rainier sighting, offers 2¢ per word + $5/photograph. Asks for photo of himself, his plane, and rough sketches.
  - **Palmer's August 5, 1947 letter to Arnold** (enclosed in file, post-crash): urges Arnold to continue investigation despite Davidson/Brown deaths. States "there's no horrible plot involved. It's probably true that the two men killed were just accidents. It could be true that it was not an accident, but I don't think there was any connection with the disks." Offers additional payment, says "that disk mystery" is "much too important to the people of America." Claims disks "have been known for nearly forty years, and I have ample proof of that." Says "those disks are not red corpses in your eyeball."
  - Dahl claimed six donut-shaped craft (~100 ft across, 25-ft center holes) dropped "tons" of material onto his boat near Maury Island on 21 June 1947, killing his dog. Crisman claimed additional sighting at sand pit. Both gave Brown samples.
  - **Springer's CIC investigation conclusion**: no evidence the dropped material was unusual; samples consistent with ordinary metal-foundry slag; boat shows no damage consistent with "tons" of debris. Crisman identified as 406th AAF reserve captain (Serial 0758951) with checkered employment record. **Dahl's FBI record**: charged with two incidents of illegal wearing of uniform and one with theft of government property.
  - **Springer's recommendations**: no further AAF investigation; consideration to revoke Crisman's Air Reserve commission as "an undesirable and unreliable officer."
  - **Box of "disc fragments"**: Crisman brought a box of alleged Maury Island fragments to the Winthrop Hotel. Shown to Brown and Davidson. **Major George Sander** (McChord PRO) examined them and concluded they were "nothing but slag from the copper mill." Brown gathered up every fragment and took them back to Hamilton Field.
  - **Arnold's sworn deposition (notarized)**: detailed first-person narrative of July 31 – August 3 events. Confirms Dahl's story, Palmer's involvement, the meeting at the Winthrop, the handover of the fragment box to Davidson/Brown. Includes timeline of phone calls after the crash.
  - **Arnold's FBI interview (27 August 1947, by SA Joseph E. Jette)**: Arnold states he thinks Palmer's business is a "blind for something else" and that Dahl/Crisman "will do anything Palmer asks." Called Palmer post-crash to return $200; Palmer said keep it and offered additional check. Arnold's FBI interviewer (Lt. Brown, in the July 16 report) assessed Arnold as "actually saw what he stated that he saw" and said if Arnold could fabricate this, "he should be writing Buck Rogers fiction." Arnold bitter about press ridicule: "if I see a ten story building flying through the air I would never say a word about it."
  - **Arnold's autobiographical statement (Exhibit A)**: born March 29, 1915 in Sublette, Minnesota; homesteaded in Scobey, Montana; Eagle Scout by 14; entered U.S. Olympic trials in fancy diving 1932; University of Minnesota; fire control engineer (Great Western Fire Control Supply); pilot since 1943; owns Callair airplane; has landed in 832 cow pastures with only one flat tire as "greatest mishap."
  - **Anonymous calls to Tacoma press**: five anonymous calls between July 31 – August 2. Caller to Tacoma Times reporter Paul Lantz and United Press wireman Ted Morello provided "blow-by-blow" descriptions of events in Arnold's hotel room (room 502). Caller knew names of dead officers before Army release, claimed B-25 was "shot down by a 20 mm. cannon." Stated "Don't think I'm doing this for you" and "All I am interested in is seeing that this information gets back to New Jersey." Later claimed one party was "flown to Alaska" and Smith would be called to Wright Field Tuesday. Lantz repeated back private room discussions to Arnold as verification of the tap.
  - **Ernie Vogel (AP wireman, Tacoma)**: Dahl admitted the entire story was false. Dahl's wife came to kitchen in "considerable rage" telling him to admit it was a "plain fantasy."
  - **Captain Smith's signed/stamped affidavit**: notarized narrative of July 31 – August 3 events. Includes Dahl's warning: "four or five men calling at his home warned him to forget about Maury Island." Dahl's 16-year-old son "run away from home" after incident, "picked up by police somewhere in Montana."
  - **FBI blind memorandum (August 19, 1947, by SA David A. MacCulloch)**: confirms Dahl and Crisman's story debunked. Concludes Crisman most likely made anonymous calls "in order to build up the flying disc story to the point where they could make a profitable sale of the story to Ray Palmer."

### Muroc Army Air Field Sightings (8 July 1947)

- **Major Richard R. Shoop** (Office of Chief of Tech. Engineering Division): at ~noon on 8 July, observed 5-8 miles north a thin metallic object, aluminum-colored, oscillating flight pattern near ground then climbing. Size of pursuit airplane, no conventional shape. Seen by his wife too. Object in view ~8 minutes.
- **1st Lt. Joseph C. McHenry** (Billeting Officer): at ~09:30 on 8 July, observed two silver spherical/disc-like objects at ~300 mph at 8,000 ft, heading 320° due north. Called Sgt. Nauman, T/Sgt. Ruvolo, and Miss Jamnette Marie Scott to verify — all three confirmed. Later sighted a third object circling tightly at 8,000 ft — too tight a circle for any aircraft, not weather balloon (traveling against prevailing wind), not birds (sun reflection). Ran to dispensary to get medical officers as additional witnesses; objects disappeared by then.
- **Capt. John Paul Strapp** (Flight Test, Muroc AAF): at 11:50 on 8 July, observed white aluminum rounded object resembling parachute canopy, falling at 3x rate of parachute. Drifted slightly NW against prevailing wind toward Mt. Wilson. **Ovular outline with two projections on upper surface** (thick fins or nobs) that crossed at intervals, suggesting rotation or oscillation of slow type. No smoke, flames, propeller marks, engine noise, or visible propulsion. Color: silvery, resembling aluminum-painted fabric. Not as dense as parachute canopy. Four of five people in observation truck saw it. **Estimated 90 seconds in vision.** Opinion: "man-made object, as evidenced distinctly by the outline and functional appearance."
- **Jamnette Marie Scott** (secretary to Lt. McHenry): at ~10:00 on 8 July, called outside by McHenry. Saw two silver disc-like objects toward Mojave at 300-400 mph at 8,000 ft. Heard no drone. Later sighted a third object in tight circle at 8,000 ft. On base ~18 months, familiar with all aircraft types.
- **S/Sgt. Gerald C. Nauman** (414th AAFBU): at 10:00 on 8 July, saw two flying discs NW direction at 7-8,000 ft, 300-400 mph. Minutes later, another object at same altitude doing "certain maneuvers such as flying in a tight circle." 20-20 vision, flying since 1943: "never in my life have I seen anything such as this."
- **AAF dismissal**: Col. Gilkey and Major Shoop determined "nothing to be reported" — no further investigation.

### R.A. Switzer Placerville Sighting (14 August 1947)

- **Ray A. Switzer** (insurance adjustor, Moduire & Wallis, Sacramento): driving Cedar Ravine Road ~5 mi SW of Placerville at ~1600 on 14 August. Saw white smoke trail, then object 4-6 ft long, 10-14 in wide, **bright like highly polished chromium**, traveling at terrific rate in shallow dive. At ~1200 ft distance, engulfed in puff of **dark gray smoke** ~10 ft diameter, then disappeared completely. No particles seen falling. Rectangular shape with slightly curved top surface. **Wife corroborated** (smoke dark gray, object ~5 ft long, ~1 ft wide, larger in front than rear). Sun behind observer, clear sky, bright reflection. Agent Moon's assessment: could have been aluminum-surfaced conventional aircraft; "no explanation as to the smoke trail observed."

### Ward L. Stewart Hamilton Field Sighting (29 July 1947)

- **Ward L. Stewart** (1st Lt Reserve, charge of machine shops at UC Berkeley): after 12 noon on 29 July with Capt. William H. Ryherd (415th AAF BU RT), observed unidentified flying object following a P-80 at "terrific rate of speed." P-80 estimated at ~250 mph on approach to Hamilton Field. Object maintained speed of **3-4 times that of the aircraft**. A moment later, a **second object** appeared, flew course "similar to a fighter aircraft's maneuvers" — left to right movement over first object. Maneuvering continued ~15 seconds, course ~120°. Estimated under 6,000 ft altitude. **Objects described as milky white, unlike any conventional aircraft.** Stewart certain objects were not Navy "Flapjack." Pilot of considerable experience.

### Byron B. Savage Oklahoma City Sighting (17-21 May 1947)

- **Byron B. Savage** (38, Field Engineer, RCA of America, Dallas TX; private pilot since 1934): between 17-21 May, just after dusk, observed object at ~160° south. Initially elliptical, then perfectly round and flat — disc shape, 10:1 diameter-to-thickness ratio, thicker in center. **Altitude: 10,000-18,000 ft. Size: bulk of six B-29s at same altitude.** 15-20 seconds in vision. Speed: ~3x jet-propelled aircraft. No trailing effects. **Frosty white color.** No protrusions. Swishing sound (like rushing air) a few seconds after passing — "not very loud, did not last very long." Called wife to see it but it had disappeared. Concluded: "radically built and powered, **probably atomic**." Not a meteor.

### Schulgen-Requested Background Investigations

- FBI directed by **General Schulgen** (Army Air Forces Intelligence) to investigate Kenneth Arnold and Byron Savage for subversive connections or ulterior motives. Schulgen's memo (24 July 1947) to Security Officer, OCAMA Tinker Field: "among the first to sight the alleged flying discs." Arnold's CIC interviewer (Brown) assessed him as credible: "difficult to believe that a man of Mr. Arnold's character and apparent integrity would state that he saw objects and write up a report to the extent that he did if he did not see them." Savage was cleared (no subversive record found).

### Redmond, Washington Sighting (13 August 1947)

- **L. R. Brummett and Sidney Decker** standing near Redmond Post Office at ~0900. Two very bright objects traveling at extreme rate, **no wings, no tail, both ends tapered, resembling a belly tank**, noiseless, at least 3x faster than a plane, traveling NE over Redmond. One slightly behind and above the other, maintaining equal speed. Watched 5-8 seconds, then disappeared. **Lighter than aluminum.** Mrs. Mamie English also saw them — described as "two silver balls traveling at a fast rate."

### Anchorage, Alaska Sighting (date unspecified, pre-Bureau Bulletin 42)

- Two army officers reported to Intelligence HQ, Alaskan Department, Fort Richardson, that they witnessed an object passing through air at tremendous speed. **Spherical (not saucer-like)**, metallic, silver, ~2-3 ft diameter. No vapor trail. Traveling **against the wind** — "one of the remarkable features." Second officer estimated ~10 ft diameter, altitude 3,000-4,000 ft, "broadside," no spinning motion or reflection. Observed 15-20 seconds, traveling due south at speed "considerably in excess of any plane." Exact date not furnished; no further investigation conducted.

### Danforth, Illinois "Instrument" Found on Farm

- Instrument found on farm near Danforth, Illinois. Examined by Air Force Intelligence and Wright Field. **Identified as old-type radio loudspeaker parts** — outmoded magnetic speaker diaphragm (Wathaniel Baldwin Company, Salt Lake City, patented May 1, 1910), bakelite coil forms crudely rewrapped, Polymet electronic filter capacitor, unidentified metallic ring. **No connection to Operation Mogul or any classified project.** Declared a "hoax." Mrs. Whedon (Army Engineers) had initially claimed connection to Mogul; Major Hopkins (Intelligence Division) refuted this.

### 4AF Intelligence Analysis of "Flying Saucer Specimens" (25 August 1947)

- **Col. H. M. McCoy** (Deputy Commanding General, Intelligence, 4AF) report on "alleged 'Flying Saucer' specimens" brought to him by Col. H. M. McCoy: examined by Analysis Division (T-2) and Electronics Sub-division (T-3). **Conclusion: all a hoax.** Specimens were:
  1. Plaster of paris or similar ceramic fragments with electrical resistance wire (heated electrically)
  2. Outmoded magnetic speaker diaphragm (Baldwin Company, Salt Lake City, patented May 1, 1910)
  3. Bakelite coil forms crudely rewrapped with enameled copper wire
  4. Electronic filter capacitor (Polymet Manufacturing Co., NY)
  4. Unidentified metallic ring
- "These specimens, therefore, are considered as part of a hoax that could be perpetrated by most anyone seeking publicity or for any other reasons."

### Guam Crescent-Shaped Objects (14 August 1947)

- Air Transport Command Weekly Intelligence Summary (20 August 1947): three American enlisted men of 147th Air Ways and Air Communications Service Squadron at **Harmon Field, Guam** observed two **small, crescent-shaped objects** at 1040 on 14 August. Traveling at **twice fighter-plane speed**, zigzag course, westerly direction, ~1,200 ft altitude. Objects disappeared into clouds; similar object emerged and proceeded west. No further details.

### Urie Family Snake River Canyon Sighting (13 August 1947)

- **A.C. Urie** (Auger Falls Trout Farm operator, 6 mi W of Blue Lakes, Idaho) and sons Billy (10) and Keith (8) at Salmon Dam area. At ~1300 on 13 August, saw **sky-blue contrivance** at ~75 ft altitude in Snake River Canyon (~1,000 mph). **Tubular fiery glow on side/top.** Trees "spun around on top as if they were in a vacuum." Urie opinion: "guided by instruments and be powered by atomic energy." County Commissioner **L.W. Hawkins** and **J.H. Brown** also saw two circular objects at great height with roaring sound, same day.

### Twin Falls, Idaho County Commissioner Sighting (13 August 1947)

- **L.W. Hawkins** (County Commissioner, ex-sheriff) and **J.H. Brown** (Idaho State Warehouse Inspector) fishing ~40 mi SW of Twin Falls at ~0930 on 13 August. Saw two objects flying at great height, appeared disc-shaped, moving rapidly, heard roar similar to motor truck. Hawkins: "I've been skeptical all along until I saw it with my own eyes."

### Bruce Armstrong / M.A. Nichols Boeing Sighting (12 August 1947)

- **Bruce Armstrong** (electronics technician) and ~10 research engineers at Boeing Aircraft headplant, Seattle, at ~2200 on 12 August. Saw small black object ~200 ft high, going south over Boeing Field runway. **Triangular shape, floated in air like a leaf**, estimated ~15 mph. **Control tower log ultimately attributed it to a large piece of burnt paper blown across runway** by wind.
- **M.A. Nichols** (1125 SW 102nd St, Seattle) at ~1600 on 12 August: object at ~35,000 ft, southwest direction. Neighbor Ray Stanley examined with binoculars — described as "rock moving, cylindrical," reflecting sun with purplish tinge. **Weather Bureau explained it as a weather balloon** (released every 6 hours from Boeing Field).

### Philadelphia Flaming Object (6 August 1947)

- Multiple corroborating witnesses at ~2245 on 6 August: **Eleanor Naddle** and Cecelia Fine (western Philadelphia) saw large white object traveling fast south with buzzing sound and thin gray smoke trail. **John Snyder** (former B-24 pilot, Army Air Corps) and **John Kelly** (retired police officer) + families (Oxford Circle, NE Philadelphia, ~10 mi from Naddle) saw bluish-white flaming object traveling NE to SW at 400-500 mph, 1000-3000 ft, 2-second smoke trail, hissing sound. Kelly: "no falling star, maintained same altitude." Wives verified accounts. Charles Lennig Chemical Company fire on Aug 6 ruled out (2.5 mi from Oxford Circle, wrong direction). Naval/Army Intelligence: no experimental work in area.

### FBI Institutional Posture

- **Springer's "restricted" letter (3 September 1947, by command of Lt. Gen. Stratemeyer, signed by Col. R. H. Smith, ADC Mitchel Field)**: established FBI/AAF cooperation — ADC Air Forces would interview responsible observers; **FBI would investigate incidents of "discs" found on the ground**. FBI enlisted to "relieve the numbered Air Forces of the task of tracking down all the many instances which turned out to be ash can covers, toilet seats and whatnot."
- **SAC San Francisco's objection (19 September 1947)**: wrote Director FBI objecting to the "scurrilous wordage" calling FBI investigations "ash can covers, toilet seats and whatnot" as "insulting to the Bureau." Recommended discussing with AAF and protesting the arrangement.
- **Ladd memo (25 September 1947)**: Bureau was asked to assist AAF to rule out Communist-caused "mass hysteria." Results "failed to reveal any indication of subversive individuals being involved." Recommended Bureau "protest vigorously" and **discontinue all activity in this field**.
- **Bureau Bulletin No. 57, Series 1947 (1 October 1947)**: the FBI formally **discontinued its flying disc investigations** and turned the matter over to the Air Forces.

### Warden Henry / McHill Sighting (5 May 1947)

- **Warden Henry** (Montana State Prison, Deer Lodge), **Melbourne McHill** (convict chauffeur), and **Mrs. Henry** driving between Ellensburg and Seattle at ~1530 on 5 May. Saw silver object streaking across sky at "excessively high rate," went into nose dive, then **disintegrated leaving a long pillar of "gas" that remained in form and did not blow away**. Three people saw it over 20-30 miles. Initially thought jet plane, but "motions were erratic."

### Salt Lake City Multiple-Couple Sighting

- Multiple couples from Logan area (Ells, Shoers, Gaylons) reported seeing yellowish-white objects flying at high speed, size of pigeons, at several thousand feet, breaking into groups. Could have been birds but appeared to move faster than birds for about two minutes.

## Open Questions

- The anonymous caller to Tacoma press (July 31 – August 2) had intimate knowledge of Winthrop Hotel room 502 events and knew names of dead officers before Army release. Who was this caller? The FBI never identified them. The caller's statement "All I am interested in is seeing that this information gets back to New Jersey" is particularly intriguing.
- Ray Palmer's "nearly forty years" of disk knowledge and "ample proof" — what did Palmer claim to possess? His August 5 letter hints at deeper knowledge but reveals nothing specific.
- The "pillar of gas" from the May 5 Ellensburg-Seattle sighting that "remained in form and did not blow away" — never explained.
- The Muroc Army Air Field sightings of August 8 involved multiple credible military witnesses observing objects with oscillating/rotational flight patterns and no propulsion noise — dismissed internally without investigation. Why?
- The 4AF Intelligence analysis of "flying saucer specimens" identified them as plaster of paris and old radio parts — were these the same specimens that Dahl/Crisman gave to Brown/Davidson, or a separate set?
- The Guam crescent-shaped objects at Harmon Field (14 August 1947) — what happened to this report? Was it followed up?
- The Danforth, Illinois "instrument" hoax — who placed it there and why? The connection to "Operation Mogul" claims suggests a deliberate attempt to feed the UFO narrative.
- General Schulgen's request to investigate Arnold and Savage for subversive connections — what was the basis for Schulgen's interest in these particular observers?
