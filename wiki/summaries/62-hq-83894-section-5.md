## Key Points

- This section reflects the FBI's hands-off posture: agents now mostly route incoming flying-saucer letters to OSI / Air Force without investigation, but Hoover's office continues to receive personal correspondence and forward it.
- **The Guy Hottel memo (22 March 1950)** — preserved in full. SA Guy Hottel (SAC, Washington) reports that SA R. H. Kurtzman received information from "KARTAHOWE, Special Investigator, Sex Squad, Metropolitan Police Department" (the informant's real identity is obscured in the OCR; the source chain is Police Dept → SA Kurtzman → SA Hottel → FBI HQ). The informant — an Air Force investigator — stated that **three flying saucers had been recovered in New Mexico**: each circular with raised centers, approximately 50 feet in diameter, each occupied by **three 3-foot-tall human-shaped bodies in metallic cloth of fine texture, bandaged in blackout-suit fashion**. The saucers were detected because government high-powered radar in the area interfered with their control mechanisms. This is the famous "three saucers, nine bodies" memo, the most-viewed page in the FBI Vault.
- **Knoxville / Oak Ridge radar tracking (March 1–8, 1950)** — Stuart E. Adcock (operator of radio station WROL, Knoxville) used an Army Surplus APN-7 radar set in his home to detect objects at 40,000–100,000 ft directly over Oak Ridge TN on multiple occasions (March 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6). Multiple agencies responded: AEC Security Division, OSI (Mackell Field, Alabama), CIA (technician dispatched from Washington — arrived but timing unclear), Naval Reserve radar (detected nothing but acknowledged equipment limitations at extreme altitudes), 3rd Army, and USAF NEPA Oak Ridge. Colonel C. D. Gasser (Engineering Officer, USAF Material Command, NEPA) and Captain Robert Cross (radar expert) examined Adcock's equipment and found him "technically incorrect in several matters relating to radar theory." Adcock was found to have "some degree of inebriation" on multiple visits. Despite this, Gasser stated that flight at 100,000 ft was "absolutely possible from a scientific and engineering standpoint" and the matter warranted follow-up. Adcock's reliability was ultimately deemed "extremely dubious" by Gasser after he was unable to be found at his residence on March 5. AEC's procedural assessment noted an "impressive lack of any agency actually taking the responsibility" — the incident was later characterized as a "speaking 'dry run'" with no positive action possible during many hours of elapsed time.
- **U.S. News & World Report article (April 7, 1950)** — pasted into the file in full: "FLYING SAUCERS — THE REAL STORY: U.S. BUILT FIRST ONE IN 1942." Comprehensive article arguing flying saucers are real aircraft conforming to accepted aerodynamic principles. Key claims: first U.S. model designed by Charles H. Zimmerman of NACA in 1942 (elliptical, twin piston engines, twin propellers); Chance-Vought V-173 / XF5U "Flying Flapjack" as the Navy's full-scale development; current saucers use variable-direction jet nozzles around the rim with central control; speed 400–500+ mph; vertical takeoff/landing; hover capability. Cites White Sands Proving Ground ground instruments measuring a saucer at "well over the speed of sound." References Chicago and Southern Airlines pilots (Arkansas, March 1950) and Eastern Airlines DC-3 pilots (Georgia, July 1948). Points to Navy's massive guided-missile spending as the likely developer. Bureau filed without comment, suggesting they took it seriously enough to retain.
- **Peter Cameron Jones letter (August 1947, LA mountains)** — preserved in full via Walter Winchell / Ernest Cuneo correspondence (July 12, 1949). Jones described hiking in LA mountains in August 1947, observing a large silver/greenish metal object shaped like a child's top, balloon-sized, with two windows and transparent-metal portions. He gained the impression of "life within the object." When he waved, the object took off in a second, knocking him to the ground. Cuneo assessed the letter as "very well written, obviously by a man of intelligence." Los Angeles SAC investigated — negative: apartment-court owner had no record of Jones.
- **George Koehler Denver saucer lectures (January–March 1950)** — Koehler (315 Franklin St., Denver) claimed to know a prominent Denver oilman ("STLASAN WION," alias "Mysterious 'X'") and an official of Newton Oil Co. who leased Mojave Desert land where a flying disc was found intact with three 3-foot-tall dead human-like occupants. Koehler claimed to have obtained a radio set as a "souvenir." He had been telling this story off-and-on for three months prior to January 1950, had an article in *True Magazine*, and was visited by Don Shoemaker (author). Denver OSI considered Koehler a "probable mental case." Later surfaced in a Denver University lecture (March 9, 1950) claiming to have seen several saucers, one landing in New Mexico, with three-foot-tall human occupants.
- **Jonathan Caldwell "flying disks discovered in Maryland" (August 1949)** — LA Times article dated August 20, 1949, U.P. release from Washington, August 19. Hoover directed that Caldwell's Manhattan Beach CA address be furnished to OSI.
- **Glen Sprouce yellow object report (September 25, 1949, West Virginia)** — anonymous source described to Hoover a bright canary-yellow rocket-shaped object (15–18 inches long, 4" diameter, sharp needle nose ~6" long, no wings, no visible propulsion) passing under his plane at 3,450 ft over Lubeck WV at ~240 degrees. Resembled fuselage of a Lockheed Air Force X-90. Hoover forwarded to Air Force OSI.
- **Michael Halfery Mars photographs (May 1950, New Orleans)** — Halfery sold two photographs for $1.00 to John R. Esposito, who gave them to CID, New Orleans Port of Embarkation. Photos depicted flying saucers and a man from Mars in custody of two U.S. Army Military Policemen. Intelligence Branch classified source as "usually reliable" and information as "probably true."
- **Lewis A. Ward "Russian drawings" (April 1950, Yuba City CA)** — Ward wrote to the Bureau claiming contact with an individual named "Ubalsky" and referencing "Russian drawings" of flying saucers. San Francisco SAC instructed follow-up to discourage further correspondence and obtain information. Ward was later described as "odd" by his union secretary and "getting along in years and prone to exaggerate on queer imaginings" by his union business rep. FBI interviewer assessed him as "abnormal mentally."
- **Al Hixenbourg Louisville Times saucer film (June 28, 1950)** — Louisville Times staff photographer Al Hixenbourg took 50 feet of 16mm film purporting to show a flying saucer. The object appeared "motionless for about ten seconds" then vanished. The film was carried on a national television network and received national comment from Walter Winchell. Courier-Journal photographers Steinau and Davis debunked it: a friend reported Hixenbourg had previously faked a "flying disk" photo using matches across the camera lens. Steinau noted the "saucer" appeared in sharp detail while the DC-3 plane in the background was out of focus, indicating the disk was closer to the camera — consistent with a projection or optical trick.
- **Alice TX "disc" hoax (July 4, 1950)** — Chief of Police Alice TX reported a small elliptical contraption found in a field near the municipal airport. Upon examination, determined to be constructed by welding a portion of two airplane wings together, freshly painted, with serial number "X-DASH ONE FOUR SEVEN A" and instructions "DO NOT TOUCH." No motor or means of propulsion. Air Force officials at Ellington Field had no information. Local radio reported it as a hoax.
- **Robert Petrone / "Albert Holmberg" Martin aircraft letter (July 25, 1950)** — Midwest Times publisher brought a letter claiming a new flying saucer designed by "Fred Spaunholdt" (former Lincoln skywriter), "Frank Hoochpaw" (aircraft mechanic), and "Carl Teichman" (German WWI ace), financed by Howard Hughes, tested by Glen F. Martin Aircraft Co. Ship named "Danse Macabre," range 4,000 miles, ceiling 25,000 ft, speed 750 mph, one-way bombing craft with no wheels. Midwest Times knew no one named "Albert Holmberg" in their circulation area. Chicago indices negative for Holmberg.
- **Walter D. Jones Canadian sighting (July 19, 1950, Toronto area)** — Farm owner 12 miles northeast of City Hall, Toronto, observed a hazy light object circling his property for 35 minutes (10:30–11:05 pm) on a full moon. Had houseman corroborate. Object neither rose nor fell, changed direction, had no visible shape — only a "filter of light." Hoover forwarded to Air Force. FBI note: Walter D. Jones in 1944 was Treasurer of the National Council for Canadian-Soviet Friendship; RCMP advised he was a "progressive man" not connected to prominent business.
- **DCOMSA (District of Columbia Metropolitan Security Agency?) flying saucer analysis** — A comprehensive multi-page document analyzing the flying-saucer phenomenon, covering: (1) history from Ezekiel through Swedish ghost rockets; (2) five theories (space ships, Soviet missiles, U.S. weapons, natural phenomena, mass hysteria); (3) debunking of ETH based on technical impossibility; (4) debunking of Soviet origin based on lack of evidence; (5) attribution to mass hysteria amplified by press and "name" individuals like Rickenbacker. States 75% of reports related to known causes.
- **Donald Keyhoe book note** — Hoover's office noted they would review Keyhoe's "The Flying Saucers are Real," which contained false references to FBI investigating saucers and alleged FBI agents witnessed saucers at Las Vegas, New Mexico, 12-8-48.
- **Louisville Adous letter (July 3, 1950)** — Hoover's response directing correspondent to Air Force, noting Keyhoe's book contains "no quotations from the FBI but merely mentions this agency."
- **Lewis Ward follow-up (June 15, 1950)** — Ward relocated from Yuba City to San Jose. SAC SF interviewed him; he added nothing to his previous story. Advised that flying-saucer information does not fall within FBI jurisdiction.
- **Stuart Adcock / Oak Ridge procedural analysis** — The AEC's detailed assessment of the March 1950 radar incident is preserved in full, documenting the inter-agency coordination (or lack thereof) between AEC, 3rd Army, USAF, CIA, and Naval Reserve. The document is notable for its procedural critique: "despite the fact that all agencies seemed to be operating in the manner prescribed by agreement... there nevertheless seemed to be an impressive lack of any agency actually taking the responsibility for the situation."

## Open Questions

- The Hottel memo's informant chain — "KARTAHOWE, Special Investigator, Sex Squad" — is an unusual source for such a high-level intelligence claim. Who is Kartahowe? What is the Sex Squad connection to an Air Force investigator's saucer report?
- Colonel C. D. Gasser's dual role — he was both USAF Material Command engineering officer at NEPA/Oak Ridge AND later wrote letters to his commanding officer at Wright Field criticizing the handling of the Adcock incident. Does Gasser appear elsewhere in the corpus?
- The U.S. News & World Report article of April 7, 1950 is unusually detailed for a civilian publication. Was it an authorized leak, genuine engineering analysis, or disinformation? The Bureau's decision to file it without comment suggests it was taken seriously.
- Donald Keyhoe's "The Flying Saucers are Real" contains false claims about FBI investigation — when was this published, and how did the Bureau's review conclude?
- The "Russian drawings" claim by Lewis Ward — was there any substance to this, or was it purely a delusion? The Bureau's instruction to "discourage further correspondence" suggests some caution was warranted.
- The Alice TX disc hoax serial number "X-147A" — does this number appear elsewhere in the corpus as a real or referenced designation?
