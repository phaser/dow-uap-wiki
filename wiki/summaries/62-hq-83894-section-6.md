## Key Points

- The driving narrative of Section 6 is **Project Twinkle**, the Air Force's contracted scientific study of unexplained aerial phenomena (green fireballs and discs) over sensitive installations in New Mexico.
- **Dr. Lincoln LaPaz's 10-point analysis of green fireballs vs. meteors** — preserved in full from his 1948 report and 1950 follow-up. Ten significant differences: (1) horizontal path of green fireballs is "most unusual" (genuine meteors rarely move horizontally); (2) very low height (green fireballs) vs. 40+ miles for genuine meteors; (3) velocity much less than meteoric but greater than V-2 rockets or jets; (4) no noise ever observed (genuine meteors penetrating to that level always accompanied by violent noise); (5) brightness variation differs from meteoric pattern; (6) frequency of sightings peaks at 2030 local time (coincides with neither meteor shower max at 2 AM nor meteorite fall max at 4 PM); (7) the green color is a distinguishing feature; (8) some fireballs exhibit lateral movement not seen in meteors; (9) some penetrate to lower altitudes than typical meteors; (10) some show maneuvers impossible for meteors. Approximately half of recordings are meteoric; the other half (green fireballs + discs) he believes to be **U.S. guided missiles**, but if not, they could be Soviet missiles launched from the Urals (which could reach New Mexico in <15 min at observed speeds).
- **Los Alamos conferences (Feb 17, 1949 and Oct 14, 1949)** — Two multi-agency conferences on green fireball phenomena. Representatives from: Fourth Army, Armed Forces Special Weapons Project, University of New Mexico, FBI, AEC, University of California, USAF Scientific Advisory Board, Geophysical Research Division Air Materiel Command, and OSI. **"A logical explanation was not proffered with respect to the origin of the green fireballs... generally concluded that the phenomenon existed and that they should be studied scientifically until these occurrences have been satisfactorily explained."** Continued occurrence near sensitive installations deemed "cause for concern."
- **Stanfield photograph (Sighting No. 175, Datil NM, Feb 24–25, 1950)** — Photograph taken by Cpl. Lerius B. Stanfield of Holloman AFB. Dr. LaPaz's analysis: angular diameter ~1/4 degree, angular velocity >0.5 degrees/minute. Concluded: not the moon (too small), not Venus or any planet (too large), not a bright star out of focus (motion double diurnal rotation rate). Object was genuinely moving.
- **Os. W. Belton summary memorandum to D. M. Ladd (August 1950)** — lays out the state of play: OSI is "concerned" with continued green fireballs / discs / meteors near sensitive NM installations; ~150 observations recorded since December 1948; phenomena classified into 3 types (green fireballs, discs/round flat objects with white light, meteor-like high-velocity objects); LaPaz analysis; Air Force / Land-Air Inc. (Alamogordo) contract for observation posts at Vaughn NM; 24 May 1950 Land-Air personnel sighted 8–10 objects; 24-hour day-watch established as **"Project Twinkle"**; Dr. Anthony O. Mirarchi (Project Engineer) briefed on FBI espionage/sabotage jurisdiction.
- **Oak Ridge radar detections (Dec 1950 – Jan 1951)** — Multiple detailed reports from the 663rd AC&W Squadron at McGhee Tyson Airport, Knoxville:
  - **18 December 1950, ~08:27 hrs** — NEPA Division employees (Fairchild Engine and Airplane Corp.) riding to work observed bright circular light (brighter than full moon, no spectrum refraction, no exhaust trail), elevation 15–30°, NW azimuth, traveling NW; appeared to diminish in size over ~30 seconds. Independent witnesses: Lt. Col. John R. Hood Jr. (USAF), Cdr. E. W. Hribar (USN), Maj. James L. Steele (USAF), Maj. Walter L. Carss (USAF), civilians James R. Gray and William G. Frey.
  - **19 December 1950, 08:20–08:30 hrs** — Dr. A. J. Miller, Dr. V. P. Calkins, Mr. J. Frank Coneybear, Mr. A. O. Mooneyham, Mr. F. T. Bly observed light in shape of circle, intensity much greater than bright moon, circular appearance with form (not point source), white, no refraction, 15–30° elevated, W to NW azimuth, traveling NW. Circular area darkened from 7–9 o'clock perimeter inward until concentrated at 1–3 o'clock position, similar to large star.
  - **20 December 1950, 12:47 hrs** — 663rd AC&W Squadron radar recorded "very, very slow" small paint over Oak Ridge controlled area; F-82 fighter intercept "perfect intercept and orbit surrounding small smoke cloud."
  - **14 December 1950, 16:05 hrs (3 hours)** — Radar group of targets blanketed scopes over Oak Ridge AEC project; F-82 interception negative; Lt. Robinson photographed radar scope traces with 4×5 Speed Graphic on Plus-X civilian film.
  - **16 January 1951, 21:45 hrs** — Two bright objects east and west of McGhee Tyson Airport. Eastern light identified as star (refraction phenomena). Western light through 20× spotting scope took "many peculiar forms with lines, cores, tails" — Controller Capt. T. C. M. Clevenger said it "generally fits the description of all 'flying saucers' ever described to him." AEC ground observers reported another sighting 12 miles SE of K-25 plant; F-82 vectored south made no contact; later identified as star.
- **LOOK magazine article (January 1951)** — preserved in full. Author: Richard Wilson (LOOK Washington Bureau). The article presents **Dr. Urner Liddel's Skyhook balloon explanation** as the definitive answer. Key claims: flying saucers "were, and are, undeniably real" but they are Skyhook balloons — 100 ft diameter, traveling at speeds up to 200 mph at heights up to 19 miles; carrying instruments to study atomic explosions in the atmosphere; $40 million/year program; "When this project first began, it was kept secret. Now, there is no longer any need for secrecy on a scientific basis." Liddel explains: Captain Mantell and other pilots were pursuing a Skyhook balloon; "squadrons" of saucers are clusters of 20–30 Skyhook 10–15 ft test balloons; 270+ Skyhook flights made; saucers "unheard of until the ONR's experiments in the stratosphere began." Best viewing time: dusk on cool summer evening.
- **Air Force memo (October 1950)** — acknowledges the Liddel-Skyhook explanation but stresses "Subject in its proper perspective as part of the normal USAF requirement for information of air technical intelligence interest."
- **D. M. Ladd memo to Hoover (9 October 1950)** — average 3–4 flying-saucer complaints per month June–September 1950; OSI (Wright Field) handles all investigations; OSI's investigation "fails to indicate that the sightings involved space ships or missiles from any other planet or country." No definite pattern of activity. No increase in sightings as a result of the Korean War.
- **Philadelphia "parachute-disc" case (26 September 1950)** — full report preserved: two Philadelphia police officers on Vare Boulevard near 26th Street observed a round object ~6 ft circumference slowly floating down. Object was lavender-colored, "dewy, sort of like soap suds," evaporating within 15–20 minutes. So light it did not depress weeds when it landed. Officers attempted to pick it up; it broke leaving odorless residue. "Flying Saucer Just Dissolves" — Philadelphia press.
- **Aaron L. Hitchens New Haven sighting (October 20, 1950)** — Chemical Engineer at Winchester Repeating Arms Co. observed sphere-like object with steady golden-orange glow, 10× Venus diameter, 10–20k ft altitude, no sound, no attachments, calculated speed 400–700 mph. Wife and daughter corroborated. Neighbors arrived too late. Described as "a complete circular ball" — very reliable and sincere individual with technical knowledge.
- **Hoover teletype on Frank Scully (October 13, 1950)** — Hoover instructs LA SAC to discreetly determine if Frank Scully, author of "Behind the Flying Saucers," is the same person who has been "actively engaged in communist activities since the late 1930s" in the LA territory. Follow-up teletype on Oct 18 ordered "IMMEDIATELY RESULTS OF YOUR INVESTIGATION."
- **Oak Ridge radar detections (October 12, 15, 16, 1950)** — Three additional radar detections of unidentified objects over Oak Ridge from the 111th CIC Detachment. No explanation found.
- **Walter D. Jones Toronto sighting (August 29, 1950)** — Hoover forwarded Jones's letter (July 19 sighting over Toronto farm) to Air Force OSI. FBI note: Jones was 1944 Treasurer of National Council for Canadian-Soviet Friendship; RCMP advised he was a "progressive man" not connected to prominent business.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **Feb 24–25, 1950, Datil NM** — Stanfield photograph of unknown aerial phenomenon; LaPaz analysis rules out moon, Venus, and stars.
- **Feb 17, 1949 + Oct 14, 1949, Los Alamos** — Multi-agency conferences; "logical explanation not proffered"; phenomenon deemed real and worthy of scientific study.

## Open Questions

- Project Twinkle's ultimate fate: the post-1951 thread is not in this sampled span. Did the program continue, and were its results ever published or declassified?
- The Liddel "Skyhook explains all saucers" position became official ONR / Navy doctrine — yet Air Force OSI continued to investigate. What was the inter-service tension?
- The Oak Ridge radar / interceptor data from Dec 1950 – Jan 1951 imply something physical was being detected (F-82 "orbiting small smoke cloud"). How does the official "all sightings explained as natural phenomena" position (per Ladd Oct 9 memo) reconcile with this?
- Hoover's investigation of Frank Scully's alleged communist activities — what was the result? The follow-up teletype of Oct 18 ordered "IMMEDIATELY RESULTS" but the answer is not in this section.
- Dr. LaPaz's classification of ~50% of New Mexico phenomena as potentially Soviet-guided-missile-launches from the Urals — how did this influence U.S. strategic thinking in 1950?
