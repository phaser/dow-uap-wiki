## Key Points

- The driving narrative of Section 6 is **Project Twinkle**, the Air Force's contracted scientific study of unexplained aerial phenomena (green fireballs and discs) over sensitive installations in New Mexico.
- **Belmont (FBI) summary memorandum to D. M. Ladd (August 1950)** lays out the state of play:
  - OSI is "concerned" with continued green fireballs / discs / meteors near sensitive New Mexico installations.
  - Approximately **150 observations** have been recorded in the vicinity since December 1948.
  - Phenomena classified into 3 types: (1) Green fireballs; (2) Discs / round flat objects emitting brilliant white light; (3) Meteor-like high-velocity objects of varying color.
  - Dr. La Paz analysis (23 May 1950): about half of recordings are of meteoric origin; the other half (green fireballs + discs) he believes to be **U.S. guided missiles**, but if not, they could be Soviet missiles launched from the Urals (which could reach New Mexico in <15 min at observed speeds).
  - **Air Force / Land-Air Inc. (Alamogordo) contract** sets up observation posts at Vaughn, NM, for photographing and triangulating the phenomena. On 24 May 1950 Land-Air personnel sighted 8-10 objects. A 24-hour day-watch is established and designated **"Project Twinkle."**
  - **Dr. Anthony O. Mirarchi**, Project Twinkle's Project Engineer, is briefed by SAC Albuquerque about the FBI's espionage/sabotage jurisdiction.
- **D. M. Ladd memo to Hoover (9 October 1950)**: average 3-4 flying-saucer complaints per month June-September 1950. OSI (Wright Field) handles all investigations. OSI's investigation "fails to indicate that the sightings involved space ships or missiles from any other planet or country." No definite pattern of activity.
- **Philadelphia "parachute-disc" case (26 September 1950)** — the same Vare Blvd / 26th St lavender-mist disc covered in Section 5; reported by SAC Cornelius at length to L. L. Laughlin / FBI HQ. Two officers attempted to pick it up; it broke leaving an odorless residue and disintegrated within 25 minutes. Officer accounts emphasize the object's lightness ("did not bend the weeds"). Reported to OSI Major Watts.
- **Knoxville TN / Oak Ridge multi-witness sightings, December 1950 – January 1951** — 111th CIC Detachment summaries:
  - **18 December 1950, ~05:20-05:30 hrs** — six NEPA Division employees of Fairchild Engine and Airplane Corporation (Drs. A. J. Miller, V. P. Calkins, J. F. Coneybear; Messrs. A. O. Mooneyham, F. T. Bly, with Plant Protection Manager Gene A. Goedjen) and (independently) Lt. Col. John R. Hood, Jr. (USAF), Cdr. E. W. Hribar (USN), Maj. James L. Steele (USAF), Maj. Walter L. Carss (USAF), and civilians James R. Gray and William G. Frey, riding in vehicles within the controlled area observe a **bright circular light** (much brighter than a full moon, no spectrum refraction, no exhaust trail), elevation 15-30°, NW azimuth, traveling NW; appears to diminish in size over ~30 seconds.
  - **20 December 1950, 12:47 hrs** — 663rd AC&W Squadron radar at McGhee Tyson Airport (Knoxville) records a "very, very slow" small paint over Oak Ridge controlled area; F-82 fighter intercept "perfect intercept and orbit surrounding small smoke cloud."
  - **14 December 1950, 16:05 hrs (3 hours)** — radar group of targets blanketed scopes over Oak Ridge AEC project; F-82 interception negative. Lt. Robinson photographs the radar scope traces with a 4×5 Speed Graphic on Plus-X civilian film.
  - **16 January 1951, 21:45 hrs** — two bright objects east and west of McGhee Tyson Airport. The eastern light is later determined to be a star (refraction phenomena); the western light, observed through a 20× spotting scope, takes "many peculiar forms with lines, cores, tails" — the controller, Capt. T. C. M. Clevenger, says it generally fits descriptions of all "flying saucers" he has heard. AEC ground observers report another sighting 12 miles SE of K-25 plant; F-82 vectored south makes no contact; later identified as a star.
- **LOOK magazine article (January 1951)**: pasted in. Author Riley Crabb-style framing aside, the actual report is by **Dr. Urner Liddel**, chief of the Nuclear Physics Branch, Office of Naval Research, who ran the **Skyhook balloon program** (huge plastic helium balloons launched by General Mills from Camp Ripley MN, Holloman AFB / White Sands NM, and Navy aircraft carriers in the Caribbean and Pacific). Liddel concludes the Skyhook balloons "explain virtually all the saucer reports" — including:
  - **Captain Thomas F. Mantell death (7 January 1948, KY)** — a Skyhook balloon descended in spirals from Godman AFB; three F-51s pursued; Mantell died chasing it without supplemental oxygen at altitude.
  - "Squadrons" of saucers (clusters of 20-30 Skyhook 10-15 ft test balloons sometimes used as substitutes for the big bag).
  - At least 270 Skyhook flights had been made by the time of the report.
- **Air Force memo (October 1950)** acknowledges the Liddel-Skyhook explanation but stresses "Subject in its proper perspective as part of the normal USAF requirement for information of air technical intelligence interest."

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **24 May 1950, Vaughn NM** — Land-Air Project Twinkle observers see 8-10 unexplained objects; first systematic instrumented observation effort.
- **18 December 1950, Oak Ridge TN** — NEPA / USAF / USN officers, multiple-vehicle, daylight sighting of bright circular light over the AEC controlled area.
- **20 December 1950, Oak Ridge TN** — radar paint over AEC area, F-82 successful intercept; nothing visible to pilot.
- **14 December 1950, Oak Ridge TN** — multi-target radar event lasting 3 hours over AEC project; F-82 interception negative; scope photographs taken.
- **26 September 1950, Philadelphia** — Vare Blvd / 26th St "lavender-mist" parachute-disc.
- **7 January 1948, Godman AFB KY** — **Mantell incident** referenced in Liddel/LOOK article as a Skyhook balloon misidentification leading to a fatal fighter chase.

## Open Questions

- Did Project Twinkle ever produce a published report or were its results buried? The post-1951 thread is not in this sampled span.
- The Liddel "Skyhook explains all saucers" position became official ONR / Navy doctrine — yet Air Force OSI continued to investigate; what was the inter-service tension?
- The radar / interceptor data from Oak Ridge in Dec 1950 – Jan 1951 imply something physical was being detected; how does the official "all sightings explained as natural phenomena" position (per Ladd Oct 9 memo) reconcile with this?
