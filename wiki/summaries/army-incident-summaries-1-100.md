## Source Metadata

- **Originating agency**: U.S. Army Air Forces / U.S. Air Force, Air Materiel Command (AMC), Wright-Patterson AFB — the case-by-case "Incident Summary" series compiled under Project SIGN (later passed to Project GRUDGE / Project BLUE BOOK). Cases originate at AAF/USAF installations across CONUS plus a small number of FBI / civilian / police reports.
- **Series / identifier**: NARA RG 38, container 143685, Box 7, "Incident Summaries 1-100." Authority block on cover sheet: "NND 917033."
- **Date range of records**: spans the 24 June 1947 wave through at least mid-1948. Cases 1–10 cluster on 4–8 July 1947 (Muroc AAF; Portland, OR police wave); later cases extend into early 1948.
- **Page count**: 209 pages (PDF). Typical case = 1 cover Check-List page + 1–4 narrative / witness-statement pages, so the 100 numbered cases are spread across all 209 pages with sub-letters (e.g., 48a, 48b, 48c, 48d, 50a) for multi-witness incidents.
- **Classification markings observed**: "RESTRICTED" / "CONFIDENTIAL" stamps on most pages; many partly redacted / faded.
- **File size**: ~247 MB; encrypted (print-only).
- **Raw path**: `raw/38_143685_box7_incident_summaries_1-100.pdf`.

## Coverage

`sampled (pages 1-20 and 100-115 of 209)` — too long to read end-to-end. Sampling characterizes the document at the index level: the format is a fixed 26-line "CHECK-LIST – UNIDENTIFIED FLYING OBJECTS" followed by narrative remarks and witness statements, one case per few pages, numbered 1 through ~58 in the sampled range (case 58 begins around page 113–115). Confidence: `medium` — incidents named below are taken from the actual sampled OCR; case numbers between sampled ranges are inferred to follow the same template.

## Key Points

- The collection IS the operational evidence base of Project SIGN: each case is filed on a standardized 26-field Check-List (Date, Time, Location, Observer Name / Occupation / Address, Place of Observation, Number of Objects, Distance, Time in Sight, Altitude, Speed, Direction of Flight, Tactics, Sound, Size, Color, Shape, Odor, Apparent Construction, Exhaust Trails, Weather, Effect on Clouds, Sketches/Photographs, Manner of Disappearance, Remarks). This is the same checklist documented in [[summaries/army-1948-general-vol-1]].
- The early cases (1–10) are dominated by the **8 July 1947 Muroc Army Air Field cluster** — the most famous early military test-center sightings — and the **4 July 1947 Portland, Oregon police wave**.
- Investigators distinguish witness reliability ("appeared calm and normally sensible," "Witness appeared honest in his convictions," "Mrs. Evans appears to be a 'crackpot'") and cross-reference cases ("See also 48, 48a, 48b, 48c, 48d & 33, 33a–33f and Incidents 30 and 32.").
- Many cases are "Confirmed by other sources" — i.e., Project SIGN treats multi-witness corroboration as the primary evaluation criterion.
- Dr. Lincoln La Paz (Director, Institute of Meteoritics, University of New Mexico, Albuquerque) is cited as the meteoritic-explanation expert; Project SIGN routes ambiguous "fireball" cases to him for adjudication (per Norcatur, KS material in [[summaries/army-incident-summaries-101-172]]).
- Object descriptions in the 1947–1948 sample are dominated by silver / aluminum / white discs and spheres at 5–25 ft estimated diameter, ~200–600 mph speeds, altitudes 4 000–30 000 ft, no exhaust trail, no sound, sometimes performing tight circles or 90° instantaneous direction changes.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **Incident 1 — 8 July 1947, 0930, Muroc AAF, California**. 1st Lt. Joseph C. McHenry (Billeting Officer), S/Sgt Gerald E. Newman, T/Sgt Joseph Ruvolo, Miss Jeannette Marie Scotte. 2 silver disc/spherical objects at ~8000 ft, ~300 mph, 320° heading toward Mojave; later a 3rd silver disc seen circling tightly over the airfield. McHenry rejected balloon explanation (objects moved against prevailing wind).
- **Incident 2 — 8 July 1947, ~noon, Muroc AAF**. Major Richard R. Shoop (Tech Engineering Division). Aluminum-colored, "unconventional shape" object 5–8 miles north, oscillating between intermediate altitude and ground level then climbing again, observed for 8 minutes.
- **Incident 3 — 7 July 1947, 1010, Muroc AAF**. Major J.G. Wise (Test Pilot, running up the XP-84 on the ground). Yellowish-white sphere 5–10 ft diameter at 10 000–12 000 ft, 200–225 mph, west-to-east, oscillating in a "forward whirling movement" without losing altitude.
- **Incident 4 (also "the Strapp sighting") — 8 July 1947, 1150, Rogers Dry Lake (Area #3), Muroc, California**. Captain John Paul Strapp (Flight Test). Silvery ovular object ~50 ft, descending against prevailing wind during a planned ejection-seat experiment with two P-82s and an A-26 at 80 000 ft. Strapp recorded "two projections on the upper surface" suggesting rotation/oscillation. Witness: Mr. Lens (civilian, Wright Field, Dayton, OH).
- **Incident 5 — 4 July 1947, 1305, Portland, Oregon, police HQ**. Patrolman Kenneth A. McDowell, Portland City Police. 5 disc-shaped objects east of Portland — 2 flying south, 3 east — oscillating up/down at great speed.
- **Incidents 5a / 6 — 4 July 1947, 1305, Portland and Milwaukie, Oregon**. Patrolman Earl E. Patterson (former Air Corps pilot); Sgt. Clarence Cross (Oregon State Police, District #1, Milwaukie); Patrolman W.A. Lissy (Portland PD, also a private pilot). Multiple aluminum/whitish-brown discs at high speed, including a single disc that "performed 90° angle without difficulty," interpreted by the witness as radio-controlled.
- **Incident 8 — 29 July 1947, 1450, Hamilton Field, California**. Capt. William H. Ryherd (Asst. Operations Officer, USAF) and Lt. Ward Stewart. Two white shiny circular objects 15–25 ft diameter, very fast above a P-80, flying south toward Oakland.
- **Incident 48 et seq. (48, 48a, 48b, 48c, 48d) — 7 January 1948, 1920, Clinton County AAF, Wilmington, Ohio**. Cpl. James H. Hudson (Control Tower), S/Sgt. John P. Haag, Mr. Ralph L. Stirr. Triangle/cone object SW of field, 15 000–20 000 ft altitude, color cycling white→red→green, surrounded by "burning gas," green exhaust mist, ascending and descending vertically without forward motion, 35 minutes total in sight, then off SW at high speed at ~1955 EST. Cross-referenced with Incidents 30, 32 and 33 (multi-state event the same evening — this is the **same evening as the Mantell incident**, see Incidents 30/32/33 — page sample suggests these are the Fort Knox / Godman AFB cluster).
- **Incident 49 — 9 January 1948, Danville, Kentucky**. Mr. & Mrs. Markham. Pencil-shaped high-altitude object trailing smoke that broke into a "beautiful fireworks display of sparks," reported via WHIO News Broadcast 2300–2315.
- **Incident 50 / 50a — 10 January 1948, 22:00 EST, Wildwood, New Jersey**. Mrs. Ethel Evans and Mrs. Krueger, 401 Pine Ave. Yellow/red/amber tear-drop / ovular object that split into two pieces and fell into the ocean. Investigator note: "Individual appears to be 'crackpot'. No one (with the exception of Mrs. Krueger) seems to have seen the many sightings mentioned." Mrs. Evans wrote to President Truman about repeated sightings.
- **Incident 51 — 3 September 1947, 1215, Oswego, Oregon**. Mrs. Raymond Dupui. 12–15 round silver objects at high altitude.
- **Incident 58 — Hamilton Field-area, ~1948** (sampled but partially OCR-corrupt) — military pilot witness, 6 000 ft, 120° heading, milk-white, behaving "similar to fighter a/c when accompanying heavier ships."

## Open Questions

- The Clinton County AAF cluster of 7 January 1948 (Incidents 30, 32, 33, 33a–33f, 48, 48a–48d) is the same evening as the Mantell incident at Godman AFB / Fort Knox; pages 100–105 cross-reference this set but the "core" Mantell pages (the 33-series) were not in the sampled ranges. Need a focused re-read of pages covering Incidents 30–33 to confirm the Mantell crash is here.
- Several investigator dispositions cite "Confirmed by other sources" without naming the corroborating sources — what is the corroboration mechanism? Probably radar plots or independent civilian reports, but file structure not visible in sample.
- Witness "Mrs. Evans / Mrs. Krueger" (Incident 50) appears to be a long-running "repeat reporter." Are similar repeat-reporter cases statistically clustered? Worth a synthesis.
- Page 1 has only an "Authority NND 917033" stamp and a barcode — what is the parent NARA finding aid for this RG 38 / Box 7 series?
- The case-numbering scheme is **not strictly chronological** (Incident 51 is 3 Sep 1947, after Incidents 48–50 which are January 1948), suggesting cases were assigned numbers in the order they reached AMC, not date-of-event. Confirm this hypothesis with pages outside the sample.
