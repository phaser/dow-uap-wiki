## Key Points

- Case-numbering is continuous with the prior volume (1–100); the same 26-field UNIDENTIFIED FLYING OBJECTS Check-List is used, prefilled by AMC investigators.
- This volume opens with the **Norcatur, Kansas fireball/explosion of 18 February 1948 (Incident 101)** — a multi-state event seen across Oklahoma, New Mexico, Colorado, Kansas, and Nebraska, with a substantial paper trail: amateur photograph (Duane W. Wray, Norton, KS), recovery of an "achondrite" stony meteorite (~109 lb piece, plus smaller fragments) by the Institute of Meteoritics, multiple ground-witness statements (Leland Sammons, Kenneth Hays, Ralph New, Edgar Young's son, Mrs. Hays).
- **Important methodological wrinkle**: a Pueblo, CO civilian (Norman G. Markham, "323 W. Tenth St., Pueblo, Colorado," writing 20 February 1948 and again 1 March 1948 to the Chief, Signal Officer, USA, Washington) submitted a long speculative letter arguing that the Norcatur explosion was a **rocket / spacecraft inbound from the Moon**, basing the speculation on geometric alignment with the Moon's orbit at "100 hours" (the rocketry-theory Earth–Moon transit time per Willy Ley's *Rockets*, p.192). Markham's letter cross-references the **Boise object of 24 June 1947** (Lt. Gov. Whitehead and Chief Justice Lampert), Earl Holle's "Ship in Flames" off Fort Ross, CA (3 July 1947), the Cape Mendocino Navy blimp incident (1 July 1947), the Oakland object (13 October 1947), the Ticonderoga (tanker) "flying discs" sighting (12 November 1947), the Phantom Reef ASW echo off San Francisco (24 November 1947), the **Mantell incident** ("Lt. Mantell" pursuing a flying disc on 7 January 1948 over Fort Knox / Franklin / Nashville, killed gaining altitude), and the **Maury Island Puget Sound incident** (June 1947, two military officers killed bringing back material evidence).
- Dr. Lincoln La Paz (Director, Institute of Meteoritics, University of New Mexico) responds (letter 11 April 1948) on Markham: dismisses the moon-rocket theory but concedes the Norcatur and Four Corners falls have "many curious aspects" (no fragments recovered despite intensive search; battery-case fall in Norton, KS street; Sammons' very-near-fall testimony). La Paz: "either the earth is under a most unusual cosmic bombardment or many of the fireballs are not meteorites at all."
- Witness Leland Sammons (farmer, 1.5 mi W of Stockton, KS) gives a near-encounter testimony: object "wobbled" 5 ft from his face at face level, "fire belching out of it and sucking back in," about 4 ft long, funnel-shaped, with a pipe sticking out the back, before lighting "as if a fuse might have lighted" and taking off NW.
- Incident 102 (Green River, UT, 18 February 1948, 1500 MST): two B-29s of the 718th Bomb Squadron, 28th Bomb Group (VH), Weaver AFB, SD, observed a huge multi-colored fireball trailing dense smoke at ~20 000 ft, ~100 mi SE, heading SE of Limon, CO. Witnesses: 1st Lt. Howard H. Berodt (AO-49504), Capt. Maurice T. Ritenour (AO-50443), 1st Lt. Leonard P. Marchese (AO-748714), 1st Lt. Carl W. Stueki (co-pilot). This appears to be the same fireball as Incident 101 — Project SIGN treated the airborne and ground reports as separate cases with cross-referencing.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **Incident 101 — 18 February 1948, ~1650 CST (1750 EST), Norcatur, Kansas (multi-state visibility)**. Bluish-white smoke smudge, mushroom shape, single explosion followed by smaller explosions. Witnesses: M.R. Stenbeil (Editor, Norton, KS) (newspaper account), Leland Sammons (farmer), Kenneth Hays (5 mi S of Prairie View), Mrs. Hays, Ralph New (Postmaster, Norcatur), Edgar Young's son (Reager, KS), and the Norton airport tower (~20 000 ft estimated). Photograph by Duane W. Wray of Norton (4 minutes after the explosion). Object identified by Oscar Monnig (Texas Observers, amateur astronomy, 1010 Morningside Drive, Fort Worth) as a meteor with recovered "achondrite" fragments up to 109 lb. La Paz interpretation: meteorite. Markham interpretation: returning Moon-rocket. Investigator note: large fragment was hit two feet deep in soil in a clover field.
- **Incident 102 — 18 February 1948, 1500 MST, near Green River, Utah (39°N 100°W)**. Same event observed from the air. Two B-29 aircraft of the 28th Bomb Group (VH) crew. Multi-colored fireball with dense smoke trail, "huge," "very high speed," at estimated 20 000 ft, heading SE of Limon, CO. Sound not perceptible due to B-29 engine noise.
- **Cross-referenced incidents in Markham letter** (not necessarily in this folder): Boise object 24 June 1947 (Whitehead/Lampert); Holle "Ship in Flames" 3 July 1947 (Sonoma County, CA, near Fort Ross); Cape Mendocino blimp accident 1 July 1947; Oakland "Saturn-like" object 13 October 1947 (Ben Dobus, A.J. Goldman); Ticonderoga tanker discs 12 November 1947 (3°15′N 124°5′W, 0620 GCT); Phantom Reef episode 24 November 1947 (USS Maury survey, ~400 mi west of San Francisco); Mantell pursuit 7 January 1948 (Wilmington OH / Fort Knox KY / Franklin KY / Nashville TN); Maury Island Puget Sound incident June 1947.

## Open Questions

- This volume contains a fascinating early civilian extraterrestrial-hypothesis letter (Markham, March 1948) preserved in U.S. Army Signal Corps files. Did Markham receive any official response, and was his speculation logged anywhere else?
- La Paz claims "no fragments recovered" from the Four Corners fireball but says ~109 lb of achondrite was recovered from Norcatur — but the SHC/Markham/Sammons material strongly disputes the meteorite explanation. Resolution: was the recovered "meteorite" ever scientifically published?
- Cases 103–172 were not sampled — they are presumed to span late February 1948 through end of 1948 / early 1949 based on the case-numbering trajectory. Worth a re-sample.
- The Mantell incident reference inside the Markham letter (page 11) implies the **core Mantell file** may be in Incidents 30–33 (in the 1–100 volume) rather than here. Confirm.
- The Markham reference to "two military officers who were said to have been bringing back material evidence from 'flying discs' one of which was said to have met with some kind of accident over Maury Island, Puget Sound, around June, 1947" is a contemporary encoding of the Maury Island / Kenneth Arnold / Capt. Davidson & Lt. Brown B-25 crash narrative. Worth its own entity page.
