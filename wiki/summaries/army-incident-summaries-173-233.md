## Source Metadata

- **Originating agency**: U.S. Air Force, Air Materiel Command (AMC), Wright-Patterson AFB — Project SIGN incident-summary series, third volume continuing [[summaries/army-incident-summaries-101-172]].
- **Series / identifier**: NARA RG 38, container 143685, Box 7, "Incident Summaries 173-233." Authority block visible on covers: "B SL S 02576 73-9-9 / Summary Sheets 4."
- **Date range of records**: cases sampled span **August–December 1948**. Earliest dated event in the volume is **Incident 187 (19 August 1948 — Godman AFB Venus tracking)**; latest dated material runs into mid-December 1948 (Incident 227 covers a meteor-typing analysis "Interval Dec 5-13"; Incident 230 cross-references the Perseid shower). The volume therefore spans the operational transition from **Project SIGN → Project GRUDGE** (formal renaming February 1949) and captures the increasing analytical sophistication that came with that transition.
- **Page count**: 144 pages (PDF).
- **Classification markings observed**: cover sheets stamped (OCR distorted) — mostly "RESTRICTED"; a handful Confidential.

## Key Points

The volume documents the **first explicit non-CONUS case load** in the Project SIGN incident corpus, the **continuing institutional vetting framework** for witnesses, the **emergence of analytical (rather than purely descriptive) case-handling** — Venus and Perseid identifications, comet correlations, weather-balloon and cosmic-ray-balloon adjudications — and contains the **November 1948 Andrews AFB pursuit case** that is one of the most operationally substantive military-pilot UFO encounters in the early-program record.

### Form redesign confirmed

The 25-line "INCIDENT SUMMARY" template (vs. the original 26-line CHECK-LIST in vols. 1–172) is consistently used across this volume. Witness occupation/hobbies, peculiarities, and luminosity-character fields signal the move to a more analytically-conscious investigation method.

### Geographic expansion — the volume's core change

For the first time the corpus carries a substantial **non-CONUS** case load:

- **Pacific / East Asia**: Hawaii (Incident 178 — Maj. Drum); off Iruka Shima Japan (Incident 183 — F-61 night radar+visual, two unidentified aircraft); Wakkanai Japan radar site (Incident 198 — 65-min "Ferret"-classed circling target); Clark AF Base Philippines (Incidents 206 — Sgt. Wright sky-writing observation; 207-3 — two fighter-pilot corkscrew-vapor case at 30,000 ft); Korea (Incident 209 — 14 Nov 1948, twin-engine unknown bomber circling Army Reserve Posts at 700 ft over coordinates 37°N 125°E).
- **Atlantic Canada**: AAF Goose Bay, Labrador (Incident 188 — 29 Oct 1948, GCA-radar slow-target sequence, Hamilton River + Lake Melville plotted positions).
- **Europe**: Neubiberg AFB Germany (Incident 190 — 11 Oct 1948, six 86th Fighter Wing personnel observe round high-altitude object 45 mins; Incident 193 — 6 Oct 1948 multiple base-housing observers).

### Substantive single-case findings

- **Incident 187, 19 Aug 1948, Godman AFB Kentucky** — the **Godman AFB / Venus case**: Lt Col **John Waugh** notified Wright-Patterson Flight Service Center; spherical bright silver object 30,000-40,000 ft, theodolite-tracked from the ground continuously; F-51 from Standiford AFB couldn't locate. Definitively identified as the planet **Venus** by Mr. Moore, Head Astrologer, University of Louisville, KY, with MCI verification later that day. *This is the same airfield where the [[entities/mantell-incident|Mantell case]] occurred 7 January 1948* — i.e., Godman had a documented Venus-tracking event seven months after Mantell. The proximity is significant for the long-running Mantell-was-Venus debate; the case shows the AF was on the ground tracking Venus at Godman.
- **Incident 194, 3 Nov 1948** — pursuit of unidentified high-altitude object over Salem area by **two 52d Fighter Wing P-51 Mustangs** climbing to 37,000 ft; object ~5,000 ft above them, "intestine-shaped" (5-6× longer than wide), ~2 mph. Initially evaluated by Strategic Air Command as "heavenly body"; subsequently identified by **Lt Roherkemper, Coast Guard Officer, Salem** as a **cosmic-ray equipment carrier**: a cluster of eight balloons used by **MIT for cosmic-ray research**. Closed.
- **Incident 197 / 198, late Oct – early Nov 1948** — references the **Comet 1948 V (Eclipse Comet)** discovered by **Dr. Harley Wood, Sydney Observatory**, "tail extending for 15 million miles", visible naked-eye in southern and western US for ~6 minutes before sun obliterated it. Cross-referenced to a *Look* magazine article 22 Nov 1948 about an "object" sighting on Nov 6. Note appended: *"to be submitted to Dr. Hynek for his viewpoint as to whether the object reported and the comet mentioned in this article are one and the same."* — first explicit reference in this corpus to **J. Allen Hynek** as the program's astronomical consultant.
- **Incident 198, 6 Nov 1948 0812Z, Wakkanai Japan** — radar site target circled the station for **1 hr 5 min** at speeds varying from 160 to 240 mph, in a 2-mile-radius pattern, sometimes appearing as 2 dog-fighting aircraft. **Evaluated as a Soviet "Ferret" electronic-reconnaissance mission** — an early Cold War evaluation that would become a recurring program-disposition for radar-track-only cases over Japan and Korea. Significant context: Wakkanai sits on Hokkaido's northern tip, ~40 km from the Soviet-held Sakhalin coast.
- **Incident 199, 30 Oct 1948 1430 PST, 15 mi NW of Grays Harbor, WA** — Major Morris (pilot) and Lt. Kunsmen, 64th All-Weather Fighter Squadron / 325th Fighter Group, in F-82 at 8,000 ft observed a group of 10–20 small "egg-shaped" translucent objects moving westerly then northerly for ~20 sec, "no apparent change of altitude — not trying to attack or to escape," yellow then white, eventually "seemed to dissolve". Notable for the formation count and the explicit translucency.
- **Incidents 200 / 200a / 200b / 200c, 17 Oct 1948 1610 PST, Crescent City CA** — **mass-witness sighting** with four independent observers (Major W. Allen, barber; Andrew N. Siler, fisherman; Ernest Haley, dry cleaner; Mrs. Elise Henggi, housewife) all at separate vantage points reporting the same silver, egg/bullet-shaped object banking sharply at ~10,000 ft estimated altitude. Each filed independently. Investigator vetted each witness's character; no military or commercial flights matched.
- **Incidents 201 / 201A / 201B, 31 Oct 1948 0230Z, AAFS APO 406, Newark area** — Staff Sgt. Joseph J. Barnett observed flat round yellow-orange object overhead for 3-5 minutes; PFC Robert R. Anderson confirmed. Investigator A-2 evaluated as **weather balloon with light**.
- **Incident 203, 8 Nov 1948 1850, Newark AF Base NJ** — Edmund J. Cisek observed pale-luminous round disk, ~moon-diameter, traveling NNW → SSE in arc, ~800 mph estimated (roughly 200 mph faster than jet aircraft tactic-approaches he was familiar with). 75% of arc visible from his vantage at Hangar #7.
- **Incident 207 / 207A, 18 Nov 1948 2145–2230, circling Andrews AFB / Camp Springs MD** — the **Andrews AFB military pursuit case**. **2nd Lt Kenwood W. Jackson, USAFR** (telephone cableman, 1326 28th St SE, Washington DC) and corroborating witness **2nd Lt Glen L. Stalker, USAAF** observed the object first; **2nd Lt Henry G. Combs, USAFR** (Capitol St, Washington DC) pursued it in his aircraft at 80–600 mph for 10–12 minutes. Combs reported the object: "oblong ball" with one continuous glowing white light, no wings, no exhaust, ~smaller than a T-6, capable of **vertical climbs from 1,700 to 7,500 ft** and **turns inside Combs's flap-down 360° climbing turn**. He used his landing light on it: object responded with "a very dull gray glow." Eventually departed eastward at 500–600 mph; Combs lost contact at 8,000 ft. Combs's witness statement is preserved verbatim. **This is one of the most operationally substantive single-pilot UFO pursuit reports in the entire 173–233 volume.**
- **Incident 207-3, 17 Nov 1948 1430, Clark AFB Philippines** — two 1st Fighter Squadron fighter pilots (names heavily OCR-degraded; one is "Cap **TEt**" possibly Capt. *Tate*, the other Capt. **R. S. ***) observed "burst of flak sitting still in the atmosphere connected to a curved trail of corkscrew appearance" at 30,000 ft, ~3 minutes duration. Object/trail dissipated like smoke. **Two military pilots, broad-daylight sky** — the corkscrew geometry is unusual.
- **Incident 209, 14 Nov 1948, Korea (37°57'N 125°31'E and adjacent posts)** — unidentified twin-engine bomber circled four Army Reserve Observation Posts at 700 ft, sighted independently from posts #1, #2, #3, #5. Pre-Korean War period; subsequently a recurring pattern of unidentified aircraft over the Korean coast.
- **Incident 212, 3 Dec 1948 1715 EST, Dayton OH** (near Wright-Patterson) — George E. Hoffman and Roy B. Statzer near Stillwater Bridge / Siebenthaler Ave: two 1/2-second pulses of light in NW sky, ~10–20,000 ft, no exhaust evident. *Observed within ~10 mi of Wright-Patterson AFB itself*.
- **Incidents 220 / 225 / 226 / 229 / 230, mid-to-late December 1948** — increasing density of meteor / astronomical attributions. Incident 227 ("Interval Dec 5-13 & Typical Meteors") and Incident 230 ("of the Perseid shower") indicate the program had begun **systematic meteor-shower correlation** by late 1948 — a direct precursor to the kind of analysis that Hynek's later work formalized.

### Witness vetting continues

The investigators continued the practice from earlier volumes of explicitly evaluating witness reliability — character references, cross-checks of distance estimates, verification of professional standing. Examples in this volume:

- *Incident 186* — investigator caught Mr. Fred Nash overestimating tree height ("over 100 ft" was actually ~40 ft), tempering confidence in his "300 mph at 500 ft" estimate.
- *Incident 187* — Lt Col Waugh's tracking-tower-trained observation team given high reliability; their case nonetheless resolved as Venus.
- *Incident 189* — Lt Rubisch's command officer interviewed; "Lt Rubisch is inclined to *not* exaggerate and is highly regarded".
- *Incident 200/200a/200b/200c* — all four Crescent City witnesses character-vetted independently; agreement between four reports despite different vantage points reinforces case substance.
- *Incident 206* — investigator notes "Sgt Wright's character and his honesty are questionable according to the interrogating officer" — case downgraded.
- *Incident 207A* — Combs's pursuit narrative includes precise altitude/speed bracketings, indicating his account was treated as substantive (it was preserved verbatim).

## Open Questions

- The Andrews AFB pursuit case (Incident 207A, Lt. Combs, 18 Nov 1948) has the structure of a major military encounter but does not appear in the standard early-program canon (Mantell, Chiles-Whitted, Gorman, etc.). Was Combs's report ever escalated to the **Project SIGN** *Estimate of the Situation* package, or did the program's late-1948 ETH-skepticism turn cause it to be downgraded?
- The **Wakkanai 1 hr 5 min radar track** (Incident 198) classed as Soviet Ferret is one of the earliest unambiguous "UFO → Soviet ELINT" reattributions in the corpus. Were subsequent Wakkanai-area radar tracks systematically reclassified the same way? (See also the [[summaries/army-intelligence-collection-1948-1955]] USAFE TT-1524 series for a parallel European analytical pattern.)
- The **Korea Incident 209 (14 Nov 1948)** unidentified twin-engine bomber circling four observation posts at 700 ft is a notable pre-Korean-War-period datum. Cross-checking against KMAG / Far East Command intelligence records of the same period would clarify whether this was North Korean, Soviet, or genuinely unidentified.
- **Hynek's first appearance** as named astronomical consultant is in the Incident 197/198 comet-cross-reference note. The wider 173-233 volume's astronomical attributions (Venus 187, comet 197, Perseids 230, "typical meteors" 227) appear consistent with a single consultant-driven analytical approach — was this the Hynek apparatus, or someone else's?
- The **Godman AFB Venus case (Incident 187)** seven months after Mantell suggests Godman's own observation infrastructure was tracking Venus contemporaneously with the period. Worth integrating into the Mantell-Venus-or-Skyhook synthesis.
- Several incidents (179, 180, 191, 192, 195, 202, 208, 210, 213-219, 221, 222, 224, 228, 231-233) were not enumerated in the OCR readout above; the volume probably contains them but they are interleaved with carbons / cross-references and need a structured second-pass index. Case 207-3 vs. 207A naming suggests internal renumbering went on during the volume's compilation.
