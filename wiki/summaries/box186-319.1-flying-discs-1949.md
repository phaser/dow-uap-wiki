## Key Points

### Regulation 200-4 reporting infrastructure

- This is **the operational regulation-200-4 file** — the standardized USAF sighting-report channel post-Mantell that funneled all CONUS UFO reports through regional Flight Service Centers (Lowry, McChord, Olmsted, Maxwell, Hamilton, etc.) to AMC at Wright-Patterson. The reports are by-the-book and follow a much more rigorous template than the earlier Project SIGN incident summaries.
- This is **chronologically the Project GRUDGE era** (GRUDGE replaced SIGN in February 1949). The reporting infrastructure is mature: dedicated regulation, standardized form, multiple channels.
- The reports are **dominated by post-Mantell flightline witnesses** — controllers, tower operators, ARTC controllers, AACS air/ground operators — rather than the largely civilian/police witnesses of 1947. This represents a professionalization of UFO observation.
- Standard close-out for each report: weather data at the time, winds-aloft data, local flight schedules of commercial / private / military aircraft, and Ordnance / Navy / Air Force / Army / Weather / Research / AAA activity in the area, plus a "no opinion / unknown" reliability rating on the observer.
- **McChord FSC** is the dominant reporting center in this file, forwarding reports from the Pacific Northwest (Spokane, Seattle, Medford, Portland, Mountain Home).
- **Olmsted FSC** (Middletown PA) forwards reports from the Northeast (Bedford MA → Griffiss AFB Rome NY).
- **Hamilton FSC** forwards reports from the Midwest (Fairfield OH, Boston area).

### Notable Incidents / Cases

#### Spokane, Washington — 26 July 1949, 2020 PST

- **Bill Miller**, civilian commercial pilot, 411 Carahan, Spokane, saw **eight (8) dish-shaped objects, each "larger than a B-29,"** in formation east-to-west, ~14,000 ft altitude, "much faster than a B-29," sound similar to a formation of jet aircraft, accompanying a B-29 (AF-7742) at 13,500 ft. The B-29 pilot reported **nothing unusual**. Miller was not drinking, had good vision. Reported via Spokane Radio → Seattle ARTC → McChord FSC.

#### Medford, Oregon — 8 August 1949, 1120P–1230P

- **Multiple AACS Air/Ground operators** of Medford Airways: **Cpl. E.H. Conger**, **S/Sgt Arthur Paulson**, **Cpl. Frank Bishop** (all AACS Detachment 1905-1, Medford). Also **G.E. Milligan** and **Jack Edmonds** (CAA Tower operators, Medford Airport). **W.D. White** and **Earl Wescott** (Communicators, CA Range Station, Medford).
- Objects east and southeast of Medford. **Shiny** objects, speed **varying from slow to very fast**, heading undetermined. Objects **did not hold any specific heading**, last seen heading northeast. Objects **maneuvered horizontally but not vertically**.
- Personnel from Medford Airways first sighted objects, notified Medford Radio. Personnel from Medford Radio used binoculars and were **able to distinguish wings on the objects** but no further identification could be detected. Objects would **fly in formation, then break off and reform in a trail formation**, disappear for short periods and reappear in the same general areas. Objects were **visible to the unaided eye when they reflected the sun's rays**.

#### Portland / Troutdale / Mount Hood, Oregon — 30 July 1949, ~2100P

- **Captain Thrush**, pilot, Northwest Airlines, approaching Portland from the west, saw an aircraft displaying lights — one white light. Object was at 21100 ft at an altitude of approximately 5 miles east of Stevenson, WA. Object was behind him and slightly to the left, maintained that position some time over Troutdale. Captain Thrush heard Portland Tower giving instructions to other aircraft to **hold over the airport because of an unidentified flying object dropping flares**. Thrush turned 180° and attempted to intercept the object displaying two red lights and one white light. Object then turned southeast and **pulled away from him quite easily**. His indicated airspeed was 210 mph while trying to intercept.
- **Mr. Penhallegan**, civilian instructor pilot, Western Skyways, Troutdale, saw the object while in the traffic pattern at Troutdale, flying at 1,300 ft indicated. Object appeared 500-800 ft above him at an estimated distance of 20 miles to the east.
- **B-29 AF-7758** at 7,500 ft northbound over Portland at 2130P — pilot questioned and stated he **did not drop any flares, did not circle over Portland, and did not see anything unusual**.

#### Mountain Home, Idaho — 24 July 1949, 1205 MST

- **Harry Clark**, Airport Manager of Ritchie Field, Nampa, Idaho, saw **seven (7) delta-wing objects** flying in a **V formation**, 10 miles northwest of Mountain Home.
- Objects were **larger than an F-51**, **darker than normal aluminum skin and not shiny**. Surface between light gray and dirty white with no markings or diffusion of colors. Speed **faster than an F-51**, estimated at least 600 mph.
- Formation was heading ~300°, passed by Clark and then made a **one hundred and eighty (180) degree turn** to ~120°. No banking during the turns. **Outer wing surfaces appeared to move slightly** when first sighted.
- **Dark circular bulge** noted on the bottom side of the objects (where the pilot normally sits) — leading edge of this dark circular structure was approximately 1/4 the distance from the apex of the delta wing to the rear.
- Altitude between 8,500 and 10,000 ft. **No sound was noted. No exhaust trail was visible.**
- Clark was enroute from Burley, Idaho to Nampa, Idaho, in a Cub Cruiser at 10,000 ft. When formation made the 180° turn, Clark descended to 8,500 ft and made a 90° left turn in an effort to intercept. **Captain John S. Batie** vouches for Clark's reliability.

#### Fairfield, Ohio — 26 June 1949, 1750H–1755H

- **Major B. L. Long**, Wright-Patterson AFB, and an **unidentified civilian** (resident of Fairfield, Ohio) saw a **long tail of flame accompanied by a long tail of smoke**, very high altitude, high speed.

#### Boston area — 10 June 1949

- **1st Lt. Kirshbaum**, Fort Devens, Massachusetts, saw a **tubular white object, 100 feet long** at 30,000 ft, 20 miles southwest of Boston, heading 330°–340°, speed 150 mph. No exhaust, no sound.

#### Seattle, Washington (over the city) — 22 August 1949, 1845P

- Three USAF NCO witnesses: **Sgt. Jack Faulkner**, **S/Sgt T.D. Mullen**, **Sgt. Roger H. Studeman** — all controllers of the **143rd National Guard Aircraft Control and Warning Squadron** (6736 Ellis Avenue, Seattle). Single circular object ~75–100 ft diameter, 10–15 ft thick, **shiny aluminum**, north-to-south, ~500–600 mph, ~10,000 ft, **jet-like sound, no exhaust trail**. Reported through Boeing Field CAA tower → McChord FSC. Both Sgt. Faulkner and Sgt. Studeman saw other aircraft in the air at the same time and reported that the circular object **"bore no resemblance to any aircraft they had ever seen before."**

#### Northwest of Seattle-Tacoma Airport (~30 mi WNW) — 22 August 1949, 1920P

- **ARTC Controllers Ben Frieman** and **Howard Watson** at Seattle ARTCC. **F-84-shaped, F-84-sized, F-84-colored** object — "displayed visual characteristics of a jet fighter" — south-to-north, 4,000–5,000 ft, **no sound, no exhaust trail**. McChord verified no jet flight plans were active in the area; all base inquiries returned negative.

#### In-flight, AF 7110, Bedford MA → Griffiss AFB Rome NY — ~22 September 1949

- Pilot: **Lt. Col. Phillip J. Kuhl**, Air Materiel Command, AF Procurement Field Office, Bedford, MA, accompanied by **Major Earl Goodrich** (co-pilot). **Cylindrical silvery/aluminum object** ~30 mi off the right rear at 4 o'clock, descending from 20,000 ft into overcast at 7,000 ft, with **flame more than twice the length of the object**, appearing rocket- or jet-propelled, at high speed. Coordinates of sighted object ~**43°40′N 74°55′W** (Adirondacks region of upstate New York). Winds aloft: 280° / 40 kts.

#### Kansas City, Kansas / Olathe, Kansas — 6 January 1950, night

- **James F. Grey** (6200 Hadley St., Raytown, MO; pilot, employed Bendix Aviation Kansas City) and **Robert Van De Vyvere** (5532 Raytown Rd., MO; Bendix Aviation). **Two spherical brilliant-white objects** emanating orange/red flashes, sized like old-fashioned street lights at two-block range, moved from Kansas City to Olathe, **hovered motionless 10–15 minutes over Olathe**, then departed SW at high speed at 7,000–8,000 ft altitude, no sound, no exhaust. Reported via Fairfax AFB Operations → Lowry FSC. Signed **Lt. Col. Robert M.** (surname OCR-corrupt).

#### Greenville, South Carolina — 15 August 1949, 1930E

- **Mr. Harper** (employee, Greenville Municipal Airport, Lansing Avenue) and one unidentified witness. Round, fairly large, gray object moving NE at undetermined constant speed/altitude, no sound, no trail. **Operations section of the 316th Troop Carrier Wing investigated and determined the object was a Geological Weather Service balloon released in Tennessee.** This is a documented "explained" case in the 1949 file.

#### RB-29 test hop — 2 May 1949

- Radar operator from the **514th Reconnaissance Squadron (VER) Weather** on aircraft **RF-21824** noticed **interference on the radar scope** that "came in in the same form as a regular range marker except that the pulses were approximately ten (10) miles apart." The operator **"believes that what was picked up on this flight was intentional jamming."** Operator was one of the squadron's most experienced radar operators; checked the set after landing and found no malfunctions.

#### RB-29 weather reconnaissance — 5 June 1949, position 12°25′N 140°20′E

- **1st Lt. Ernest Swanson** (weather observer) and **M/Sgt Walter Rogers** (engineer), 514th Reconnaissance Squadron, sighted a **white billowing substance** at altitude of 30,000–35,000 ft while flying a heading of 80° at 18,500 ft on a routine Vulture Baker weather reconnaissance mission. What appeared to be a **traveling contrail**.

#### Kodiak, Alaska — 8 April 1949

- Intelligence Officer observed a **celestial manifestation crossing the sky above the U.S. Naval Operating Base, Kodiak, Alaska, 8 April 1949 at approximately 2040 hours**. A **strange bright blue object streaked across the sky**. A **bus driver** reported seeing the same object at 2045 hours. Weather for 8 April 1949 at 2000 hours documented. This appears to be a "sighting" that was likely a meteor or atmospheric phenomenon.

### Air Weather Wing semi-monthly summary (16 June 1949)

- **214th Air Weather Wing** (APO 925) submitted a semi-monthly intelligence summary for the period ending 15 June 1949. This is the standard Air Weather Service reporting format.

## Open Questions

- 125 pages of the file remain unread. Sampling suggests roughly one report per 2–4 pages (40–60 unique sightings expected for the year 1949). Notable expected incidents in 1949 include the **Green Fireballs** wave over New Mexico atomic facilities (late 1948 → 1949), the **Lubbock Lights** precursors, and the **Ellington / Ellington-Pasadena and Killeen Base** sightings — confirm coverage by re-sampling later page ranges.
- Project GRUDGE was officially in operation throughout 1949 — does this folder reflect GRUDGE's debunking-leaning posture? The Greenville SC balloon-explanation outcome is consistent with GRUDGE-era practice.
- The 6 January 1950 Olathe / Kansas City case is a temporal outlier (after the year); this may indicate the folder's actual close date is early 1950 or that the report was filed retroactively.
- Witness "Lt. Col. Robert M. ___" (Lowry FSC commanding) — surname OCR-corrupt; needs re-OCR at higher DPI.
- The Mountain Home Idaho case (24 July 1949) describes **delta-wing objects with a "dark circular bulge" on the underside** — this is a distinctive report that doesn't match the classic "flying disc" template and may represent a different class of object.
- The RB-29 radar operator's claim of **"intentional jamming"** (2 May 1949) is notable — this is one of the few cases in the file where an observer attributes the phenomenon to deliberate electronic warfare activity.
- The **Captain Thrush** case (30 July 1949) includes an interesting detail: Portland Tower was instructing other aircraft to **hold over the airport because of an unidentified flying object dropping flares** — but the B-29 pilot questioned denied dropping any flares. This creates a contradiction in the official record.
