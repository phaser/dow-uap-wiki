## Key Points

- **Conference**: Conference on the Origins of Life in Yerevan, Armenia, USSR, September 6-8, 1971.
- **Authors**: N.S. Kardashev (Institute for Space Research, Moscow) and Andrei D. Sakharov (internationally famous theoretical physicist, later known as a Soviet dissident and Nobel Peace Prize laureate).
- **Paper topic**: General relativity and the possibility of a body collapsing into a black hole in space. Specifically studied what happens when a *charged* mass collapses.
- **Key finding**: If the mass were not charged, it would simply collapse past the point of gravitational singularity and "be gone forever" — have no more contact with the outside world. But if the mass were charged, it would "collapse but bounce back out" — however, it would come back into "a different part of space time."
- **Motivations**: Sakharov was mostly interested because "the structure of space may be much more complicated than is presently thought." Kardashev was interested in astrophysical implications if the basic physics was correct.
- **U.S. assessment**: A US attendee felt the speculation was "a little fantastic" and the authors had "taken off in a rather wild way," but believed the paper was "grounded in good and sound physics."
- **Kardashev's style**: Described as "not afraid to speculate in his effort to find a solution," similar to his superior and former teacher I.S. Shklovskiy.
- **UAP relevance**: The paper itself is purely theoretical physics, not about UAP. However, the concept of charged objects traversing different regions of spacetime via gravitational bounce — effectively a theoretical mechanism for "teleportation" or interdimensional travel — has implications for UAP propulsion hypotheses.
