## Key Points

- **Background**: Project Blue Book research consultants (Battelle Memorial Institute) analyzed approximately 3,200 reports received out of ~4,000 by the end of 1952. Data was transferred to IBM punch cards for statistical analysis under the code name Project STORK.
- **Purpose**: Prepare data for briefing the Robertson Panel in January 1953 and produce a systematic statistical analysis. The basic report was 116 pages; a later-added so-called "summary" of ~30 additional pages dealt mainly with 1953-1955 reports.
- **Report significance**: This is the definitive statistical analysis that underlay the Robertson Panel's conclusions. It was distributed on a restricted basis (not public) — only about 100 copies were distributed; the RAAF destroyed their copy in 1959. The publicly available "summary" that accompanied later releases was described by the [[summaries/cia-uap-019-australian-dod-ufo-assessment]] as not actually summarizing the 1947-1952 data but rather concentrating on 1953-1955 reporting "designed to reduce the residual unknowns to an insignificant number, no matter how senseless the identification became."
- **Sighting reliability analysis** — findings that contradict the official narrative that "more data would reduce unknowns":

  | Sighting Reliability | No. of Reports | Unknown (%) | Insufficient Info (%) |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Poor | 435 | 16.6% | 21.4% |
  | Doubtful | 794 | 13.0% | 14.0% |
  | Good | 757 | 24.8% | 3.6% |
  | Excellent | 213 | 33.3% | 1.2% |

  **The percentage of unknowns INCREASED with better reliability** — excellent-reliability sightings (astronomers, pilots, scientists, surveyors, meteorologists, radar operators with instrumented values) were unknown in 33.3% of cases, while poor-reliability sightings were unknown in only 16.6%.

- **Statistical independence test**: Blue Book consultants statistically tested the unknown object population to determine if it was similar to the population of identified objects. **Probability that unknowns and knowns were from the same population: less than 1 in 10^28** (ten thousand trillion trillion to one against). After removing astronomical identifications (which had been "arbitrarily" assigned to all green fireballs and short-duration night sightings), the odds were reduced to 1 trillion trillion to one.
- **Analysts' reaction**: The consultants "could not find a way to reduce these odds sufficiently further to warrant additional testing, and irrationally considered the results to be 'inconclusive'" — as characterized by the Australian DoD assessment.
- **The "summary" deception**: The report's original conclusions, which showed mathematically that the evidence favored an explanation that was scientifically unknown, were buried. The publicly available version concentrated on 1953-1955 data and "was clearly designed to reduce the residual unknowns to an insignificant number" — per the Australian assessment.
