## Key Points

### Background and Purpose

- Battelle Memorial Institute was contracted under Project STORK to analyze UFO sighting reports. Work began in March 1952. Initially ~1,300 reports were available; by the study's end, roughly 4,000 reports had been received.
- Data was transferred to IBM punched cards using a standardized questionnaire, coding system, and work sheets. A "panel of consultants" — experts both within and outside ATIC, covering "major scientific fields and numerous specialized fields" — provided guidance.
- The study ran from 1953 with a data cut-off of end of 1952. All charts and tables terminate with year 1952 and contain 3,201 cases (ALL SIGHTINGS). Additional analysis of 1953-1955 data appears only in the SUMMARY section at the front of the report.

### Data Structure

- **ALL SIGHTINGS**: 3,201 cards (all reports received)
- **UNIT SIGHTINGS**: 2,554 cards (actual sighting events, consolidating multiple reports of same event)
  - Single observer: 2,232
  - Multiple observers: 322
- **OBJECT SIGHTINGS**: 2,199 (assumed number of distinct phenomena — consolidating multiple sightings of same object)
- About 800 reports were rejected before statistical analysis (too nebulous or internally contradictory).
- The concept of "INCIDENT SERIAL NUMBERS" (4-digit major + 2-digit minor) was developed to track reports across the system.

### Evaluation Methodology (Three-Step Process)

1. **Fact deduction**: Deduce discrete facts from subjective data (e.g., calculating approximate angular velocity and acceleration). Reports where this completely failed were eliminated (~800 cases).
2. **Observer/report rating**: Assigned based on four factors weighed in relation to each other:
   - Observer's experience (occupation, age, training)
   - Consistency among separate portions of the description
   - General quality and completeness of the report
   - Fact-reporting ability and attitude (manner of describing the sighting)
3. **Identification** (done twice):
   - **Preliminary**: By the individual who transcribed the data
   - **Final**: By a conference of four persons — two ATIC representatives, two consultant panel members. Group decision required for ALL reports finally recorded as UNKNOWN. If deadlocked, report was submitted to senior panel members for opinion; continued disagreement → recorded as UNKNOWN. Any prior ATIC identification was NOT introduced or referenced.

### Identification Categories

- Balloon, Astronomical, Aircraft, Light Phenomenon, Birds, Clouds/Dust, Insufficient Information, Psychological Manifestations, Unknown, Other
- Each identification (except Insufficient Info and Unknown) could be "Certain" (>95% confidence) or "Doubtful" (>50% confidence)
- **KNOWNS**: All categories except UNKNOWN
- **UNKNOWNS**: Reports where "the description of the object and its maneuvers could not be fitted to the pattern of any known object or phenomenon"
- **INSUFFICIENT INFORMATION**: Not a dumping ground for "poor unknowns" — specifically for reports that could have been one of several known objects but lacked an essential item of data
- **PSYCHOLOGICAL MANIFESTATIONS**: Religious fanaticism, desire for publicity, over-active imagination

### Sun Position Analysis

The report computed the angle of elevation and bearing of the sun for every sighting to determine:
- Whether the object could have been illuminated by sunlight
- Whether the object could be a mock sun (sun dog)
- Whether there was a possibility of specular reflection from an aircraft at the object's position (which would give the appearance of a "flying disc")

### Sighting Reliability Analysis — Contradiction to Official Narrative

| Sighting Reliability | No. of Reports | Unknown (%) | Insufficient Info (%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Poor | 435 | 16.6% | 21.4% |
| Doubtful | 794 | 13.0% | 14.0% |
| Good | 757 | 24.8% | 3.6% |
| Excellent | 213 | 33.3% | 1.2% |

**The percentage of unknowns INCREASED with better reliability** — excellent-reliability sightings (astronomers, pilots, scientists, surveyors, meteorologists, radar operators with instrumented values) were unknown in 33.3% of cases vs. only 16.6% for poor-reliability sightings. Excellent cases had the lowest insufficient-information rate (1.2%), meaning the evaluators HAD enough data — they still couldn't explain them.

### Chi-Square Statistical Tests — What the Report Actually Says

Six chi-square tests compared KNOWN vs. UNKNOWN distributions on: color, number of objects, shape, duration, speed, and light brightness (Tables II-VII). Results:

- **5 of 6 tests**: Probability that KNOWN and UNKNOWN distributions come from the same population was less than 1% (significant at both the 5% and 1% levels).
- **Light brightness** (6th test): Not significant — classifications deemed "too nebulous to be of real value."

Key chi-square contributors:
- **Green sightings**: 130 known; 98 were astronomical (green fireballs, Southwest U.S.). Major excess of knowns over unknowns in green.
- **Multiple objects**: Higher proportion of UNKNOWNS in the multiple-object category — "apparently these are harder to identify."
- **Rocket/aircraft shape**: Higher percentage of UNKNOWNS — might be familiar objects for which unusual maneuvers were reported.
- **Over-400-mph**: Large excess of UNKNOWNS. "Some radar sightings, which are practically impossible to identify, show objects with speeds of 1,000 to 2,000 mph and over."

**Removal of astronomical sightings**: Because astronomical phenomena showed a large excess of KNOWNS over UNKNOWNS in every significant comparison, astronomical sightings were deleted and tests re-run (Tables VIII-XIII). The fit improved but remained ambiguous — chi-square increased for light brightness and speed (attributed to subjective reporting).

### Critical Admission: The Analysis Was Stopped

> "It was decided that this process would not be carried to its logical conclusion (that is, the determination of a linear combination of KNOWNS that would give a negligible chi square when compared with the UNKNOWNS), since it was felt that the inaccuracies in the reports would give a distorted and meaningless result."

The report explicitly DECLINED to compute an overall probability — calling the individual chi-square results "inconclusive since they neither confirm nor deny that the UNKNOWNS are primarily unidentified KNOWNS, although they do indicate that relatively few of the UNKNOWNS are actually astronomical phenomena."

**Note on the "10^28:1 odds" claim**: This figure does NOT appear in the report. It appears to be an external interpretation (likely from the Australian DoD assessment in `[[summaries/cia-uap-019-australian-dod-ufo-assessment]]`) that multiplied the chi-square probabilities to estimate the odds that the two populations were the same. The report itself never computed or published such a figure; instead, it characterized the results as "inconclusive" and stopped the analysis short.

### The "Flying Saucer" Model — Attempt and Failure

A second approach: could a physical model of a "flying saucer" be derived from the UNKNOWN reports? Process:

1. **Re-evaluation of UNKNOWNS**: Panel re-examined 164 of 186 OBJECT SIGHTINGS in sun-elevation groups 1, 2, 3, 6, and 7 (sun above or less than 10° below horizon). Results:
   - 18 possible aircraft
   - 20 possible balloons
   - 7 "good UNKNOWNS" (described in sufficient detail for model-building)
   - 100 UNKNOWNS (insufficient detail)
   - 19 other possible KNOWNS
   - Two cases identified as mock suns based on sun position data
   - 5 additional "good UNKNOWNS" found in nighttime groups (4 and 5)

2. **Compelling observation**: "If these UNKNOWNS, which represent all but about 40 of the UNKNOWN SIGHTINGS, were reported to have performed maneuvers which could be ascribed to known phenomena, they would probably have been identified as KNOWNS." The single factor preventing identification was reported maneuvers.

3. **Radar sightings**: The report dismisses radar-only cases: "the most logical explanation is that they are ground targets reflected by an atmospheric temperature inversion layer." States radar sightings "are of no significance whatsoever unless a visual sighting of the object also is made."

4. **Model attempt failure**: Only 12 cases had sufficient detail for model-building. After analyzing all 12, the report concluded: **"It is not possible, therefore, to derive a verified model of a 'flying saucer' from the data that have been gathered to date."** The 12 cases were classified into four shape categories (propeller, aircraft, cigar, elliptical/disc) but each either violated one of the four model-building conditions or conflicted with the other cases.

### Psychological Observation About Reading the Reports

> "It is a definite fact that upon reading a few reports, the reader becomes convinced that 'flying saucers' are real and are some form of sinister contrivance. This reaction is independent of the training of the reader or of his attitude toward the problem prior to the initial contact."

The report states this feeling "fades" as more reports are read, eventually reaching a "point of saturation" where reports "contain no new information at all." This "feeling of surfeit was universal among the personnel who worked on this project, and continually necessitated a conscious effort on their part to remain objective."

### The 1953-1955 "Current Cases"

Evaluated on the same basis as the 1947-1952 data:

- **1953**: 425 reports; **1954**: 429 reports; **Total**: 854
  - Balloons: 16%, Aircraft: 20%, Astronomical: 25%, Other: 13%, Insufficient Info: 17%, **Unknown: 9%**
- **1 Jan – 5 May 1955** (under improved AF Reg. 200-2, with 4602d Air Intelligence Service Squadron field investigations): 131 reports
  - Balloons: 26%, Aircraft: 21%, Astronomical: 23%, Other: 20%, Insufficient Info: 7%, **Unknown: 3%**

The report attributes the decline in unknowns (from ~20% in 1947-1952 → 9% in 1953-1954 → 3% in early 1955) to "improved methods of investigation and reporting" via rapid on-the-spot investigations by the 4602d AISS.

### Official Conclusions

- No marked patterns or trends found in the data as a whole
- Combination of unreported maneuver data and unavailable supplemental data (aircraft flight plans, balloon records) prevented identification of most UNKNOWNS
- No verified model of a "flying saucer" could be derived
- Complete lack of any valid physical evidence ("physical matter") in any case
- "Probability that any of the UNKNOWNS are 'flying saucers' is concluded to be extremely small"
- "Highly improbable that any of the reports...represent observations of technological developments outside the range of present-day scientific knowledge"

## The Twelve "Good Unknown" Cases

Out of 434 OBJECT SIGHTINGS evaluated as UNKNOWN, only 12 had descriptions detailed enough to attempt model derivation. The report's analysis dismissed all 12 as failing one or more model-building conditions (general shape must fit many UNKNOWNS, observer must be reliable, must eliminate known objects, must be consistent with other good UNKNOWNS).

### Case I (Serial 0573.00) — Propeller Shape
- **Date**: July 29, 1948, 0955 hours
- **Witnesses**: Two men (rug-cleaning firm employees)
- **Location**: Driving across a bridge
- **Description**: Shiny metallic object, 6-8 feet long, 2 feet wide. Flat glide path at ~30 feet altitude, moderate left turn. Seen for "only a few seconds." Went down in a wooded area; no trace found. Had round cup-shaped protrusions.
- **Disposition**: Failed conditions 1 and 4 — propeller shape "not duplicated in any of the other UNKNOWNS."

### Case II (Serial 4508.00) — Aircraft Shape with Red Glow
- **Date**: April 20, 1952, 2115-2240 hours
- **Witnesses**: Naval aviation student, his wife, "several others"
- **Location**: Drive-in movie theater
- **Description**: ~20 groups of objects, 2-9 per group, shaped like conventional aircraft. Flew in straight lines with "standard aircraft turns." Each had a red glow, glowing itself on a cloudless night.
- **Disposition**: Failed conditions 1 and 3 — few UNKNOWNS were aircraft-shaped; red glow could have been reflection of city lights from birds or aircraft.

### Case III (Serial 2013.00, 2014.00, 2014.01) — Aircraft Shape with Instantaneous Movement
- **Date**: January 20, 1951, 2020-2026 hours
- **Witnesses**: Two tower operators; two commercial airline pilots (asked to investigate)
- **Location**: Over a city airport
- **Description**: Cigar-shaped body, straight wings, somewhat larger than a B-29. No engine nacelles. Blinking lights resembling running lights. While pilots were in climbing turn, object "made a turn toward the plane and flew across its nose." As they turned to watch, it "instantly appeared on their other side flying in the same direction," then in "2 or 3 seconds slipped under them" and vanished. Total observation time not stated.
- **Disposition**: Failed condition 1 (shape) and condition 4 when Case II was eliminated.

### Case IV (Serial 4599.00) — Two Cigar-Shaped Objects
- **Date**: July 19, 1952, midnight
- **Witnesses**: Part-time farmer and hired hand
- **Location**: Curing tobacco
- **Description**: Two cigar-shaped objects. One hovered while the other moved east and returned. Both then ascended until out of sight. Duration: 3-4 minutes. Both had an exhaust at one end. "Appeared to be transparent and illuminated from the inside." No projections.
- **Disposition**: Failed conditions 1 and 2 — few cigar/rocket shapes in the data; observer "not considered to be well-qualified technically."

### Case V (Serial 0565.00 to 0565.03) — Rocket-Like Object with Windows
- **Date**: July 24, 1948, 0340 hours
- **Witnesses**: Pilot and copilot of DC-3; one passenger saw a flash
- **Location**: In flight
- **Description**: Object approached, passed to the right and slightly above, then steep climb into clouds. Duration: ~10 seconds. "Seemed powered by rocket or jet motors shooting a trail of fire some 50 feet to the rear." No wings or protrusions. Two rows of lighted windows. Cockpit windshield-like feature visible.
- **Disposition**: Failed conditions 1 and 4 when Case IV eliminated.

### Case VI (Serial 4822.00) — Rolling Object, 15-Minute Observation
- **Date**: December 22, 1952, 1930 hours
- **Witness**: Instrument technician
- **Location**: Driving toward an Air Force base, then stopped car
- **Description**: Object moved up toward zenith in spurts from right to left at ~45°, then level flight at high speed. "Appeared white most of the time but apparently rolled three times showing a red side." "About halfway through its roll it showed no light at all." Stabilized south of Jupiter, then "darted back and forth, left and right alternately." Duration: 15 minutes.
- **Disposition**: Failed condition 2 (observer reliability) — the "band of no light" as diagrammed created a physical contradiction; without the drawing there was "not enough detail."

### Case VII (Serial 2728.00) — Tumbling Object Near Sun, Korea
- **Date**: June 6, 1952, 0842 hours
- **Witnesses**: Flight Sergeant; second man also saw it
- **Location**: Over an Air Force base in Korea
- **Description**: Object flew in "a series of spinning and tumbling actions" on an erratic course — flying level, stopping momentarily, shooting straight up, flying level, tumbling again. Disappeared into the sun, reappeared, seen "flying back and forth across the sun." An F-86 passed between the observer and the object. Disc-shaped, with black lines evenly spaced, proportion 7 to 1.
- **Disposition**: Failed conditions 1 and 4 — disc-like but maneuvers "unique both among the UNKNOWNS and among the good UNKNOWNS."

### Case VIII (Serial 0576.00) — Smooth Elliptical Object
- **Date**: July 31, 1948, 0825 hours
- **Witnesses**: Electrician; wife also saw it
- **Location**: Home bathroom window, then kitchen
- **Description**: Object flew "straight and level course from horizon to horizon, west to east." Duration: ~10 seconds. 6-8 feet across, 20 feet long (ratio ~3:1). Smooth, no protrusions or details.
- **Disposition**: Satisfies conditions 1-3 but fails condition 4 — smooth with no protrusions, inconsistent with other good UNKNOWNS that have features.

### Case IX (Serial 0066.00) — Hedge-Hopping Disc with Flames
- **Date**: August 13, 1947, ~1300 hours
- **Witnesses**: Farmer and his two sons (ages 8 and 10)
- **Location**: Fishing camp, near canyon wall
- **Description**: Object ~300 feet away, 75 feet above ground, seen against 400-foot canyon wall. "Hedge hopping, following the contour of the ground." Sky blue, ~20 feet diameter, 10 feet thick. Pods on sides "from which flames were shooting out." Made a "swishing sound." Trees "highly agitated by the craft as it passed over." Seen for only "a few seconds."
- **Disposition**: Satisfies conditions 1-3 but fails condition 4 — rocket/jet pods with flames, inconsistent with other good UNKNOWNS.

### Case X (Serial 1119.00) — Dirty Metallic Object, Binocular Observation
- **Date**: May 24, 1949, ~1700 hours
- **Witnesses**: Employee of supersonic laboratory of an aeronautical laboratory; "some other employees"
- **Location**: By a river, 2.5 miles from its mouth
- **Description**: Object reflecting sunlight; viewed with 8-power binoculars, at which point "there was no glare." Metallic construction, good enough resolution to show "the skin was dirty." Moved off in horizontal flight, "gradually increasing rate of speed" until approaching "the speed of a jet." No propulsion visible. Duration: 2.5-3 minutes. Had "something equivalent to a patch" — a fin or rudder.
- **Disposition**: Satisfies conditions 1-3 but fails condition 4 — fin/rudder feature, inconsistent with other good UNKNOWNS.

### Case XI (Serial 1550.00) — 100-Foot Disc with Portholes, >1000 mph
- **Date**: March 20, 1950, 2126 hours
- **Witnesses**: Reserve Air Force Captain and an airlines Captain (commercial flight)
- **Location**: ~15 miles north of a medium-sized city, in flight
- **Description**: Object approached from the south. Passed in front of airliner at no more than 1/2 mile distance, ~1000 feet higher altitude. "Circular, with a diameter of approximately 100 feet," vertical height "considerably less than the diameter" (disc-like). Top center: blinking light at ~3 flashes per second, "so brilliant that it would have been impossible to look at it continuously had it not been blinking." Underside: 9-12 symmetrical oval or circular portholes in a circle, emitting "a soft purple light about the shade of aircraft fluorescent lights." Straight-line flight, no spinning. Estimated speed: "in excess of 1000 mph." Duration: 25-35 seconds.
- **Disposition**: Satisfies conditions 1-3 but fails condition 4 — portholes/windows on underside, inconsistent with other good UNKNOWNS.

### Case XII (Serial 3601.00) — Hovering Craft with Occupant Visible
- **Date**: August 25, 1952, 0535 hours
- **Witness**: Musician for a radio station (had an artificial leg, could not leave road)
- **Location**: Driving to work from home
- **Description**: Object hovering ~10 feet above a field, ~100 yards from witness. "Not absolutely still, but seemed to rock slightly." Deep throbbing sound when car engine off. Upon vertical ascent: sound "similar to a large covey of quail starting to fly at one time." Ascended vertically through broken clouds until out of sight. Vegetation "blown about by the object when it was near the ground." Investigation afterward: "some evidence of vegetation being blown around"; grass and soil samples showed "nothing unusual."

  Object: ~75 feet long, 45 feet wide, 15 feet thick. "Shaped like two oval meat platters placed together." Dull aluminum color, smooth surface. Front section: one window with "medium-blue continuous light"; "head and shoulders of one man, sitting motionless, facing the forward edge of the object, were visible." Midsection: multiple windows from top to rear edge; blue light changing to different shades; "large amount of activity and movement...could not be identified as either human or mechanical." No windows/doors in rear or underside. Series of propellers 6-12 inches in diameter "spaced closely together along the outer edge," mounted on brackets, "revolved in a horizontal plane along the edge of the object" at high speed. Observer reliability: "considered good."
- **Disposition**: Satisfies conditions 1-3 but fails condition 4 — windows in top/front, propellers around waist, inconsistent with other good UNKNOWNS.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- The report analyzed 3,201 ALL SIGHTINGS representing ~2,199 distinct phenomena. The 12 good-UNKNOWN cases (detailed above) are the only individually described sightings in the entire report body.
- Kenneth Arnold's June 1947 sighting near Mount Rainier is cited in the Introduction as the incident that triggered the "flying saucer" phenomenon.

## Open Questions

- The "10^28:1 odds" figure (cited in `[[summaries/cia-uap-019-australian-dod-ufo-assessment]]`) does NOT appear in the report itself. Where exactly did this number come from, and who computed it?
- The report's explicit decision to NOT carry the chi-square analysis to its "logical conclusion" — was this a statistical judgment or an intentional choice to avoid producing a number that would be politically inconvenient?
- The 1953-1954 "unknown" rate of 9% (dropping to 3% in 1955 under improved procedures) — did the "improved" investigation process genuinely resolve unknowns, or did it reclassify them?
- Where are the surviving copies of the ~100 distributed? How many still exist?
- What was in the 800 rejected reports?
- Did the consultants' "feeling of surfeit" and their acknowledgment that reading reports initially "convinces" the reader that saucers are real — followed by a fade into skepticism — represent genuine analytic objectivity or desensitization bias?
