## Key Points

This is one of the most important cross-cutting intelligence assessments in the collection. Written by an Australian Department of Defence analyst with apparent access to classified U.S. information, it provides a remarkably candid history and critique of U.S. UFO policy.

### U.S. Official Attitude to UFOs (detailed chronology)

1. **1947**: ATIC assumed responsibility for "flying saucer" reports. Within a month, concluded phenomena were "real and probably of Soviet origin."
2. **End of 1947**: Project SIGN authorized. Most investigators shifted to interplanetary rather than Soviet origin hypothesis.
3. **September 1948**: Project SIGN sent a "written estimate" to the Pentagon stating UFOs were of interplanetary origin. **Rejected** by Air Force Chief of Staff General Vandenberg on grounds of "insufficient hard evidence." This caused a reaction at ATIC against "trying to unravel the UFO problem."
4. **January 1948**: Dr. J.E. Lipp of RAND Corporation replied to Director of Research and Development, USAF: if flying objects were interplanetary, they would "most likely originate from Mars" but noted Venus was possible.
5. **February 1949**: Project SIGN personnel replaced by new personnel to form Project GRUDGE. "A definite attempt was made during 1949 to use Project GRUDGE to destroy any acceptance of UFOs." **Motives**: possibly Air Force embarrassment, fear of national panic, or "to provide a breathing space for another 'investigative agency' to reach some conclusion." This agency was "not the FBI, had rocket, nuclear, and intelligence experts, and their purpose was to study UFO reports in an effort to attain design data on interplanetary spaceships." The Australian analyst concludes: **"This agency was almost certainly the CIA."**
6. **1949**: GRUDGE final report (Technical Report No. 102-AC 49/15-100) concluded all reports were misidentifications, mass hysteria, and hoaxes — despite admitting 23% were "unknown."
7. **September 1951 – March 1952**: UFO investigation regained adequate financing and administrative support. Project BLUE BOOK established.
8. **Summer 1952**: More than twenty-fold rise in reporting rate. Two extensive July sightings involving Washington D.C. Components of USAF intelligence **"considered that UFOs were interplanetary spaceships which were about to make closer contact."** Previously classified reports were released for publication (August 1952 – February 1953), **contradicting earlier official USAF policy** of dismissing reports.
9. **CIA view**: CIA regarded the summer 1952 activity as a "threat to national security" — primarily because of communications overload and defense forces involvement lessening alertness against possible enemy attack.
10. **January 1953**: Robertson Panel convened. ATIC showed classified UFO films and early statistical results. Panel recommendations adopted — but the Robertson panel "felt restricted to recommend" continuation with increased personnel. **However, the CIA in a February 16, 1953 report "showed a preference to publicly abandon the investigation whilst intensifying the collection of data."**
11. **By September 1953**: Blue Book reduced from 10 qualified personnel at Top Secret level to one airman. Investigating component transferred to **4602nd Air Intelligence Service Squadron** (trained for rapid intelligence procurement, reporting to ADC and USAF Intelligence Washington — NOT Blue Book).
12. **JANAP 146 (1953)**: Prohibited service personnel from discussing UFOs — up to 10 years jail and $10,000 fine. Revised JANAP 146E (1960) made it an offense under the Espionage Act.
13. **Retiring officers publicly contradicted official policy**: Major D. Fournet (Blue Book Project Officer at Pentagon until late 1952), Captain E. Ruppelt (headed GRUDGE and BLUE BOOK until September 1953), and **Admiral Hillenkoetter (CIA Director from inception until October 1950)** — on retiring, all publicly stated the U.S. Government knew UFOs were extraterrestrial but was withholding it from the public.
14. **NICAP (1955)**: First Chairman was Admiral D.S. Fahrney (directed Navy's guided missile program). Other directors included Admiral Hillenkoetter, Major Keyhoe, Major Fournet, Rear Admiral H.B. Knowles, General A. Wedemeyer, and Col. J.J. Bryan (special assistant to Secretary of the Air Force).
15. **Blue Book Special Report No. 14**: Statistical analysis showed (a) unknowns increased with better reliability — 33.3% for "excellent" vs. 16.6% for "poor"; (b) the unknown population was statistically distinct from the identified population with odds of 10^28 to 1 against. The report's actual body was suppressed and replaced with a misleading "summary."
16. **Covert gravity research program**: "The government agency (almost certainly CIA) that had been collecting data on UFO performance and propulsion methods during 1948-52 presumably influenced U.S. governmental funding of certain advanced projects." This led to:
    - **Avro Canada saucer project** — drawing released October 1955 showed "a typical flying disc as described in many UFO reports." Secretary of the Air Force Donald Quarles appeared "moderately confident" of success. The U.S. assumed funding when Canada withdrew in 1955; U.S. even prevented Canadian officials from inspecting the premises.
    - **Gravity control research** — despite science having "not attained a level of competence to deal with either gravity or anti-gravity," the U.S. "chose to support six universities and government agencies in an all-out drive to conquer the problem." Participating institutions: Institute for Advanced Study (Princeton), Princeton University, University of Indiana, Purdue Research Foundation, University of North Carolina, MIT/Babson Gravity Research Institute. **"Such an intensive onslaught on the gravity enigma was entirely irrational from the standpoint of conventional science, and can only be rationalized within the context of a firm belief that UFOs were real and that the intelligences behind them know how to control gravity."**
    - By 1966: **46 separate gravity/anti-gravity projects** receiving financial support, **33 under USAF supervision**.
17. **August 1965 wave**: 262 reports — 6x average, 2x any previous month since November 1957. This led to the Condon Committee.
18. **Condon Committee discredited**: Condon stated publicly on January 25, 1967: "My attitude right now is that there's nothing to it, but I'm not supposed to reach a conclusion for another year." Combined with a memorandum outlining a method to trick the public, most staff resigned after investigations were completed but not written up. Condon's conclusions were at variance with staff conclusions.
19. **Dr. J. Allen Hynek**: Scientific consultant to Blue Book 1948-1969. Began with conviction that all sightings could be conventionally explained. "Even though doubts grew in his mind, he found himself obliged to support official USAF public policy." After 1966 he became increasingly outspoken against USAF attitude.

### Australian Recommendations

- Australia "lacks an intelligence viewpoint that can assess the nature and possible consequences of the problem, a scientific viewpoint that could derive scientifically valid data from the reports, and a public relations viewpoint that can honestly satisfy public interest."
- "It would appear wrong for Australia to remain ignorant of the true situation."
- Strong case for "another government department to assume responsibility for investigation and analysis of UFO reports" rather than RAAF.
- **Independent approach recommended**: Rather than following the "public USAF attitude," Australia should "follow the USAF/CIA role of concentrating on gaining a knowledge of the power sources involved." Or "act independently of the U.S. and initiate a programme that is scientifically sound and intellectually honest towards unravelling the UFO mystery."

## Open Questions

- The Australian analyst had apparent access to classified information about the CIA's covert role — how did an Australian defence analyst obtain this information?
- The admission that the U.S. prevented Canadian officials from inspecting the Avro saucer project premises is extraordinary — what was being hidden?
- "JANAP 146E, passed in 1960, made it an offense under the Espionage Act if data on UFOs were revealed" — this effectively classified UFO data at the highest level. Is this still in effect?
- The document states that "46 separate projects" in gravity research existed by 1966 — what was the total U.S. government investment, and what results were obtained?
- This assessment, candidly criticizing U.S. policy, was apparently an internal Australian document. Was it ever shared with U.S. counterparts?
