## Key Points

- January 28, 1985, the Embassy received an **informal inquiry from the PNG National Intelligence Organization (NIO)** about reported sightings of high-altitude, high-speed aircraft over Papua New Guinea on the **evening of January 24, 1985**.
- Trigger for inquiry: NIO's officer in **Wewak** reported local residents had been "frightened by overflights," prompting the Provincial Premier to call a public meeting on the subject — attended by the **Prime Minister** (who was weekending in his electoral district).
- Reports include "fast-moving objects with lights, contrails, and noise."
- **Most credible report**, per NIO: an **Air Niugini pilot** who had just taken off from Wewak en route to Port Moresby. The pilot's radar picked up aircraft "flying south to north at high altitude and high speed" when he was over **Angoram (vic 4°S, 144°E)**.
- Additional reports: visual sightings of contrails — one aircraft moving N→S at 1900 local; **six to eight aircraft traveling S→N at 2200 local**.
- Embassy's response (para 3): Based on its records and a telcon with 43SW (Anderson AFB Guam), the Embassy told NIO it knew of "no B-52 overflights and no U.S. aircraft in PNG airspace on January 24."
- Embassy requests USCINCPAC confirmation of para 3 and any clarification.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **Papua New Guinea Multiple-Overflight Event, January 24, 1985 (evening)** — multiple visual and one radar witness report unidentified high-altitude high-speed traffic over PNG; included a flight of 6-8 aircraft at 2200 local moving S→N. Air Niugini pilot's radar captured the formation near Angoram. U.S. and PNG authorities both deny aircraft presence. Public meeting held; PM attended.

## Open Questions

- Did USCINCPAC respond to confirm or deny U.S. aircraft activity? The follow-up cable (if any) is not in this batch.
- Were the unidentified aircraft Soviet or other foreign assets transiting near PNG, or genuinely anomalous? The S→N evening tracks are consistent with aircraft from Australia/SE Asia heading toward Pacific staging points but the Embassy and 43SW had no record of friendly traffic.
- The cable is referenced as "DoS UAP D1" in the State Dept release naming convention, suggesting it is the first of a series of UAP cables.
