## Key Points

This file is a **large correspondence collection** from Sandia Base (a U.S. Army/Air Force installation near Albuquerque, NM) spanning multiple documents. The UAP-relevant content is concentrated in several key sections:

### 1. Security Inspection Report (7 April 1949)
- Lt Col Richard T. Mandelkorn (Detachment D, 1100th USAF Special Reporting Group, Campbell AFB) reports on security inspection of Sandia Base.
- Guard orders amended, electric gate hours changed, inspection gate bolts replaced, inspection port locks added, alert plan reviewed, jeep radios installed, radio communication with Camp Campbell Military Police in effect.
- Erosion control issues noted at estimated cost of $20,000.

### 2. Dr. Lincoln LaPaz — Copper Particle Analysis (10 August 1949)
- Letter from an unnamed author to **Dr. Lincoln LaPaz** at the Institute of Meteoritics, University of New Mexico.
- Reports preliminary examination of **eighteen collections taken at nine locations** on a Monday trip.
- Large number of **copper-bearing particles** found on one collection (R-104L) taken on Highway 84, 17 miles north of Highway 66.
- Tentative conclusion: no widespread significant dispersion of copper-bearing particles, but one heavy copper collection should be presumed of local origin until evidence to contrary is found.
- Route included Pondera, Santa Rosa, junction of Routes 66 and 84, and Las Vegas on Monday afternoon; Las Vegas, Cannon City, Glorieta, and Oca on Tuesday morning.

### 3. Orozter & Seely — Fireball Particle Analysis (August 1949)
**"An Attempt to Collect Meteoritic Particles Associated with the Fireball of July 24, 1949"**
- A fireball was reported over **Socorro, New Mexico at 8:26 p.m. on July 24, 1949**.
- Impactment equipment developed in connection with the aerosol research project of the **New Mexico School of Mines** was used to systematically collect airborne material.
- Equipment processed air at ~34 liters/minute; particles separated by impactment against an adhesive-coated plate in an air jet. ~90% of airborne particles >1 micron collected.
- Adhesive used: glycerin-gelatin mixture with **rubeanic acid (dithiooxamide)** to enable identification of copper, nickel, and cobalt.
- **First collection (10:00 AM, July 25, ~13.5 hours after fireball)**: Several large particles gave **positive copper tests**. At least one showed copper reaction **before ammonia treatment**, indicating **soluble copper compound**. Particles up to **100+ microns** in largest dimension — unusually large.
- Eight days of collections over a period of eight days.
- **Key finding**: Particles in smallest size group (1-15 microns) practically absent from early collections; in later collections, particles in largest (30+ micron) and middle (15-30 micron) groups became sparse while counts became large in smallest size group.
- **Cobalt and nickel tests**: In the 200-liter collection of **July 26, three remarkable particles** gave **very strong cobalt indications**. Particles were **apparently perfect spheres** (perfectly circular in cross-section), **twelve microns in diameter**. Dense yellow-tan halo, 60 microns diameter, suggesting high-cobalt composition with some metal present. **"Such particles are quite unique in the experience of the present investigators."**
- Two particles gave nickel indications.
- **Conclusion**: "Very hazardous to draw any definite conclusion associating the copper-bearing particles collected with the fireball of July 24." But a "residuum of possibility" remains — particles of the smallest size group did not appear in large numbers until **~35 hours after passage of the fireball**.
- **Airplane collection (August 8)**: B-25 from Kirtland Field, max altitude 23,000 ft. 41 hours after event. A few copper indications obtained, but associated with material of surface origin. **No nickel or cobalt indications.**

### 4. Dr. LaPaz — Sixth Report on Green Fireballs (August 17, 1949)
- **January 30, 1949 green fireball**: No fragments recovered despite air and ground search along determined earth-trace. Possibility that fireball was "so completely volatilized during flight that only fine dust from it filtered down."
- Copper or copper alloy expected as primary component of such dust (rather than ferro-magnetic substances found in ordinary meteorites).
- Dr. William Orozter's impactum dust collector used (developed for Aerosol Project at New Mexico School of Mines). Orozter previously worked at **Hanscom Proving Grounds** in 1943-44 on sabot and projectile-fused shells.
- **July 24 fireball**: Dr. Orozter alerted by appeal from **Institute of Meteoritics**. Made dust collections on campus of Socorro on morning of July 25. Collections contained **first copper particles he had found in air dust collections**, and these were **unusually large — up to 100 microns**.
- Field search party dispatched from **17th District OSI HQ** to Socorro: **Major Charles L. Phillips** (from Kirtland Field via Col. Harold Gunn), **Mr. Paul Tait** (U.S. Weather Bureau, Albuquerque), **S/A Jack Bolin**, and the undersigned. Conference with Dr. Orozter and Mr. Seely on July 26.
- Dr. Orozter initially inclined to attribute large copper fragments to particles blown off the roof/attic of the Assaying Building on the campus. When asked if copper would show up in collections made in the open summit at distance from campus, he said yes — would rule out campus source.
- Ground collections made along route through San Antonio, Carthage, and Bingham — **all contained copper particles indistinguishable from those collected in Socorro at about the same time**.

### 5. LaPaz's Critical Commentary on Orozter's Conclusion
LaPaz (the writer) offers critical commentary on why Dr. Orozter reached a "negative or inconclusive" conclusion, listing factors in decreasing order of importance:
- **(3.1)** Copper indications found in dust collected at Socorro on **July 14, 1949** (10 days before the main fireball incident). Orozter said this collection was carefully wrapped and couldn't have been contaminated. LaPaz counters: an unobserved green fireball shortly before July 14 could have put down the copper dust.
- **(3.2)** Discrepancy between computed and observed rates of descent of copper particles. LaPaz notes that **nearly vertical real paths** of the fireballs of July 24 and August 6 extended from altitudes of **100+ miles down almost to ground level** — optimum conditions for long-continued fall of dust particles.
- **(3.3)** Continued scarcity of cobalt particles (smaller and smaller in size). Such decrease in particle size has been observed in meteoritic dust collections.
- **(3.4)** Failure of August 8 airplane collection to detect non-terrestrial copper particles.

### 6. LaPaz's Recommendations
- **(6.1)** Arrange for dust collections on airplane flights at **40,000+ feet** through the region beneath green fireball paths as soon as possible after occurrence.
- Requires coordinated observing network for rapid trajectory determination.
- **Key scientific point**: Copper is **one of the rarest elements found in meteorites**. LaPaz cites G.P. Merrill (Memoirs National Academy of Science, vol. 14, 1925) and Harrison Brown (Journal of Geology, vol. 66, March 1948). **"I know of no case in which even the tiniest particle of copper has been reported in a dust collection supposedly of meteoritic origin."**
- **"If in future more detailed work shows that the numerous copper particles found by Dr. Orozter and Mr. Seely are from green fireballs, then the fireballs are not conventional meteorites."**

### 7. OSI Summary of Green Fireball Information (19 April 1949)
- **NW 91526** document from **17th District Office of Special Investigation, Kirtland AFB, New Mexico**.
- Comprehensive summary of all observations of unidentified aerial phenomena in the **New Mexico – West Texas area**.
- **Common characteristics**:
  - (a) **Green color** (sometimes greenish-white, bright green, yellow-green, or blue-green)
  - (b) **Horizontal path**, sometimes with minor variations
  - (c) **Speed less than that of a meteor, but more than any known type of aircraft**
  - (d) **No sound** associated with observations
  - (e) **No persistent trail or dust cloud**
  - (f) **Period of visibility from one to five seconds**
- None of the incidents reported possess all characteristics, but one or more are present in each.
- **No natural or man-made object ever determined to be responsible in any reported incident.**
- Tabulation drawn from OSI personnel interviews; many other persons interviewed by Dr. LaPaz and colleagues not included due to incomplete data.
- **Dr. Lincoln LaPaz** identified as "internationally known and respected as a meteoriticist."

### 8. Green Fireball Sighting Tabulation (December 1948 – February 1949)
- Tabulation of sightings from **5 December 1948 through 17 February 1949**.
- Multiple sightings on **5 December 1948** alone: 15+ objects between 15:13 and 23:15, primarily **green**, horizontal path, seen by civilians, military pilots, university students, and AESS security agents.
- Locations: **Las Vegas NM, Albuquerque NM, Lucy NM, Sandia Base, Los Alamos**.
- **6 January 1949**: Green object at 03:10 from Los Alamos (AESS Inspector).
- **14 February 1949**: White-greenish at 18:40 from Moe Ganado, Arizona (Dr. Salzman).
- **17 February 1949**: Additional entries.

### 9. Los Alamos Conference on Green Fireballs (16 February 1949)
**Report of Trip to Los Alamos, 16 February 1949, by Commander Richard S. Mandelkorn, U.S.N.**

- Conference held at Los Alamos to consider the **green fireball phenomena** that commenced about **5 December 1948**.
- **Attendees for Los Alamos**:
  - **Mr. N. E. Bradbury** (Director, Los Alamos)
  - **Mr. Marshall Holloway**
  - **Mr. Fred Reines**
  - **Mr. John Manley**
  - **Mr. Edward Teller**
  - **Mr. Elmo Morgan** (AEC)
  - **Mr. Sidney Neuberger** (Security)
  - **Mr. Maxwell** (AESS)
  - **Mr. Hoyt**
- **Fourth Army**: Major William A. Godsoe, Major Wynn
- **USAF**: Captain Neef
- **University of New Mexico**: **Dr. Lincoln LaPaz**
- **Sandia Base**: Commander Richard Mandelkorn

- **Captain Neef** opened: Problem classified military **SECRET under the name "Project Grudge"**. Investigation now primary responsibility of USAF, Air Materiel Command, T-1.
- **Dr. LaPaz** described the **"Starvation Peak Incident"** he observed himself:
  - Initial bright light (no period of intensity increase), constant intensity during duration
  - **Yellow-green color (~6,200 angstroms)**
  - Essentially **horizontal path**
  - Trajectory traversed at **constant angular velocity**
  - Duration about **two seconds**
  - **No accompanying noise**
- Since ~5 December 1948: **more than ten incidents analogous to the green fireball**, and **twenty more presenting minor deviations**.
- **Geographic confinement**: Green fireballs confined to the **Los Alamos – Las Vegas – West Texas triangle**. No green fireballs reported in any other areas despite average meteor observing conditions throughout the continental U.S.
- **Edward Teller's analysis**:
  - Material object traveling at ~**8 miles per second** would need mass of ~**20 grams** (assuming all kinetic energy converted to light, light output ~10^?? ergs per second).
  - Shock wave from such object at **8-10 mile altitude** at **7-8 miles per second** would produce **loud noise easily audible 10 km from source**.
  - **No sound has been observed.**
  - **Teller's tentative opinion: they are NOT material objects passing through the air. "We should look to electronics and optics for an explanation rather than in the field of hydrodynamics."**
  - It was "almost incredible" that a large object (guided missile or unknown vehicle) could pass through atmosphere at 8 miles altitude at 7-8 miles/sec without producing loud noise.
- **Bradbury demurred** on the electronic explanation: "if it were assumed that the answer lay in that region, many more difficult problems would have to be solved."
- **Recommended actions**:
  - (a) Recalculate Teller's data with more accurate treatment
  - (b) Establish well-equipped observation stations for photometric and photographic coverage in the geographic area
  - (c) If Teller's theories are borne out, **declassify the Project to permit participation by scientists throughout the country**
- **LaPaz and Neef fruitlessly attempted** to obtain information from a meteorite observers' group at White Sands working under contract for the Navy.
- **Mandelkorn's conclusion**: These phenomena "are deserving of serious consideration until their source and meaning have been satisfactorily explained."
- **"Although Mr. Teller's discussion tends to disprove the hypothesis that guided missiles or unknown vehicles are responsible, there is cause for concern of the continued occurrences of unexplainable phenomena of this nature in the vicinity of sensitive installations."**
- **Captain Neef reports**: Blue fireball visible from Sandia at 05:30, 17 February 1949; yellow-orange cigar-shaped light at 17:59, visible until 18:06, 17 February.

### 10. Visual Lights — Camp Hood, Texas (March 1949)
- **19 March 1949**: Four unidentified lights near 915.21 – 855.19 — three yellow, one red.
- **18 March 1949**: Another yellow light seen by one man near 910.41 – 855.10. No indication of movement, no noise.
- **19 March 1949**: Blinking lights appeared in dowry of Rock Quarry at 905.82 – 855.61. Investigation failed to reveal cause.
- **Captain Horace McCullough** (HQ 2d Armored Division) and **Mr. Bayard Schidiieke** (Special Agent, Killeen Base) spent several hours from observation point in "Q" Area attempting to observe blinking lights.
- FBI San Antonio notified. Distribution to AC of G-2, OSI, Kirtland AFB, Sandia Base.

## Documented Examples / Cases

- **Green fireball wave (December 1948 – February 1949)** — Multiple sightings across New Mexico and West Texas triangle, primarily green objects with horizontal path, no sound, speed between meteor and aircraft.
- **July 24, 1949 fireball over Socorro** — Green fireball at 8:26 PM; copper, cobalt, and nickel particles collected in subsequent days. Unusually large copper particles (up to 100 microns), perfect-sphere cobalt particles (12 microns).
- **August 6, 1949 fireball** — Second green fireball; ground and airplane collections made.
- **January 30, 1949 green fireball** — No fragments recovered; complete volatilization hypothesized.
- **16 February 1949 Los Alamos conference** — Bradbury, Teller, LaPaz, Holloway, Reines, Manley, and others convened to discuss green fireball phenomena. Teller argued against material-object hypothesis based on acoustic analysis.
- **5 December 1948 multi-object wave** — 15+ green objects sighted in single evening from Las Vegas to Albuquerque to Sandia Base.
