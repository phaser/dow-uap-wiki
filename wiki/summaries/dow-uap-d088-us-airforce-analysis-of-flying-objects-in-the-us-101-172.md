## Key Points

This volume bridges **Project SIGN** and the transition toward **Project GRUDGE**. The sample covers the **Norcatur, Kansas fireball of 18 February 1948** in extensive detail (Incidents 101-103), plus cases through mid-1948. The Norcatur case is the most heavily documented single incident in the sample, with multiple witness statements, scientific analysis, and speculative interpretation by civilian correspondent [[entities/markham-norman-g|Norman G. Markham]].

### Norcatur, Kansas Explosion — 18 February 1948 (Incidents 101-103)

A massive aerial explosion over Norcatur, Kansas at approximately 1500 hours (CST) was witnessed across multiple states (Oklahoma, New Mexico, Colorado, Kansas, Nebraska). Initially classified as a meteor/fireball, the incident generated significant controversy.

**Multiple Witness Accounts (Incident 101 supplement)**:
- **Leland Sammons** (farmer near Norcatur): Described a "rocket-like" craft that "lit up my whole yard." Object on the ground started up, produced sparks, then "took off in a northwesterly direction very fast, gaining altitude as it went." Left a trail of smoke. Terrific explosion heard seconds later. Hot spot where it rose from was in the yard.
- **Kenneth Hays** (east of Norton, KS): Saw "what appeared to be a rocket, just like he'd seen during the war in Europe." Object exploded with a big cloud of smoke.
- **N.W. Jewell** (Postmaster, Norcatur): At ~1450 observed a "blinding flash as if someone had taken a flashlight picture." ~1.5 minutes later, a "terrific explosion and jar, shaking the ground and causing windows to rattle." Low reverberating rumblings across the heavens afterward.
- **Edgar Young** (Regar, KS): Observed the explosion and a huge cloud of smoke almost overhead.
- **Norton battery case**: A "burned flashlight battery" fell in the street of Norton just after the explosion, "too hot to be picked up for several minutes." Held by Chief Scott of Norton police.

**B-29 Aircrew Corroboration (Incidents 102-103)**:
- Two B-29 aircraft of the 28th Bombardment Group (VH) sighted a "**huge multi-colored ball of fire** trailing a dense cloud of smoke" at approx. 1500 hours MST, 18 Feb 1948, approx. 39°N-100°W near Green River, Utah.
- Object heading southeast of Limon, Colorado, at approx. 20,000 ft. Estimated 100 miles from the B-29s. Size: "huge" — unable to determine accurately due to distance. Speed: "very high."
- Witnesses: Capt Howard B. Berodt, Capt Maurice T. Ritenour, 1st Lt Leonard P. Marchese, 1st Lt Carl W. Stucki. All from 718th/77th Bomb Squadrons.

### Norman G. Markham's Correspondence (Letters to Army Signal Office, February-March 1948)

Two letters from Norman G. Markham to the Chief Signal Officer, U.S. Army, preserved in the file (Incident 101 appendix):

**Letter 1 (20 Feb 1948) — "Moon Rocket" Hypothesis**:
- Markham argues the Norcatur object was not a meteor but may have been a **space-craft bound for the moon**.
- Observes that a line from Norcatur's meridian at hour-angle 4 o'clock, projected outward to lunar orbit, would intersect where the moon would be ~60-100 hours after the explosion.
- Notes rocketry theory: ~100 hours to travel Earth-to-Moon distance.
- Suggests the object was first seen as a "streak of smoke" and later as a "ball of fire," then a "tremendous explosion" — consistent with a multi-stage rocket gaining speed and elevation for "take-off" toward the moon.
- Explicitly references "flying saucers" and bases speculation on "long consideration of various oddments of reports whose significance might be of space-craft from other worlds."

**Letter 2 (14 March 1948) — Latitudinal Belt Hypothesis**:
- Catalogues a series of 1947-1948 sightings within a latitudinal belt from 43°36'N (Boise, ID) to 36°10'N (Nashville, TN) — about 485 miles wide.
- **Boise Object** (24 June 1947): Seen by Idaho Lt. Gov. Whitehead and Chief Justice Lampert. Comet-like appearance in western sky for 20 minutes. Markham suggests possible rocket-type craft using reaction blasts to brake for landing.
- **Holle's "Ship in Flames"** (3 July 1947): Forest lookout in Sonoma County, CA saw what he thought was a "tanker in flames." Navy/Coast Guard search found nothing. Soviet tanker ELBRUZ queried and found "all right."
- **Cape Mendocino Blimp Incident** (14 July 1947): Navy blimp suddenly settled into water, slipped crew, rose again. Markham links to possible unknown forces generated by unknown objects.
- **Oakland "Saturn-like" Object** (13 October 1947): Photographer Hen Dobus and taxi driver A.J. Goldman saw an object that "looked like Saturn with a ring around it" shooting at "terrific speed" westward.
- **Ticonderoga Discs** (12 November 1947): Two "flying discs" heading southwest at 43°15'N, 124°54'W.
- **Phantom Reef** (24 November 1947): Navy survey ship MAURY searched for reported obstruction 400 miles west of San Francisco. Picked up echo from something in water at 1,600 yards; at 400 yards, echo vanished.
- **Mantell Incident** (7 January 1948): Linked to the latitudinal belt pattern.

Markham's conclusion: "I submit there is a likelihood that on June 24, 1947, something like a space-ship came from the moon and on February 18, 1948, returned to the moon." He offered his services to the Army for anomaly recognition.

### Dr. Lincoln LaPaz's Response (11 April 1948)

Excerpt of letter from Dr. Lincoln LaPaz, Director, Institute of Meteoritics, UNM, to the Deputy Executive Director, Committee on Geophysical Sciences, R&D Board:

- Characterizes aspects of Markham's letters as "fantastic" but acknowledges Markham "is justified in calling attention to certain incidents as unexplained."
- On Norcatur: Remains convinced it was a "genuine meteorite fall," albeit "one of exceptional size" — but notes "curious aspects": intensive searches found "**not a trace of meteoritic material**."
- Describes witness testimony conflicts: Tower men at McCook Field, Nebraska described the Norcatur object as "a black object with an extremely bright jet of flame pouring out of the rear" — denying it was a fireball. A 14-year-old schoolgirl in Oberlin, KS gave similar description.
- Battery case examined by Dr. Victor Regener (Dept of Physics, UNM): Identical to small batteries used in portable radios. Glenn first reported it as "red hot" but changed account to "too hot to handle" when LaPaz noted the paper on it wasn't charred.
- Broader assessment: "The 'meteoritic' incidents from the great fall of 1945... through the sequence of similar falls culminating in the Four Corners and Norcatur incidents, coupled with such things as the Ussuri incident, convince me that **either the earth is under a most unusual cosmic bombardment or many of the fireballs are not meteorites at all**."

### Additional Sampled Incidents

**Incident 104 (March 1948)**: Smyrna AAF, Tennessee — Yellow-orange object observed ~6 miles away for ~45 minutes between west and northwest, moving slowly away. Faded into horizon. No exhaust trail, no sound. Witnesses: Maj Frank L. [REDACTED], Col Richard C. Pettingill, Pvt Frank C. Johnson. Two photographs taken showing "yellow orange flare-like orb."

**Incident 105 (8 March 1948)**: Belmont, North Carolina — Round metallic-appearing object moving at ~600 MPH observed for ~10 seconds. Color: "bright or silver — looked metallic." Reflected sunlight steadily without flickering. Moved in straight line at constant speed, above clouds. Witness: Mr. A.C. Morrison (Sapt. Meter Dept, Duke Power Co.) — described by investigating agent as "a man of excellent character and reputation, a first-grade engineer... conservative and sound." Morrison was "careful in his statements" and "does not claim he saw the 'flying disc' — merely an object he could not identify."

**Incidents 106-109 (March 1948)**: Bakersfield, California wave — Multiple witnesses reported burning objects falling from the sky, trailing black/red smoke. Investigation by Fourth Air Force (Hamilton Field) concluded "no evidence to substantiate" and attributed to possible rocket flares. Key witnesses included George L. Buchner, H.B. Nix, Dr. J.E. Johnson, and Mrs. Callie R. Mason. A parachute-like object was observed drifting after one incident. Searches by aircraft and rescue units found nothing.

**Incident 110 (March 1948)**: Baltimore/Hamilton, Maryland — FBI relay: confidential informant (wife of ex-military member) reported hearing "peculiar sounding noises in the sky" between 0300-0500 hours on multiple occasions over 6-8 weeks. Husband and another neighborhood veteran both identified the sound as "foreign type airplane" engine noise.

**Incident 111 (1 April 1948)**: Philippine Islands — 1st Lt. Robert W. Meyers, P-47 pilot, 67th Fighter Squadron, sighted object resembling "a half moon closely resembling a flying wing type aircraft" with "turtle back and an indistinct dorsal fin." Silver in color. Estimated wingspan 30 ft, length 20 ft. At ~3 miles distance, 1,000 ft altitude. When Meyers turned to intercept, object "made a 90 degree left turn, then leveled out on a heading of 270 degrees, accelerated rapidly and disappeared from sight in approximately five seconds." No sound, no exhaust. Speed at sighting: ~200 MPH but "disappeared in 5 seconds indicating great acceleration." Far East Air Forces evaluation: "object was probably a bird."

**Incident 112 (8 April 1948)**: Ashley, Ohio — Single object observed by two women (bank cashier and housekeeper). Shape/size details not fully legible in sample.
