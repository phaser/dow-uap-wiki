## Key Points

- **John Glenn's MA-6 "luminous particles"** ("Glenn effect"): A memorandum to the Director of the Office of Space Sciences (dated January 13, 1962) analyzes Col. Glenn's observation of thousands of yellow-green luminous particles traveling with the spacecraft at precisely the same speed and direction across all three orbits. The scientific assessment: **these were not extraterrestrial particles but spacecraft-associated** — likely water vapor condensation (snowflakes), dust, or insulation fragments originating from the capsule.

- **Los Alamos Scientific Laboratory analysis** (Dr. Jocelyn Gill correspondence, July 1962): Detailed atmospheric physics analysis of the Glenn-observed absorbing/haze layer near 100 km altitude. Investigated N₂O₄, O⁻, and O₂⁻ as candidate absorbers. Concluded the Glenn-observed layer color "fits very well" with NO₂ and proposed a slow-speed movie camera experiment to photograph star fields passing through the layer.

- **Carpenter MA-7 observations** (May 1962): The "Glenn effect" particles were seen on all three orbits. Carpenter performed the decisive experiment: hitting the capsule wall with his hand promptly liberated large numbers of particles, confirming capsule origin. Particles described as snowflake-like, between 1 mm and 1 cm in size, with brightness up to magnitude -15 (100× brighter than full moon). Airglow layer measured at 89 km altitude via timed star-passage observations. Noted exaggerated angle perception near the horizon (8°–10° estimated, actually 2°–3°).

- **Schirra MA-8 scientific debriefing** (1963): Detailed discussion of an airglow band observation — described as a diffuse, cirrus-like band at the equatorial region, covering the full field of view. Color matching and star-field identification difficulties discussed at length; star chart reconstruction attempted. Schirra used a mirror unsuccessfully; proposed planetarium reconstruction.

- **Cooper MA-9 flashing-light beacon experiment** (June 1963): Cooper deployed and observed a flashing light beacon. Distance judgment was extremely difficult without prior experience — "you can talk yourself into believing it is almost any distance." On the second night pass, the sun illuminated the beacon, providing better depth perception from its glow and drift.

- **Scientific recommendations**: (1) Astronauts should publish their observations in scientific journals under their own names. (2) Proper dark-adaptation methods should be incorporated into spacecraft. (3) Airglow and haze layer investigations should continue. (4) Lightning-storm monitoring from orbit is feasible.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **MA-6 "Glenn effect" luminous particles** — The foundational Mercury UAP-relevant observation. Scientifically resolved as spacecraft-origin condensation/insulation particles.
- **MA-7 "Glenn effect" confirmation and knock test** — Carpenter's capsule-wall hitting experiment proved the particles came from the spacecraft.
- **MA-8 airglow band observation** — Schirra's observation of a diffuse equatorial luminous band, possibly related to red airglow.
- **MA-9 flashing beacon distance estimation** — Early study of the difficulty of range estimation in orbit.
