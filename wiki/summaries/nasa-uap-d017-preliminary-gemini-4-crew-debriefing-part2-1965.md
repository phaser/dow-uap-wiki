## Key Points

- Part II covers **systems operation** (platform, OAMS, RCS, ECS, communications, electrical, computer, crew station, bio-medical), **operational checks** (Apollo landmark identification, yaw orientation, thruster failure, horizon scanner, HF checks, orbit navigation, humidity test, zodiacal light check), **visual sightings**, **experiments**, pre-mission planning, mission control, and training.

- **Section 10.0 Visual Sightings** reveals crew observations across all flight phases:
  - **Countdown**: Wasps swarming on the RCS section and crawling over windows.
  - **Powered Flight / BECO**: White did not see the "big sheet of flame" that previous astronauts (John Glenn, Gus Grissom) described at first-stage separation.
  - **SECO / Spacecraft Separation**: "A lot of stuff coming by the spacecraft. Just plain debris — pieces of white junk." Fairing jettison — pieces flying "all over the place."
  - **Orbital Flight (geographical)**: Ships' wakes, roads, towns, industrial areas (oil tanks), canals (Suez clearly visible), runways. Not individual houses, cars, or airplanes. Resolution estimated at 50 feet or below with proper contrast. Night: city lights "much finer defined than at 40,000 feet."
  - **Orbital Flight (celestial)**: Stars visible to 7th magnitude when windows in total darkness (no sunlight on window, nose, or horizon). Stars were NOT more numerous than from an aircraft at 40,000–45,000 feet — window coating degraded visibility. Mars color appeared washed out — "didn't look as orange" from orbit. "Fire flies" (small particles) continuously around spacecraft, visible even in daylight occasionally.
  - **"Curtain of lights" phenomenon — near Australia, observed twice**: McDivitt and White both observed parallel running lines of lights radiating upward from earth, looking "like a curtain," resembling Aurora Borealis but below them and closer. Wiggly/curved, 5–6 curves, in motion "like neon signs," brighter on first sighting, dimmer on second. McDivitt estimated it extended to 50–60 miles altitude, about halfway between spacecraft and ground. Photos taken (possibly at wrong exposure).
  - **Reentry**: Retro adapter observed floating, glowing (dull → bright → eroding → ball of flame). Pink to orange to reddish-orange with occasional green tongues. Described as "a veil of silk" rather than a big blaze.

- **"Fire flies"**: Small particles continuously around the spacecraft, visible in daylight occasionally. Consistent with the Glenn-effect spacecraft-origin particles documented in Mercury debriefings (see [[summaries/nasa-uap-d015-astronaut-scientific-debriefings-1962-1963]]).

- **Airglow observations**: The crew discussed airglow observations and noted that the "curtain of lights" was distinctly NOT in the airglow layer — it was below and closer.

- Window coating significantly degraded star visibility; crew expressed desire for a telescope mounted externally via EVA.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

- **Gemini 4 "curtain of lights" near Australia** — Two sightings of parallel, wiggling lines of light radiating upward from earth, aurora-like but below the spacecraft and closer. McDivitt and White both confirmed the observation. Not identified as Aurora Borealis; crew was firm it was between them and the cloud layer, not at airglow altitude. Photos were taken but exposure settings may have been incorrect.

## Open Questions

- The "curtain of lights" phenomenon — what was it? The crew was clear it was not Aurora Borealis and not in the airglow layer. The wiggling, curtain-like parallel light lines coming up from below do not match any known atmospheric phenomenon discussed in the debriefing.
- Did the photographs come out? McDivitt suspected he used wrong exposure settings.
- The "fire flies" — were these same as the Glenn-effect condensation particles, and why were they visible in daylight?
