## Key Points

- Curated selection clearly chosen because it contains **multiple crew observations of unidentified luminous objects**: a debris field of bright tumbling fragments after S-IVB separation, a long-lived rotating "flasher" in deep space that the crew tried to localize with NOUN-20 attitude data, and a discrete flash on the lunar surface near the crater Grimaldi reported by geologist Schmitt.
- Cernan also describes vivid ALFMED-style intra-cabin light flashes — "a very bright headlight — like a train coming at you, only with a flash" — observed before sleep on the night before the excerpt.
- A second crew remark concerns "the first whifferdill" — Cernan's joke about the live TV downlink — and is unrelated to UAP.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

### Bright tumbling debris field after S-IVB separation (MET ~00 03 35)

Shortly after spacecraft separation from the S-IVB upper stage, all three crew see large bright fragments tumbling outside the windows. Evans (CMP): *"Now we've got a few very bright particles or fragments or something that go drifting by as we maneuver."* Schmitt (LMP): *"There's a whole bunch of big ones on my window down there — just bright. It looks like the Fourth of July out of Ron's window."* Evans: *"Very jagged, angular fragments that are tumbling."* Cernan: *"flat, flake-like particles. Some may be 6 inches across … most of them seem to be twinkling. And I think, for the most part, they're all moving away from us."* The crew speculate paint peeling from the S-IVB, ice chunks, or SLA-panel material. Evans takes photographs (Frame 70). **Plausible explanation:** S-IVB / SLA panel debris dispersing after T&D maneuver. Notable for its scale and the detailed in-flight visual analysis.

### The "rotating flasher" — deep-space unidentified light (MET ~02 18 42 onward, Tape 46)

Translunar coast. Cernan (CDR) and Schmitt (LMP) both observe an object that flashes rhythmically:

> "It is a — it is a bright object, and it's obviously rotating because it's flashing. … way out in the distance … there is something out there." — Cernan
>
> "It's definitely not — not one of these particles that tends to look like a star out there. It's something physical in the distance." — Cernan

Houston (Bob Parker, CapCom) requests NOUN-20 spacecraft attitude data and a "hack" mark when the object crosses the spacecraft XZ plane. Cernan: object is up at about 11 o'clock relative to the Earth view, "10 or 12 Earth diameters" from Earth, pitched up ~45° at the center hatch window. Cernan describes a unique signature: **two flashes per rotation cycle — "very bright flash; and then you get a dull flash"** — like seeing the broadside then the engine-bell end of a cylinder. Schmitt eventually conjectures it is the spent S-IVB tumbling. Later (Tape 47) Cernan reports **two such flashers**, "alike in intensity," widely separated. He also entertains they could be the SLA panels. Identification was never independently confirmed in the released excerpt.

### Cernan — intra-cabin light flashes "between my eyes" (MET ~02 18 41 59, Tape 46/4)

Cernan recounts seeing "the same peripheral horizon-type things you said were not the type of data you were looking for" plus "sets of the streaks," and:

> "There was a very bright spot that flashed right between my eyes like a very bright headlight — like a train coming at you, only with a flash."

Houston ("we appreciate all the data. We were just trying to make the data fit the curve") indicates these reports were being collected against an experiment protocol — the **ALFMED (Apollo Light Flash Moving Emulsion Detector)** cosmic-ray-flash experiment carried on Apollo 16 and 17. Schmitt notes the LMP set picked up two pictures of the ALFMED apparatus (Frame 50).

### Schmitt — flash on the lunar surface near Grimaldi (MET 03 15 38 09, Tape 60/1)

In lunar orbit, observing the western edge of Oceanus Procellarum from window 3:

> "Hey, I just saw a flash on the lunar surface! … It was just out there north of Grimaldi. … It was a bright little flash right out there near that crater. … there is another one north of it. Fairly sharp one north of it is where there was just a thin streak of light."

Schmitt suggests Houston check the Apollo passive seismic experiment for a corresponding small impact. CapCom asks him to mark the location on a map. **This is a discrete, geographically located, time-stamped UAP-relevant observation by a trained geologist-astronaut.** Most plausibly a meteoroid impact flash — a phenomenon that decades later would be confirmed by ground-based observers (e.g., NASA Marshall's Lunar Impact Monitoring Program from the 2000s). Schmitt's report is one of the earliest first-person eyewitness accounts of a lunar impact flash from lunar orbit.
