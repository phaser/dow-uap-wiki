## Key Points

- This is **the** Apollo 11 UAP-relevant document — it captures, in the crew's own words during the official one-week-post-splashdown debrief, three discrete observations later cited in UFO literature: (1) a sizable unidentified object ("hollow cylinder / two rings / open suitcase" / "L-shape") observed about a day from the Moon, (2) intra-cabin light flashes seen by Aldrin during sleep periods, (3) a bright Earth-side light source observed during lunar orbit, initially considered as a possible laser.
- The document also contains routine technical content (rest discipline, LM-separation timing, frame of mind for extended EVA missions) — only Section 6 fragments and Section 21 "Visual Sightings" are in the released excerpt.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

### Translunar — the "hollow cylinder / open suitcase" object (~ 1 day before lunar arrival)

All three crew discuss this at length in Section 6. Aldrin: *"The first unusual thing that we saw I guess was 1 day out or something pretty close to the moon. It had a sizeable dimension to it, so we put the monocular on it."* They asked Houston about the spent S-IVB and were told it was 6,000 miles away. Through the spacecraft monocular it appeared "to have a bit of an L shape" / "like an open suitcase" (Aldrin). Through the sextant in the LEB, defocused, Collins saw "a cylinder. Or really two rings." Aldrin: *"You could see this thing tumbling and, when it came around end-on, you could look right down in its guts. It was a hollow cylinder. But then you could change the focus on the sextant and it would be replaced by this open-book shape. It was really weird."* They were in PTC ("barbecue roll") at the time so all three rotated past it.

Crew conclusion: *"We don't have any more conclusions than that really. The fact that we didn't see it much past this one time period — we really don't have a conclusion as to what it might have been, how big it was, or how far away it was. It was something that wasn't part of the urine dump, we're pretty sure of that."* — Collins

The crew **also** speculate it may have been related to LM-jettison debris later in the mission ("Pieces came off the LM. It could have been some Mylar or something") or part of the high-gain antenna assembly that had given them trouble. Armstrong observes the object was *"right at the limit of the resolution of the eye … there was no way to tell the size without knowing the range or the range without knowing the size."* The optical-defocus artifact (cylinder ↔ open-book switching with sextant focus change) is consistent with the object being one of the four S-IVB / SLA adapter panels at extreme range — but the debrief preserves the crew's own uncertainty.

### Aldrin — intra-cabin light flashes during sleep (translunar / transearth coast)

Aldrin saw faint flashes inside the darkened cabin starting "the second night" and devoted time during transearth coast to characterising them. Two morphologies: single point flashes, and double flashes "at points separated by maybe a foot" suggesting "an entry and then impact on something such as the struts." Sometimes a "line with no direction of motion." His hypothesis: *"some sort of penetration … some penetration of some object into the spacecraft that causes an emission as it enters the cabin itself."* He distinguished these from static-electricity sparks generated by his sleep restraint. He correlated direction loosely with the Sun side of the spacecraft. Armstrong, on the last night, devoted ~1 hour of focused observation and counted ~90 events. Armstrong: *"It could be something like Buzz suggested. Mainly a neutron or some kind of an atomic particle that would be in the visible spectrum."*

**This is the first systematic crew description of what would later be confirmed as cosmic-ray interactions with the retina** — the "Apollo light flashes" that motivated the ALFMED experiment on Apollo 16 and 17 ([[concepts/light-flashes-cosmic-rays]]). The Apollo 11 crew effectively discovered the phenomenon in flight.

### Section 21.5 Lunar Orbit — "possible laser" / lake-reflection light source from Earth

Aldrin reports: *"Approaching CDH when the Earth came up above the lunar horizon, I observed what appeared to be a fairly bright light source which we tentatively ascribed to a possible laser."* Coming back during the transearth phase the crew saw the same kind of phenomenon and revised their interpretation: *"It appeared as though it was the reflection of the Sun from a relatively smooth body of water such as a lake. … If no one owns up to having beamed the laser toward the Moon at that time, it was more probably a reflection off a lake. I still think it's an unusual phenomenon, at that distance, to see so bright a source of light."* The light did not register on the in-cabin film.

Section 21.4 also records a minor optical illusion: Armstrong saw "the image of Mars" appearing to come from a dark portion of the lunar limb, which he attributed to refraction/illusion at the horizon.

## Open Questions

- The "hollow cylinder / open suitcase" object: definitive post-mission identification? The crew's S-IVB-vs-SLA-panel uncertainty was never resolved in the debrief. Cross-checking with NASA's later Apollo trajectory and S-IVB tracking data (the S-IVB was placed into a heliocentric orbit on Apollo 11 — it was *not* impacted on the Moon as on Apollo 13–17) might constrain whether it could have been visible at the reported geometry.
- Whether the "possible laser" from Earth had any earthbound corroboration (no documented contemporary laser-to-Moon beam from a non-classified facility for that window).
- This document was originally **classified CONFIDENTIAL** with a 12-year automatic-declassification rider. What in a routine technical debrief warranted that classification — the UAP-adjacent passages, or the engineering-performance details? Worth comparing with Apollo 12+ debriefings, several of which appear less heavily marked.
