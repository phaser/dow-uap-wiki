## Key Points

- The **single substantive page (p. 24) is densely UAP-relevant**: in fewer than 30 lines it covers (a) Evans's reentry observation of a "tunnel with a bright spot in the middle" through the rendezvous window after fireball brightness decreased, (b) Evans's wry "tin can with us" remark on first sighting an aircraft-carrier mast through fogged windows during recovery, (c) Schmitt's recap of continuous intra-cabin light flashes during transearth coast, (d) Schmitt's reaffirmation that he saw a flash on the lunar surface.
- The page comes from the section of the technical debrief that walked the crew through entry, descent, and recovery, then back to transearth observations.
- Curated for UAP-disclosure precisely because of the Schmitt lunar-surface-flash sentence and the ALFMED/cosmic-ray light-flash discussion.

## Notable Incidents / Cases

### Evans — "Tunnel with a bright spot" during reentry

> "After the brightness of the fireball decreased, I could look back up through the rendezvous window and see what to me was kind of like a tunnel with a bright spot in the middle of the tunnel. Way down the tunnel, way back behind, I could see the fireball." — Evans

A reentry plasma-trail visual — interesting phenomenology but mundane (ionised wake behind the command module).

### Recovery — "Tin can with us"

> "The only unusual sighting I can recall during landing or recovery is when the CMP looked out the window and saw the superstructure of an aircraft carrier and said, 'Oh, we've got a tin can with us.'" — Cernan
>
> "Well, it was kind of foggy on the windows." — Evans

Joke / mundane (the recovery carrier USS *Ticonderoga*).

### Schmitt — Continuous light flashes throughout the flight

> "We had light flashes just about continuously during the whole flight when we were dark adapted. I had one which I thought was a flash on the lunar surface. That one period of time when we had the blindfolds on for the ALFMED experiment there were just no visible flashes, although that evening, that night, before I went to sleep I noticed that I was seeing the light flashes again. So, it just seemed to be that one interval either side of it where the light flash was not visible to myself or to the other two crewmen." — Schmitt

This is the **technical-debrief confirmation, in Schmitt's own post-flight words, of two key observations**:
1. The ALFMED-context cosmic-ray retinal flashes were ubiquitous when dark-adapted.
2. **The lunar-surface flash near Grimaldi** (independently captured in real-time in [[summaries/nasa-uap-d2-apollo-17-transcript-1972]]) is reaffirmed by Schmitt as a one-off, distinguished from the ALFMED retinal flashes by its lunar-surface origin.
3. Curiously, Schmitt notes that during the ALFMED blindfold period (with the photographic emulsion plates strapped over the crew's eyes) "there were just no visible flashes" — and also that "neither of the other two crewmen" reported flashes during that interval. This is operationally interesting: the ALFMED experiment was specifically designed to detect cosmic-ray events as tracks in nuclear emulsion paired with crew flash reports. Schmitt's "no flashes during ALFMED" comment, taken at face value, would be anomalous against the rest of the flight; more likely it reflects (a) reduced reporting under blindfold + concentration on the experiment, (b) the relatively short ALFMED run, or (c) selective recall.
