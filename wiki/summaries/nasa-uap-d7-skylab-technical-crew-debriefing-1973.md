## Key Points

- This is the **most directly UAP-relevant document in the batch** alongside D4. Each of the three Skylab crews independently reports unusual visual events; the Skylab 3 crew's "red satellite" — a bright, reddish, rotating, unidentified object that paced the workshop for ~10 minutes about a week before splashdown — is a textbook UFO-style sighting recorded in an official NASA crew debrief.
- All three crews additionally report ALFMED-style cosmic-ray light flashes (continuing the Apollo-era observations into LEO long-duration flight, with explicit speculation about correlation with the South Atlantic Anomaly).
- Skylab 4 crew add reports of intermittent flashing lights moving relative to the station, attributed cautiously to "other pieces of Skylab, or possibly other satellites."

## Notable Incidents / Cases

### Skylab 2 — Cosmic-ray light flashes (Conrad, Kerwin, Weitz)

Kerwin describes spot/sunburst and streak morphologies, mostly peripheral, two to three per minute at peak, often eyes-closed in the sleep restraint. He raises with the crew whether the rate correlated with the South Atlantic Anomaly (high-radiation region of Earth's magnetic field): *"Someone asked me if that was in conjunction with the South Atlantic anomaly. It may have been. I didn't have the pad with me at that time and I don't know."* Kerwin and Weitz both report **double "entry-exit" streaks** ("bing-bing, it seemed like it was one side of the eyeball, and then the other side") consistent with single relativistic particles traversing the eyeball — the same morphology Aldrin had described in [[summaries/nasa-uap-d4-apollo-11-technical-crew-debriefing-1969]] in 1969. Kerwin: *"They're not an hallucination. We didn't feel it was operationally necessary for anybody to know about it right now."*

### Skylab 3 — The "red satellite" (Bean, Garriott, Lousma) — ~ 1 week before splashdown

The most striking discrete UAP report in the entire batch. Bean (CDR) and Garriott (SPT) both describe the event in detail. Garriott:

> "Jack first noticed this rather large red star out the wardroom window. Upon close examination, it was much brighter than Jupiter or any of the other planets. It had a reddish hue to it, even though it was well above the horizon. The light from the Sun was not passing close to the Earth's limb at the time. We observed it for about 10 minutes prior to sunset. It was slowly rotating because it had a variation in brightness with a 10-second period. … From the 5- to 10-second delay in its disappearance we surmised that it was not more than 30 to 50 nautical miles from our location. From its original position in the wardroom window, it did not move more than 10 or 20 degrees over the 10 minutes or so that we watched it. Its orbit was very close to that of our own. We never saw it on any — earlier or succeeding orbits and we'd be quite interested in having its identification established."

Bean adds: *"You might point out that it never did take the shape of an object but it was always brighter than any other star or planet in the night sky. It was much brighter. … We tried monitors and everything on it but we could never make it into anything other than a bright light."* Concrete parameters captured by the crew:

| Parameter | Value (per Garriott) |
|---|---|
| First-noticed-by | Lousma ("Jack") |
| Color | Reddish, "well above the horizon" — not a sunset reddening artifact |
| Brightness | Brighter than Jupiter / any night-sky planet |
| Period of brightness variation | ~10 seconds (interpreted as rotation) |
| Time visible before sunset | ~10 minutes |
| Time to its sunset after Skylab's | 5–7 seconds |
| Inferred range | "not more than 30 to 50 nautical miles" |
| Apparent angular drift in window | 10–20° over 10 min |
| Orbit | "very close to that of our own" |
| Repeat sightings | None (not seen on prior or subsequent orbits) |

Bean explicitly requests identification: *"What satellite it was and how it happened to end up in such a similar orbit, no one ever explained to us. And I would like to hear a few words from someone about that satellite."* Garriott echoes: *"It's all debriefed in terms of time on channel A, so the precise timing and location can be picked up from there."*

The crew note timing was logged on Channel A — i.e. there should be a recoverable on-the-record timestamp. The debrief's tone is unmistakably "we want this identified and have not been told." There is no public follow-up identifying this object in the released excerpt.

### Skylab 3 — Other visual observations

- Garriott had previously seen **other satellites** during T002 sextant work, "appearing as star points of light" — i.e. he had a baseline of normal-satellite appearance and rejected that interpretation for the red object.
- A separate "wardroom window sunrise/sunset" observation eventually revealed an RCS leak on the command module — this was an internal-spacecraft anomaly localised by visual means, not UAP.
- Lousma describes the "thousands and thousands of stars" / particles drifting along the X-axis at sunrise/sunset — the well-known Skylab "constellation" of co-moving particles (urine dump frost, vented water ice, paint flakes).

### Skylab 4 — Flashing lights moving relative to the station (Carr, Gibson, Pogue)

Carr (CDR):

> "One other area of unusual events that we reported on the dump tapes was that on occasion we saw some lights flashing outside with very a definite motion relative to ours. We presumed that they were other pieces of Skylab, or possibly other satellites. We reported our two or three sightings of that kind as soon as they occurred. … but we did find it very interesting to be able to see other objects up there with us. The fact that one or two of them appeared to be tumbling was apparently due to the oscillation of the light flashes that we were getting from them."

Two or three discrete events. The crew's own preferred explanation is debris or other satellites — but they note tumbling-light periodicity, mirroring (in miniature) the Skylab 3 "red satellite" signature.

## Open Questions

- **Identification of the Skylab 3 "red satellite"** — never resolved in the released excerpt. Channel-A voice-tape timestamp + Skylab orbit data should permit a SATCAT cross-check (1973-Aug/Sep window, near-co-orbital with Skylab at ~435 km, period ~93 min, reddish appearance). Worth a focused synthesis page.
- Did the Skylab 4 crew's "two or three" flashing-light sightings ever get correlated with TLEs of contemporary debris? Carr says they were reported on dump tapes — those tapes should exist in the Skylab voice archive.
- The Skylab 2 crew's South Atlantic Anomaly correlation hypothesis (Kerwin) is testable against Skylab orbit/SAA-passage logs and the dosimeter record. Did anyone publish the analysis? (Apollo light-flash papers exist; Skylab equivalent should too.)
- This document, more than any other in the batch, looks like a deliberate inclusion in the UAP set — the Skylab 3 "red satellite" passage in particular is unambiguously a crew request for UFO-style identification that was, per the debrief itself, never answered.
