## Scope

A curated ranking of the ten most disclosure-movement-relevant primary-source documents in the wiki's corpus, considered against the contemporary (2017–2026) disclosure context: the 2017 NYT *Glowing Auras* article, the 2019 Senate briefings, the 2020 ODNI report, the AARO establishment under NDAA-2022, the 2023 Grusch testimony, the Schumer-Rounds NDAA-2024 disclosure provisions, and the 2026 Department of War / AARO public release that this corpus is built on.

"Disclosure-relevant" is operationalized here as: **primary-source weight × novelty / surprise × political or scientific load**. A document scores high if it is a verified primary record, contains material that the disclosure community has long been pointing at as significant, and engages the question of UAP institutional handling at a level above routine field-investigator paperwork.

This ranking was updated after the full post-OCR re-ingestion of all 17 previously-sampled files (2026-05-09). Three documents that were previously only "honourable mentions" — the Guy Hottel memo, the Los Alamos conferences, and the Oak Ridge radar detections — are now fully read and materially restructure the ranking.

### Justifications (long form)

**1 — Guy Hottel memo (22 March 1950)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-5]])

The most-viewed page in the FBI Vault. SA Guy Hottel (SAC, Washington) reports that an informant (identity obscured, sourced through "Kartahowe, Sex Squad, Metropolitan Police Department") — described as an Air Force investigator — stated that **three flying saucers had been recovered in New Mexico**: each circular with raised centers, approximately 50 feet in diameter, each occupied by **three 3-foot-tall human-shaped bodies in metallic cloth of fine texture, bandaged in blackout-suit fashion**. The saucers were detected because government high-powered radar in the area interfered with their control mechanisms. This is the direct primary-source anchor for the crash-retrieval / non-human intelligence narrative that the disclosure community has been citing since the 1970s. The proximity to an AEC installation (implied by the radar-interference detail) adds the nuclear-site adjacency dimension.

**2 — Los Alamos conferences (Feb 17 + Oct 14, 1949)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-6]])

Two multi-agency conferences on green fireball phenomena. Representatives from: Fourth Army, Armed Forces Special Weapons Project, University of New Mexico, FBI, AEC, University of California, USAF Scientific Advisory Board, Geophysical Research Division AMC, and OSI. The formal conclusion: **"A logical explanation was not proffered with respect to the origin of the green fireballs... generally concluded that the phenomenon existed and that they should be studied scientifically until these occurrences have been satisfactorily explained."** Continued occurrence near sensitive AEC installations was deemed "cause for concern." This is one of the strongest primary-source records of the U.S. scientific-military establishment acknowledging an unexplained phenomenon at the highest level — predating the 1969 Apollo debriefs, the 1977 Carter WH inquiry, and the 2017 NYT article by decades.

**3 — Oak Ridge radar over nuclear facilities (Dec 1950–Jan 1951 + Mar 1950)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-6]], [[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-5]])

Multiple distinct detection events over the Oak Ridge AEC nuclear complex: (a) Dec 18, 1950 — NEPA Division employees observed a bright circular light "brighter than full moon" over the AEC controlled area; (b) Dec 20, 1950 — 663rd AC&W Squadron radar recorded "very, very slow" paint over Oak Ridge with F-82 fighter conducting a "perfect intercept orbiting small smoke cloud"; (c) Dec 14, 1950 — 3-hour radar event with scope photographs by Lt. Robinson; (d) Jan 16, 1951 — western light through 20× scope showed "many peculiar forms with lines, cores, tails"; (e) Mar 1950 — Stuart Adcock's amateur APN-7 radar detected objects at 40,000–100,000 ft over Oak Ridge, prompting a multi-agency response (AEC, OSI, CIA, 3rd Army, USAF NEPA). Hard sensor data over the most sensitive nuclear installation in the U.S. is among the highest-weight evidence in the entire corpus.

**4 — Section 8 — O'Mara "do exist" + Stringfield newsletter (Sep 1954)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-8]])

In Stringfield's Newsletter, Lt. Col. John O'Mara, **Deputy Commander of Intelligence at Wright-Patterson AFB**, is quoted in a private talk: **"Flying saucers 'do exist'"** and "past contradictions were unfortunate." O'Mara categorizes saucers into three breakdowns: (1) controlled saucer from outer space, (2) secret American saucer-like device, (3) unexplained natural phenomena. When asked if all saucers seen by Americans were American devices, he said "Definitely not!" This is an exceptionally rare high-level military intelligence acknowledgment — the Deputy Commander of the nation's premier intelligence base — preserved verbatim in FBI files. The section also preserves the Detroit Flying Saucer Club's open letter to the President calling for "honest and forthright acknowledgment."

**5 — Green fireball wave at Los Alamos (Section 4)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-4]])

Dr. Lincoln La Paz's Starvation Peak incident — a bright light with constant intensity, yellow-green color at 5200 Angstroms, horizontal path, constant angular velocity, two seconds duration, no accompanying noise. Since December 5, 1948: **more than ten incidents analogous** to Starvation Peak and **twenty others with minor deviations**, geographically confined to the Los Alamos / Las Vegas / West Texas triangle. Spectrum analysis suggests a copper compound known to be used in rocket experiments. This is the most sustained, scientifically-documented fireball wave in the corpus — not a single sighting but a multi-month campaign of observations over a nuclear installation.

**6 — Hunter NASC memo, 18 July 1963** ([[summaries/59_214434_sp_16_1963-07-18]])

A National Aeronautics and Space Council *staff* memo, "Thoughts on the Space Alien Race Question," treats the alien-contact scenario as a policy-actionable concern at the executive-office level — not as a UFO investigation, but as a policy-planning paper about how an encounter would be handled. The NASC sat directly under the Vice-President. Remains the strongest pre-2017 primary-source record of the U.S. government seriously considering first-contact policy at the executive level.

**7 — Socorro / Lonnie Zamora, 24 April 1964** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-serial-438]])

The cleanest physical-evidence case in the wiki: landing trace, multi-agency on-scene investigation (Socorro PD + FBI SA D. Arthur Byrnes Jr. + Capt. Holder USAF), Project Blue Book disposition of "unidentified" — the rarest dispositional category in the program's history. The newly-surfaced Section 10 internal Bureau note (December 1966) confirms the Albuquerque FBI office produced the original Zamora witness statement that fed downstream Bureau and AF dissemination.

**8 — Serial 130 — Newfoundland Kodachrome trail photos (10 July 1947)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-serial-130]])

Serial 130 is a formal ADC intelligence bundle containing **five distinct Newfoundland reports** not previously surfaced. The most compelling: a disc-shaped object, same size as a C-54, at 10,000 ft, leaving a **bluish-black trail ~15 miles long** (similar to afterglow of powerful landing light). **Mr. Robert E. Leidy took two Kodachrome photographs of the trail** — film transmitted to Asst. Chief of Staff, Intelligence, Newfoundland Base Command. Sworn interrogation transcripts from TWA and PAA mechanics preserved. This is a complete formal military evidence package for a single sighting.

**9 — Box 186 — USAF Flight Service Center 1949 (Regulation 200-4)** ([[summaries/box186-319.1-flying-discs-1949]])

The operational regulation-200-4 file — the standardized USAF sighting-report channel post-Mantell that funneled all CONUS UFO reports through regional Flight Service Centers (Lowry, McChord, Olmsted, Maxwell, Hamilton) to AMC at Wright-Patterson. This is **the institutional backbone of the GRUDGE-era response**: dedicated regulation, standardized form, multiple channels. The file is dominated by post-Mantell flightline witnesses — controllers, tower operators, ARTC controllers, AACS air/ground operators — representing a professionalization of UFO observation. Contains notable cases: Spokane (8 dish-shaped objects, each "larger than a B-29"), Medford (multiple AACS + CAA tower operators, wings visible), Mountain Home (7 delta-wing objects, darker than aluminum, no markings, 600+ mph).

**10 — Hoover's "Iowa case" marginal note (24 July 1947)** ([[summaries/62-hq-83894-section-1]])

On the Schulgen-FBI cooperation memo, Hoover wrote: **"I would do it but before agreeing to it we must insist upon full access to discs recovered. For instance in the Ia. case the Army grabbed it and would not let us have it for cursory examination."** The "Iowa case" reference is striking — it implies an early-July 1947 Iowa flying-disc recovery the AAF retained without FBI access. The identity of the case is not specified within this section. This is the foundational primary-source statement for the "they recovered something and buried it" narrative — and it comes from Hoover himself, not a third-party claimant.

- The 1980s–early-2000s middle period is thin overall: corpus density is highest 1947–1970s and 2020–2026.

### What this corpus has that's *under-represented* in disclosure-movement discourse

- The **Guy Hottel memo** — the most-viewed page in the FBI Vault, but rarely cited in public disclosure literature beyond UFOlogy circles. The FBI's own primary file contains the three-saucers-nine-bodies claim verbatim, with the radar-interference detection theory and the AEC-proximity implication.
- The **Los Alamos conferences** — the multi-agency conclusion "logical explanation was not proffered" is one of the strongest scientific-establishment acknowledgments in the entire UAP record, yet it is virtually unknown outside the wiki.
- The **Oak Ridge radar over nuclear facilities** — hard sensor data over the most sensitive AEC installations in the U.S., with F-82 intercepts and scope photos. This is not a civilian report; it's a military radar event over a nuclear complex.
- The **late-1940s SIGN incident-summary form** as a methodological artifact ([[summaries/army-1948-general-vol-1]] and the 1-100 / 101-172 / 173-233 trilogy). The form's evolution from 26-line CHECK-LIST to 25-line INCIDENT SUMMARY captures the program's analytical maturation in a way that public disclosure narratives rarely surface.
- The **Scandinavian "Peenemünde-direction" ghost-rocket continuity into 1948** ([[summaries/army-incident-summaries-101-172]] Inc 132–133). Connects the [[concepts/foo-fighter|WW2 foo-fighter]] / [[concepts/ghost-rockets|1946 ghost-rocket]] phenomena to the 1947+ flying-disc wave with primary-source bridges.
